Mendel's First Law — Law of Dominance & Uniformity Core

Law of Dominance (First Law)

When two homozygous parents differing in one character are crossed, all F₁ hybrids are uniform and resemble one parent (the dominant parent). The character that appears in F₁ = dominant; the hidden one = recessive.

Monohybrid cross: TT × tt → F₁: all Tt (Tall = dominant). F₁ × F₁: Tt × Tt → F₂: 1TT : 2Tt : 1tt = 3 Tall : 1 Dwarf (3:1 phenotypic ratio)

Mendel's Second Law — Law of Segregation (Second Law) Mechanism

Law of Segregation (Purity of Gametes)

During gamete formation, the two alleles of a gene segregate (separate) from each other so that a gamete receives only one allele of each gene. The alleles remain pure — they do NOT blend (blending theory disproved).

Molecular basis: During Meiosis I, homologous chromosomes segregate into different gametes → each gamete gets only one allele.

Mendel's Third Law — Law of Independent Assortment Dihybrid

Law of Independent Assortment

When two pairs of contrasting characters are considered simultaneously, the genes for different characters assort independently of each other during gamete formation.

Dihybrid cross: RRYY × rryy → F₁: all RrYy. F₁ × F₁: RrYy × RrYy → 9:3:3:1 ratio (9 Round Yellow : 3 Round Green : 3 Wrinkled Yellow : 1 Wrinkled Green)

Deviations from Mendelian Ratios — Critical NEET Topics 5–6 Qs/yr

A. Incomplete Dominance (Blending)

Incomplete Dominance — Snapdragon (Antirrhinum) Example
  • When neither allele is fully dominant → intermediate phenotype in F₁
  • Cross: Red (RR) × White (rr) → F₁: Pink (Rr) — neither parent phenotype
  • F₁ × F₁: Rr × Rr → F₂: 1 Red : 2 Pink : 1 White
  • Genotypic ratio = Phenotypic ratio = 1:2:1
  • Examples: Antirrhinum (flower colour), Andalusian fowl (feather colour: black × white → blue-black), 4 o'clock plant (Mirabilis jalapa)

B. Codominance — Both Alleles Expressed

Codominance — ABO Blood Group System
  • Both alleles are equally expressed simultaneously in heterozygote (no blending, no dominance)
  • Example: ABO blood groups. Gene = I gene. 3 alleles: Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i
  • Iᴬ and Iᴮ are both DOMINANT over i but are CODOMINANT to each other
  • Genotype IᴬIᴮ → Blood group AB (both A and B antigens expressed simultaneously = codominance)
  • Multiple allelism: 3 alleles control blood group, but a diploid individual has only 2 at a time

NEET 2023 If a person with blood group A marries a person with blood group B, what blood groups are possible in their offspring? (Given both parents are heterozygous)
Answer: A, B, AB, O — all four possible

C. Pleiotropy — One Gene → Many Phenotypic Effects

  • A single gene influences multiple, apparently unrelated phenotypic characters
  • Examples:
  • Phenylketonuria (PKU): Mutation in gene for phenylalanine hydroxylase → affects pigmentation (albinism), intellectual disability, brain damage (multiple effects from one gene)
  • Starch synthesis in pea seeds: Round (R) allele → branched starch → more water absorbed → smooth seed. Wrinkled (r) allele → unbranched starch → less water → wrinkled appearance. One gene affects both starch type AND seed shape.

D. Multiple Allelism

  • More than 2 allelic forms of a gene exist in a population at the same gene locus
  • Any individual has only 2 alleles at most (diploid)
  • Example: ABO blood group — 3 alleles (Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i). Coat colour in rabbits — 4 alleles (C, cᶜʰ, cʰ, c). Eye colour in Drosophila.

E. Polygenic Inheritance

Polygenic Traits — Many Genes, One Character
  • A trait is controlled by two or more genes (additive effect)
  • Shows continuous variation → bell curve distribution in population
  • Examples: Human skin colour (multiple genes for melanin amount), Human height, Eye colour, IQ, Wheat kernel colour (Ronald Fisher's classic example)
  • Skin colour in humans: At least 3 genes (A, B, C) each contributing additive pigmentation. AABBCC = darkest; aabbcc = lightest (albino)

Linked Genes & Recombination — Bateson's Discovery Linkage Map

Linkage — Discovery by Bateson & Punnett
  • Morgan (working on Drosophila) showed that genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together = linked genes
  • Coupling (cis): Two dominant alleles on same chromosome
  • Repulsion (trans): One dominant + one recessive on same chromosome
  • Crossing over (during Prophase I of meiosis) creates new combinations = recombinants
  • Recombination frequency = (Number of recombinant offspring / Total offspring) × 100%
  • 1% recombination = 1 Morgan (centimorgan, cM) — unit of genetic distance
  • Maximum recombination = 50% (genes are effectively unlinked — behave as if on different chromosomes)

Chromosomal Theory of Inheritance — Morgan, Linkage & Sex Determination

Chromosomal Theory — Historical Development NCERT Exact

  • 1902Walter Sutton & Theodore Boveri independently proposed that chromosomes are the physical carriers of genes. Behaviour of chromosomes during meiosis parallels Mendel's factors.
  • 1910Thomas Hunt Morgan — worked on Drosophila melanogaster; demonstrated sex-linkage. Showed genes are on specific chromosomes.
  • 1911Morgan — linkage groups; showed that some genes do NOT assort independently (violating Mendel's 3rd law).
  • 1926Morgan won Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for chromosome theory of heredity.
  • 1913Alfred Sturtevant (Morgan's student) — created first chromosome map (linkage map) using recombination frequencies.

Why Drosophila melanogaster?

  • Small size; easy to breed in lab
  • Short generation time (~2 weeks at 25°C)
  • Produces large number of offspring
  • Only 4 pairs of chromosomes (3 autosomes + 1 sex chromosome pair)
  • Clear visible sex difference: males have distinctly shaped sex comb on forelegs
  • Many easily identifiable morphological mutants
  • Male Drosophila does NOT show crossing over (no recombination in male flies) — simplifies analysis

Sex Determination in Humans — SRY Gene

In humans, the Y chromosome determines maleness. The SRY gene (Sex-determining Region of Y chromosome) on the short arm of Y chromosome encodes Testis-Determining Factor (TDF). TDF → testes development → testosterone → male phenotype. XX individuals without SRY → female (default phenotype = female). XY individuals with SRY mutation → female phenotype (Swyer syndrome).

NEET 2021 In birds, the sex of the individual is determined by: (A) XX-XY system (B) ZW-ZZ system (C) XX-XO system (D) Environmental determination
Answer: (B) ZW-ZZ system

Birds use the ZW-ZZ system where the female is the heterogametic sex (ZW) — opposite of humans! Male birds = ZZ (homogametic). This is tested to ensure students don't apply the human XX-XY system to all organisms. Also applicable to snakes, butterflies.

Barr Body — Sex Chromatin Karyotype Tip

  • Discovered by Murray Barr (1949)
  • = Inactive X chromosome in mammalian somatic cells (condensed, dark-staining)
  • Number of Barr bodies = (Number of X chromosomes) − 1
  • Normal female (XX) → 1 Barr body; Normal male (XY) → 0 Barr bodies
  • Klinefelter (XXY) → 1 Barr body. Turner (XO) → 0 Barr bodies. XXX → 2 Barr bodies.
  • Mary Lyon's hypothesis (Lyonization): One X chromosome is inactivated randomly in every somatic cell at early embryo stage. The inactivated X forms the Barr body. In each cell, inactivation is permanent and clonal — this is why calico cats exist (patchy coat colour).
NEET 2022 How many Barr bodies are present in a cell of a normal human female?
Answer: 1 Barr body

Normal female = 46,XX. Number of Barr bodies = number of X chromosomes − 1 = 2 − 1 = 1. The Barr body is the inactivated X chromosome (Lyon's hypothesis). Normal male = 46,XY → 0 Barr bodies. Klinefelter (XXY) → 1 Barr body. Turner (XO) → 0.

The Genetic Material Experiment — Historical Quest Must Know

  • 1928Frederick Griffith — Transformation experiment with Streptococcus pneumoniae. S-strain (smooth, virulent) and R-strain (rough, avirulent). Heat-killed S + live R → some live R transformed to S and killed mice → "transforming principle" exists.
  • 1944Avery, MacLeod & McCarty — identified transforming principle as DNA (not protein). Used DNase, RNase, Protease — only DNase destroyed transforming ability.
  • 1952Hershey & Chase — Confirmed DNA is genetic material. Used bacteriophage T2 with radioactive labels: ³⁵S (protein coat) or ³²P (DNA). After infection: ³²P found inside bacteria (DNA injected), ³⁵S stayed outside (protein coat stayed). DNA = genetic material.
  • 1953James Watson & Francis Crick — double helix model of DNA. Used X-ray data from Rosalind Franklin (Photo 51) and Erwin Chargaff's rules.
  • 1957Arthur Kornberg — isolated DNA polymerase I from E. coli. Nobel Prize 1959.
  • 1958Meselson & Stahl — proved semi-conservative replication using ¹⁵N/¹⁴N density gradient centrifugation in E. coli.
  • 1961Jacob & Monod — proposed operon model. Nobel Prize 1965.
  • 1961–66Nirenberg, Khorana, Holley — cracked the genetic code. Nobel Prize 1968.

Chargaff's Rules — Base Pairing Rules NEET Every Year

Chargaff's Rules (1950)

From chemical analysis of DNA from many organisms:

1. A = T (Adenine always equals Thymine) in double-stranded DNA

2. G = C (Guanine always equals Cytosine) in double-stranded DNA

3. Therefore: (A + G) = (T + C) — purines = pyrimidines

4. Base ratios are species-specific but constant for a given organism regardless of tissue

Purine bases: Adenine (A) + Guanine (G) — 2 rings

Pyrimidine bases: Thymine (T) + Cytosine (C) + Uracil (U, in RNA) — 1 ring

If given %A, find others: If A = 25%, then T = 25% (A=T). G + C = 100 − 50 = 50%. So G = C = 25%.
If given A+T/G+C ratio: AT% + GC% = 100%. GC% = 100 − AT%.

NEET 2024 In a DNA double helix, if adenine content is 20%, what is the percentage of guanine?
Answer: 30%

A = 20% → T = 20% (Chargaff's rule). So A + T = 40%. Remaining G + C = 60%. Since G = C → G = 30%. C = 30% also. Quick formula: G% = (100 − 2A%) / 2 = (100 − 40) / 2 = 30%.

Watson-Crick Double Helix Model — NCERT Complete Foundation

  • Two polynucleotide chains wound around a common axis → right-handed double helix
  • Sugar-phosphate backbone on the outside; nitrogenous bases on the inside
  • Two strands are antiparallel — one runs 5'→3', the other 3'→5'
  • Bases held by hydrogen bonds: A-T = 2 H-bonds; G-C = 3 H-bonds
  • Stacking interactions between adjacent base pairs (hydrophobic) stabilize the helix additionally
  • Diameter: 2 nm (20 Å). Pitch (one complete turn): 3.4 nm. Base pairs per turn: 10. Distance between adjacent bp: 0.34 nm (3.4 Å).
  • Nucleosome: DNA wrapped around histone octamer (2 each of H2A, H2B, H3, H4). ~200 bp of DNA per nucleosome. H1 holds DNA at entry/exit (linker). "Beads on a string" = chromatin (level of packing 1). Further packing → solenoid → 30nm fibre.
  • Total DNA in human cell: ~6.6 × 10⁹ bp = 6.6 pg. Total length if stretched = ~2.2 metres per cell.

DNA Replication — Semi-conservative Model 3–4 Qs/yr

Semi-conservative Replication (Meselson-Stahl, 1958)

Each new DNA molecule retains one original (parental) strand and has one newly synthesized strand. Proved using ¹⁵N (heavy nitrogen) in E. coli. After one generation in ¹⁴N medium → hybrid density (¹⁵N-¹⁴N). After two generations → half hybrid + half light (both ¹⁴N).

Leading vs Lagging Strand
  • Leading strand: Synthesized continuously (template is 3'→5'). DNA pol III moves toward fork. One RNA primer suffices.
  • Lagging strand: Synthesized discontinuously in short fragments = Okazaki fragments (~150–200 nt in eukaryotes; ~1000–2000 in prokaryotes). Template is 5'→3' — synthesis moves AWAY from fork. Multiple RNA primers needed. Okazaki fragments joined by Ligase.
  • Replication is bidirectional from origins of replication. E. coli has ONE origin (oriC). Eukaryotes have MULTIPLE origins.
NEET 2023 DNA synthesis is always in which direction? (A) 3'→5' (B) 5'→3' (C) Both directions (D) Either direction depending on strand
Answer: (B) 5'→3' only

DNA polymerase can ONLY add nucleotides to the 3'-OH end of a growing chain → synthesis is always 5'→3'. The template is read 3'→5'. This is why the lagging strand requires multiple Okazaki fragments — it must be synthesized discontinuously in short 5'→3' segments moving away from the fork.

The Genetic Code — Complete NCERT Analysis Must Master

Properties of the Genetic Code
  • Triplet code (Codon): Each amino acid is coded by a sequence of 3 nucleotide bases (codon) in mRNA. 4³ = 64 possible codons.
  • Unambiguous: Each codon specifies ONLY ONE amino acid (but not vice versa — one AA can have multiple codons)
  • Degenerate (Redundant): More than one codon can code for the same amino acid. 64 codons for only 20 amino acids → degeneracy. Usually the 3rd base varies (wobble position).
  • Non-overlapping: Codons are read sequentially, each base is part of only one codon (no overlap)
  • Commaless (no punctuation): No bases between codons act as punctuation
  • Universal: Same codons specify same amino acids in (nearly) ALL organisms from bacteria to humans. Exceptions: mitochondria, some protists.
  • Start codon: AUG — codes for Methionine (Met) AND signals start of translation. Every protein begins with Met (removed later in many cases).
  • Stop codons (3): UAA, UAG, UGA — do NOT code for any amino acid. Terminate translation. (Mnemonic: U Are Amber, U Are Gone, U Go Away)
  • Near-universal codon: The genetic code is shared across all life — evidence for common ancestry.

NEET 2022 Which property of the genetic code ensures that a codon specifies only one amino acid?
Answer: Unambiguous (Specific)

The genetic code is unambiguous — each codon specifies only ONE amino acid. It is also degenerate — one amino acid can have multiple codons. These two properties are different: ambiguous would mean one codon → multiple amino acids (which does NOT happen). Degeneracy means one amino acid ← multiple codons (which DOES happen).

Gene Expression — Transcription, Translation & Regulation

Central Dogma of Molecular Biology Foundation

Central Dogma (Francis Crick, 1958)

DNA → RNA → Protein

Transcription: DNA → mRNA (using DNA as template)

Translation: mRNA → Protein (using ribosomes)

Reverse Transcription: RNA → DNA (by Reverse Transcriptase — in retroviruses like HIV). Discovered by Temin & Baltimore (1970). Nobel Prize 1975.

Exceptions to dogma: Some RNA viruses replicate RNA→RNA (RNA-dependent RNA polymerase / RdRp). HIV: RNA→DNA→RNA→Protein.

Transcription — DNA to RNA Deep Analysis

Prokaryotic Transcription (E. coli)

RNA Polymerase & Transcription Machinery
  • RNA Polymerase (single enzyme in prokaryotes) = core enzyme + σ (sigma) factor. Sigma factor recognizes promoter. Core enzyme = α₂ββ'ω.
  • No primer needed — RNA polymerase can start de novo (unlike DNA polymerase)
  • Template strand (antisense, 3'→5') is read by RNA pol → mRNA synthesized 5'→3'
  • Coding strand (sense, non-template, 5'→3') has same sequence as mRNA (except T→U)
  • Promoter: Region of DNA where RNA pol binds to initiate transcription. Prokaryote promoters: -10 (TATAAT = Pribnow box) and -35 sequences upstream of transcription start (+1).
  • Termination: Rho-independent (hairpin loop formed in mRNA → releases RNA pol) or Rho-dependent (Rho protein unwinds RNA-DNA hybrid).
  • In prokaryotes: No separate compartment → coupled transcription-translation (ribosomes attach to mRNA before it is even complete)

Post-transcriptional Processing (mRNA Maturation in Eukaryotes)

  • 5' Capping: 7-methylguanosine cap added at 5' end. Protects mRNA from degradation + aids ribosome recognition.
  • 3' Polyadenylation (Poly-A tail): ~200 adenine nucleotides added at 3' end by poly-A polymerase. Increases mRNA stability; aids export from nucleus.
  • Splicing (RNA splicing): Introns (non-coding sequences) removed; exons (coding) joined. Done by spliceosome (small nuclear ribonucleoproteins, snRNPs).
  • Result: Mature mRNA (5'cap + 5'UTR + coding sequence + 3'UTR + polyA tail) exported to cytoplasm.
  • Alternative splicing: Same pre-mRNA can give different mature mRNAs (different exon combinations) → different proteins from same gene → protein diversity

Translation — Protein Synthesis 3–4 Qs/yr

tRNA — Adapter Molecule

  • tRNA = adapter molecule that reads genetic code and brings specific amino acids
  • Cloverleaf structure (secondary) → L-shaped (3D, tertiary)
  • Anticodon loop: Contains 3 bases complementary to mRNA codon (antiparallel, anticodons read 3'→5' to pair with 5'→3' codon)
  • 3' CCA end: Amino acid attachment site. ALL tRNAs end in ...CCA-3'
  • Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase: Enzyme that charges tRNA with specific amino acid (uses ATP). Each AA has its own specific synthetase. "Second genetic code" — these enzymes must correctly recognize both the AA and its tRNA.
  • Wobble hypothesis (Crick): The 3rd position of anticodon can form non-standard base pairs with 1st position of codon (5' end) → one tRNA can recognize multiple codons (explains degeneracy at molecular level)

Translation Steps — Initiation, Elongation, Termination

Translation — Step by Step (Prokaryote)
  • Initiation: Small (30S) subunit binds mRNA at Shine-Dalgarno sequence (near 5' end). fMet-tRNAfMet (initiator tRNA) binds AUG in P site. Large (50S) subunit joins → 70S initiation complex forms. Requires Initiation Factors (IF1, IF2, IF3). GTP energy.
  • Elongation: (a) Aminoacyl-tRNA binding — charged tRNA enters A site (EF-Tu·GTP assists). (b) Peptide bond formation — Peptidyl transferase (part of 23S rRNA = ribozyme!) connects peptide chain from P site AA to A site AA. (c) Translocation — ribosome moves 3 nt (one codon) toward 3' end of mRNA; old peptidyl-tRNA moves from A → P; uncharged tRNA exits from E site. EF-G (translocase) + GTP. Repeated for each codon.
  • Termination: Stop codon (UAA/UAG/UGA) enters A site. No tRNA recognizes stop codons. Release factors (RF1, RF2, RF3) bind → hydrolyze peptide from tRNA → polypeptide released → ribosome dissociates into subunits.
NEET 2023 Peptide bond formation during translation is catalysed by: (A) Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase (B) Peptidyl transferase activity of 23S rRNA (C) EF-Tu (D) Release factors
Answer: (B) Peptidyl transferase of 23S rRNA

The peptide bond is formed by the peptidyl transferase activity inherent in the 23S rRNA of the large (50S) ribosomal subunit — it is a ribozyme. This is a crucial fact: the catalyst is RNA, not protein. This proved that the ribosome is essentially an RNA enzyme. Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase charges tRNA (not in ribosome).

Gene Regulation — Operon Model (Jacob & Monod, 1961) NCERT Classic

Lac Operon — Negative Regulation (Inducible System)
  • Genes: lacZ (β-galactosidase — cleaves lactose → glucose + galactose), lacY (permease — imports lactose), lacA (transacetylase)
  • Components: Promoter (where RNA pol binds), Operator (where repressor binds), Structural genes (Z,Y,A), Regulator gene (lacI — produces lac repressor)
  • Without lactose (repressed): Lac repressor (made by lacI) binds to Operator → blocks RNA pol → NO transcription of lacZYA → no β-galactosidase
  • With lactose (induced): Lactose (converted to allolactose) = inducer. Allolactose binds lac repressor → allosteric change → repressor CANNOT bind operator → RNA pol transcribes lacZYA → β-galactosidase made → lactose digested
  • CAP (catabolite activator protein) + cAMP: When glucose is LOW → cAMP HIGH → cAMP-CAP complex binds Promoter → activates transcription (CRP site). Glucose HIGH → cAMP LOW → no CAP activation → lower transcription. This is positive regulation by catabolite repression.
Trp Operon — Repressible System (Anabolic)

Tryptophan biosynthesis. When Trp is HIGH → Trp acts as co-repressor → binds inactive aporepressor → active repressor → binds operator → blocks genes → no more Trp synthesis. When Trp is LOW → no repressor formed → genes ON → Trp synthesized.

Key difference: Lac = inducible (substrate turns genes ON). Trp = repressible (product turns genes OFF).

NEET 2024 In lac operon, in the presence of lactose: (A) Repressor binds to operator (B) Allolactose binds repressor, preventing it from binding operator (C) RNA polymerase cannot bind to promoter (D) lacZ gene is not expressed
Answer: (B)

Lactose → converted to allolactose (true inducer). Allolactose binds the lac repressor → conformational change → repressor CANNOT bind operator → RNA polymerase proceeds → lacZYA transcribed → β-galactosidase produced → lactose digested. This is negative inducible regulation.

  • Deletion: Base(s) removed → reading frame shifts similarly
  • Insertions/deletions of 3 bases (or multiples of 3) do NOT shift frame — just add/remove amino acids
Sickle-Cell Anaemia — Classic Point Mutation

Mutation: Single nucleotide change in HBB gene (chromosome 11). GAG → GTG in codon 6 of β-globin chain.

Result: Glutamic acid (hydrophilic, charged) → Valine (hydrophobic). HbA → HbS.

Effect: In low O₂ conditions, HbS polymerizes into fibers → RBCs become sickle-shaped → block capillaries → pain crises, anaemia, organ damage.

Inheritance: Autosomal recessive. Homozygous (HbSHbS) = severely affected. Heterozygous sickle-cell trait (HbAHbS) = carrier; protected against falciparum malaria (balanced polymorphism).

Theories of Evolution — Complete Analysis Most Tested

Lamarckism — Theory of Acquired Characters (1809)
  • Jean Baptiste de Lamarck — "Philosophie Zoologique" (1809)
  • Use and Disuse: Organs used extensively develop and enlarge; unused organs atrophy and disappear
  • Inheritance of Acquired Characters: Characters acquired during lifetime are passed to offspring
  • Classic example: Giraffe stretched its neck → longer neck → passed to offspring
  • Refuted by: Weismann cut tails of mice for 22 generations → offspring still had tails. Genetic information only in germline, not somatic cells.
Darwinism — Natural Selection (1859, "Origin of Species")
  • Charles Darwin + Alfred Russel Wallace (proposed simultaneously!)
  • Key observations: Overproduction of offspring; Limited resources → Struggle for existence; Variations among individuals; Survival of the fittest; Favourable variations inherited → accumulate → new species
  • Fitness: Reproductive fitness — ability to survive AND reproduce in given environment
  • Natural Selection: Environment selects individuals with beneficial variations → differential reproduction
  • Darwin's finches: 14 species on Galápagos Islands — different beak sizes/shapes based on food source → adaptive radiation
  • Weakness: Darwin could NOT explain the source of variations or mechanism of heredity (pre-Mendel)
Neo-Darwinism (Modern Synthetic Theory)
  • Synthesis of Darwin's natural selection + Mendelian genetics + population genetics
  • Main architects: Fisher, Haldane, Sewall Wright, Dobzhansky, Mayr, Simpson
  • Mechanisms of Evolution:
  • 1. Mutation — ultimate source of new alleles
  • 2. Recombination — creates new allele combinations during meiosis
  • 3. Natural selection — differential reproduction based on fitness
  • 4. Genetic drift — random changes in allele frequency in small populations
  • 5. Gene flow (Migration) — movement of alleles between populations
  • Evolution = change in allele frequencies in a population over time

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium — Population Genetics Numerical Qs

Hardy-Weinberg Theorem (1908)

In an ideal, large random-mating population, allele frequencies remain constant from generation to generation IF:

1. No mutation · 2. No selection · 3. No migration · 4. No genetic drift · 5. Random mating

Equations: If p = frequency of dominant allele (A), q = frequency of recessive allele (a):

Then: p + q = 1 (allele frequencies)

And: p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (genotype frequencies: p² = AA, 2pq = Aa, q² = aa)

Deviations from H-W = evolution is occurring. Causes: mutation, selection, drift, migration, non-random mating.

NEET 2023 In a population, if frequency of recessive genotype (aa) is 0.09, what is the frequency of heterozygous (Aa) individuals?
Answer: 0.42

q² = 0.09 → q = 0.3. Since p + q = 1 → p = 0.7. Frequency of Aa = 2pq = 2 × 0.7 × 0.3 = 0.42. Frequency of AA = p² = 0.49. Check: 0.49 + 0.42 + 0.09 = 1.00 ✓

Industrial Melanism — Biston betularia — Classic NEET Example

Peppered Moth — Industrial Melanism

Before industrial revolution: Lichen-covered (white) tree trunks → white moths (Biston betularia typica) = camouflaged, survived. Rare melanic (dark) moths eaten by birds.

After industrial revolution: Soot-covered tree bark → dark moths = camouflaged, SURVIVED. White moths became visible → eaten → decreased.

Conclusion: Natural selection acts on existing variation (pre-existing dark moths); it does NOT CREATE the variation. Environment selects — it does not direct mutation.

Studied by H.B.D. Kettlewell (1950s). Classic proof of directional natural selection.

Species Concepts & Speciation Key Definitions

  • Biological species concept (Mayr): A group of interbreeding natural populations that are reproductively isolated from others
  • Allopatric speciation: Geographic isolation → populations separated by a barrier → independent evolution → reproductive isolation → new species. Example: Darwin's finches on different Galápagos islands.
  • Sympatric speciation: New species from within the same geographical area — via reproductive isolation (different mating times, preferences, polyploidy in plants). Common in plants via polyploidy.
  • Reproductive isolating mechanisms:
    • Pre-zygotic: Habitat isolation, temporal (seasonal) isolation, behavioural (sexual preference), mechanical (different flower structures), gametic incompatibility
    • Post-zygotic: Hybrid inviability, hybrid sterility (mule = horse × donkey), hybrid breakdown

Restriction Enzymes — Molecular Scissors Every NEET

Restriction Endonucleases

Enzymes that cut DNA at specific recognition sequences (palindromic sequences). Named: first letter of genus + first 2 letters of species + strain number (if needed) + Roman numeral (order of discovery). Example: EcoRI = from Escherichia coli strain R, 1st restriction enzyme discovered.

Palindromic sequence: Reads the same on both strands 5'→3'. Example: EcoRI cuts at 5'...G↓AATTC...3' / 3'...CTTAA↑G...5' → produces sticky ends (5' overhangs: 5'AATT).

Key Biotechnology Techniques NEET Essential

PCR — Polymerase Chain Reaction (Kary Mullis, 1983)

PCR — Steps
  • Denaturation (94–96°C): Double-stranded DNA heats → strands separate
  • Annealing (50–65°C): Short DNA primers (20–25 bp) complementary to target flanking sequences bind specifically
  • Extension (72°C): Taq DNA polymerase (from Thermus aquaticus, thermostable) extends from primers → new DNA synthesized
  • Cycle repeated 30–40 times → ~10⁹ copies of target DNA

Key: Taq polymerase is thermostable → not denatured at 94°C. Nobel Prize to Kary Mullis in 1993 for PCR development.

Gel Electrophoresis

  • Separates DNA fragments by size through agarose gel under electric field
  • DNA = negatively charged → moves toward anode (+) (positive pole)
  • Smaller fragments move faster (further) through gel; Larger fragments move slower (less far)
  • DNA stained with Ethidium bromide (EtBr) → fluoresces orange under UV light
  • Bands compared to DNA ladder (marker) with known sizes to determine fragment sizes
  • Southern blotting: DNA fragments separated by gel → transferred to nitrocellulose membrane → hybridized with labeled probe

Recombinant DNA Technology — Overall Process

  • Identify and isolate gene of interest
  • Cut gene + vector with same restriction enzyme → compatible sticky ends
  • Ligate gene into vector using DNA ligase → recombinant DNA formed
  • Transform into host (usually E. coli) — by heat shock, electroporation
  • Select transformants (antibiotic selection, blue-white screening)
  • Grow transformants → produce protein product

Bioethics, Biosafety & Biopatents Emerging NEET Topic

🫛 MENDEL'S LAWS — Decision Layer

If NEET asks: "Law of Segregation applies to which type of cross?"
Law of Segregation = monohybrid (ONE trait). Law of Independent Assortment = dihybrid (TWO or more traits on different chromosomes).
❌ "Independent assortment applies to linked genes" → WRONG. Linked genes violate independent assortment. ✅ Independent assortment: only non-linked (different chromosomes) genes. Morgan's exception = linkage.
If NEET asks: "Phenotypic ratio in incomplete dominance F₂?"
❌ 3:1 → That's COMPLETE dominance ✅ 1:2:1 — same as genotypic ratio. Red : Pink : White (Antirrhinum / Mirabilis)
Key distinction: In incomplete dominance, phenotypic ratio = genotypic ratio (1:2:1). In complete dominance, phenotypic ≠ genotypic (3:1 vs 1:2:1).
PYQ Pattern — Lethal alleles: "Yellow coat in mice" cross: Aʸ Aʸ = lethal. So Aʸ A × Aʸ A → 1 AʸAʸ (dead) : 2 AʸA (yellow) : 1 AA (agouti). LIVE ratio = 2:1 (yellow:agouti). NOT 3:1.
Trap: Students apply standard 3:1 — WRONG because one class is lethal. Always check if a genotype class is non-viable.
Dihybrid F₂ (9:3:3:1)→Modification by epistasis→Complementary: 9:7 | Dominant epistasis: 12:3:1 | Recessive epistasis: 9:3:4 | Duplicate dominant: 15:1
NCERT: "Genes present on same chromosome are linked and do not assort independently"
→ Q: Exception to Mendel's law? → Linked genes (Law of Independent Assortment violated)
→ Q: Who studied linkage? → T.H. Morgan with Drosophila
→ Q: What breaks linkage? → Crossing over (recombination during meiosis I)

🧬 MOLECULAR GENETICS — Decision Layer

If NEET asks: "Which enzyme removes RNA primer?"
DNA Polymerase I — has 5'→3' exonuclease activity. Removes primer, fills gap with DNA. Then DNA Ligase seals the nick.
❌ DNA Pol III — main synthesizing enzyme; no 5'→3' exonuclease ❌ DNA Ligase — joins fragments; does NOT remove primers ❌ Helicase — unwinds DNA; no primer removal ✅ DNA Pol I → removes primer → fills gap | DNA Ligase → seals nick
If NEET asks: "Peptide bond formation is catalysed by?"
❌ Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase — charges tRNA; NOT in ribosome ❌ EF-Tu — elongation factor; escorts aminoacyl-tRNA to A site ❌ Release factors — terminate translation ✅ Peptidyl transferase activity of 23S rRNA (prokaryote) = RIBOZYME. RNA is the catalyst.
DNA strand synthesis direction — hardest elimination:
❌ 3'→5' synthesis — IMPOSSIBLE for polymerase ✅ ALL synthesis = 5'→3' (adds to free 3'-OH) ✅ Template read = 3'→5' (antiparallel) ✅ Lagging strand = synthesized in Okazaki fragments (each fragment 5'→3') moving away from fork
Connected concept (within Genetics file): Okazaki fragments → DNA Pol I removes primers → Ligase joins = same enzyme cascade as above.
No primer, no synthesis→Primase makes RNA primer (~10 nt)→DNA Pol III extends (5'→3')→DNA Pol I removes primer + fills→Ligase joins nick→Complete new strand
Genetic code property elimination — most tested:
❌ "Ambiguous = one codon → multiple amino acids" — DOES NOT EXIST in standard code ❌ "Non-degenerate" — WRONG. Code IS degenerate (2+ codons per AA) ✅ Unambiguous = each codon → ONLY ONE AA (but one AA can have multiple codons) ✅ Degenerate = multiple codons → same AA. Exception: Met (AUG) and Trp (UGG) — each has only ONE codon
PYQ Pattern — Chargaff numerical: "If A=30% in dsDNA, what is G%?"
Step 1: A=T → T=30%. Step 2: A+T=60%. Step 3: G+C=40%. Step 4: G=C → G=20%.
Formula shortcut: G% = (100 − 2×A%) ÷ 2
Trap: Students say G=30% (confusing G=C with A=T pattern). Always compute G+C FIRST.
NCERT: "In eukaryotes, RNA polymerase does not directly initiate transcription"
→ Q: What helps RNA Pol II bind promoter? → Transcription factors (TF IID binds TATA box first)
→ Q: Which RNA Pol makes mRNA? → RNA Pol II (in eukaryotes)
→ Connected concept (within Genetics): RNA Pol I → rRNA (nucleolus); RNA Pol III → tRNA

🔬 OPERON MODEL — Decision Layer

If NEET asks: "In presence of lactose, what happens to lac repressor?"
Lactose → converted to allolactose (true inducer). Allolactose binds lac repressor → conformational change → repressor CANNOT bind operator → RNA pol transcribes lacZYA → β-galactosidase produced.
❌ "Lactose binds repressor directly" — FALSE. Allolactose (not lactose itself) is the inducer ❌ "Lactose destroys repressor" — FALSE. Repressor is inactivated, not destroyed ✅ Lactose → allolactose → binds repressor → operator FREE → transcription ON
Glucose LOW→cAMP HIGH→cAMP + CAP complex→Binds promoter upstream→Enhances RNA pol binding→Transcription ↑↑ (catabolite activation)
Glucose HIGH→cAMP LOW→No CAP-cAMP→RNA pol barely binds→Transcription LOW even if lactose present (glucose preferred)
Trp operon vs Lac operon — direct elimination:
❌ "Trp operon is inducible" — WRONG. Trp = repressible ❌ "Lac operon = catabolic = repressible" — WRONG. Lac = inducible ✅ Lac = inducible (substrate turns ON; anabolic/catabolic: catabolic). Trp = repressible (product turns OFF; biosynthetic) ✅ Trp = co-repressor system: inactive aporepressor + Trp → active repressor → blocks operator

🦕 EVOLUTION — Decision Layer

Hardy-Weinberg numerical — standard NEET method:
ALWAYS start from q² (recessive homozygote frequency — easiest to identify phenotypically). Then q = √q². Then p = 1−q. Then 2pq = heterozygote frequency. Check: p² + 2pq + q² = 1.
❌ Common trap: confuse phenotypic frequency with genotypic frequency. aa = q² (genotype). "Affected individuals" (recessive disease) = q² only. ✅ Carriers (heterozygous) = 2pq. Always calculate from q² → q → p → 2pq.
If NEET asks: "Industrial melanism is an example of which type of selection?"
❌ Stabilising selection — favours intermediate; melanism = extremes shift ❌ Disruptive selection — favours BOTH extremes; melanism = ONE direction ✅ Directional selection — one extreme favoured (dark moths) over time due to changed environment
Key: Selection acts on PRE-EXISTING variation — dark moths existed before pollution. Environment selects, does NOT create mutations.
Hominid brain size ranking — exam-ready order:
Australopithecus (450 cc) → Homo habilis (800 cc) → Homo erectus (1000 cc) → Homo sapiens (1350 cc). Neanderthals had ~1400 cc (slightly larger than modern human; NCERT states 1400 so pick Neanderthal if option present).
❌ "H. habilis used fire" — WRONG. H. erectus used fire ❌ "Australopithecus made tools" — WRONG. Used stone weapons, did NOT make them ✅ H. habilis = FIRST to make/use tools (Oldowan flint)
Miller-Urey (1953): H₂+CH₄+NH₃+H₂O+electric sparks→Amino acids + sugars synthesized→Proves abiogenesis possible→Disproves "life only from life" for ORIGIN (not ongoing)
PYQ Pattern — Homologous vs Analogous:
Homologous = SAME structure / DIFFERENT function → divergent evolution → common ancestor. Example: forelimbs (whale fin, bat wing, horse leg, human arm).
Analogous = DIFFERENT structure / SAME function → convergent evolution → NO common ancestor. Example: insect wing vs bird wing; dolphin flipper vs shark fin.
Trap: "Sweet potato and potato are analogous organs" → ROOT (modified) vs STEM (modified) → same function (storage) but diff origin → Analogous.
