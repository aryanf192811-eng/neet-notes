Electric Charges, Fields & Electrostatic Potential

Coulomb's Law & Electric Force 🔴 High Freq

Coulomb's Law

$$F = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$

where $k = 9\times10^9$ N·m²/C², $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85\times10^{-12}$ C²/N·m²

In medium: $F_{med} = \dfrac{F_{vacuum}}{\varepsilon_r}$ where $\varepsilon_r$ = relative permittivity (dielectric constant)

Key Constants

$k = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} = 9\times10^9$ N·m²/C²

$\varepsilon_0 = 8.85\times10^{-12}$ C²/(N·m²)

$e = 1.6\times10^{-19}$ C (charge of electron)

$1$ eV $= 1.6\times10^{-19}$ J

Superposition Principle

$\vec{F}_{net} = \vec{F}_{12} + \vec{F}_{13} + \ldots + \vec{F}_{1n}$

Vector addition of all individual Coulomb forces.

Forces are independent of other charges present.

NEET Trick: Force between two charges in medium of dielectric constant $K$: $F' = F/K$. If medium is water ($K\approx80$), force reduces 80 times.

Electric Field 🔴 High Freq

Electric Field

$$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0} = \frac{kQ}{r^2}\hat{r}$$

Point Charge

$E = \dfrac{kq}{r^2} = \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$

Infinite Line Charge

$E = \dfrac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}$

$\lambda$ = linear charge density (C/m)

Infinite Plane Sheet

$E = \dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}$ (single sheet)

$E = \dfrac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}$ (between two opposite sheets)

Uniformly Charged Ring (axis)

$E = \dfrac{kQx}{(R^2+x^2)^{3/2}}$

Max at $x = R/\sqrt{2}$

Electric Dipole

Axial: $E = \dfrac{2kp}{r^3}$ (along axis)

Equatorial: $E = \dfrac{kp}{r^3}$ (⊥ to axis)

$p = qd$ (dipole moment)

Dipole in Uniform Field

Torque: $\tau = pE\sin\theta = \vec{p}\times\vec{E}$

PE: $U = -pE\cos\theta = -\vec{p}\cdot\vec{E}$

Gauss's Law 🔵 Medium Freq

Gauss's Law

$$\oint \vec{E}\cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

Total electric flux through a closed surface = charge enclosed / $\varepsilon_0$

Electric Flux: $\phi = EA\cos\theta$

Solid Sphere (outside, r > R)

$E = \dfrac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$ (same as point charge)

Solid Sphere (inside, r < R)

$E = \dfrac{Qr}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R^3} = \dfrac{\rho r}{3\varepsilon_0}$

At center: $E = 0$

Hollow/Shell (outside, r > R)

$E = \dfrac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}$

Hollow/Shell (inside, r < R)

$E = 0$ (no field inside shell)

This is electrostatic shielding

Electric Potential & Potential Energy 🔴 High Freq

Electric Potential

$V = \dfrac{kQ}{r} = \dfrac{W}{q_0}$ (work done per unit charge)

$E = -\dfrac{dV}{dr}$ (relation between E and V)

$V_{shell\ inside} = \dfrac{kQ}{R}$ = constant

Potential Energy

Two charges: $U = \dfrac{kq_1q_2}{r}$

System of charges: $U = \sum_{i<j}\dfrac{kq_iq_j}{r_{ij}}$

$W = q\Delta V = q(V_f - V_i)$

Equipotential Surfaces

No work done moving charge on equipotential surface

$\vec{E} \perp$ equipotential surfaces always

Closer surfaces → stronger field

Dipole Potential

$V = \dfrac{kp\cos\theta}{r^2}$

Axial point: $V = \dfrac{kp}{r^2}$

Equatorial point: $V = 0$

Capacitance & Capacitors 🔴 High Freq

Basic Capacitance

$$C = \frac{Q}{V}$$

Unit: Farad (F). $1$ F $= 1$ C/V

Parallel Plate Capacitor

$C_0 = \dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$ (air/vacuum)

$C = \dfrac{K\varepsilon_0 A}{d} = KC_0$ (with dielectric K)

$E = \dfrac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0} = \dfrac{V}{d}$ (field between plates)

Spherical Capacitor

$C = 4\pi\varepsilon_0\dfrac{ab}{b-a}$

Isolated sphere: $C = 4\pi\varepsilon_0 R$

Series Combination

$\dfrac{1}{C_{eq}} = \dfrac{1}{C_1} + \dfrac{1}{C_2} + \ldots$

Same $Q$, different $V$

$C_{eq} < C_{min}$

Parallel Combination

$C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 + \ldots$

Same $V$, different $Q$

$C_{eq} > C_{max}$

Energy Stored in Capacitor

$$U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{QV}{2}$$
Energy Density: $u = \dfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$ (energy per unit volume)
With Dielectric (battery connected)

$V = $ const, $C$ increases by $K$

$Q$ increases by $K$, $U$ increases by $K$

Energy supplied by battery: $\Delta U_{battery} = KU_0$

With Dielectric (isolated)

$Q = $ const, $C$ increases by $K$

$V$ decreases by $K$, $U$ decreases by $K$

$U_{new} = U_0/K$

Ohm's Law & Resistance 🔴 High Freq

Ohm's Law

$$V = IR \qquad R = \frac{\rho L}{A}$$

$\rho$ = resistivity (Ω·m), $L$ = length, $A$ = cross-section area

Conductance: $G = 1/R$ (siemens, S); Conductivity: $\sigma = 1/\rho$

Current Density

$J = \dfrac{I}{A} = \sigma E = nev_d$

$v_d$ = drift velocity, $n$ = free electron density

$I = neAv_d$

Mobility

$\mu = \dfrac{v_d}{E} = \dfrac{e\tau}{m}$

$\tau$ = relaxation time

$\sigma = ne\mu$

Temperature Dependence

$\rho_T = \rho_0[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)]$

$R_T = R_0[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)]$

Metals: $\alpha > 0$ (resistance increases)

Semiconductors: $\alpha < 0$ (resistance decreases)

Resistivity Values (Approx.)

Conductor: $\rho \sim 10^{-8}$ Ω·m

Semiconductor: $\rho \sim 10^{-3}$ to $10^4$ Ω·m

Insulator: $\rho \sim 10^{11}$ to $10^{19}$ Ω·m

Resistor Combinations 🔴 High Freq

Series

$R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + \ldots + R_n$

Same current, different voltages

$V_i = IR_i$ (voltage divider)

Parallel

$\dfrac{1}{R_{eq}} = \dfrac{1}{R_1} + \dfrac{1}{R_2} + \ldots$

Same voltage, different currents

$I_i = V/R_i$ (current divider)

Two Resistors Parallel

$R_{eq} = \dfrac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$

$I_1 = I\dfrac{R_2}{R_1+R_2}$, $I_2 = I\dfrac{R_1}{R_1+R_2}$

n Equal Resistors

Series: $R_{eq} = nR$

Parallel: $R_{eq} = R/n$

Kirchhoff's Laws, EMF & Wheatstone Bridge 🔴 High Freq

Kirchhoff's Laws

KCL (Junction Rule): $\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$ — Conservation of charge

KVL (Loop Rule): $\sum V = 0$ around any closed loop — Conservation of energy

Cell with Internal Resistance

$\varepsilon = V + Ir$ → $V = \varepsilon - Ir$

$I = \dfrac{\varepsilon}{R + r}$ (terminal voltage drops under load)

$V_{terminal} = \varepsilon - Ir$

Cells in Series

$\varepsilon_{eq} = \varepsilon_1 + \varepsilon_2 + \ldots$

$r_{eq} = r_1 + r_2 + \ldots$

Best for high-resistance loads

Cells in Parallel (identical)

$\varepsilon_{eq} = \varepsilon$, $r_{eq} = r/n$

$I = \dfrac{n\varepsilon}{nR + r}$

Best for low-resistance loads

Wheatstone Bridge (balanced)

$$\frac{P}{Q} = \frac{R}{S}$$ No current through galvanometer

Used to find unknown resistance

Metre Bridge

$$\frac{R}{S} = \frac{l}{100-l}$$

where $l$ = length from left for balance. $R$ = known, $S$ = unknown, $l$ = balance length in cm.

Potentiometer

Principle: $\dfrac{\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_2} = \dfrac{l_1}{l_2}$ (comparison of EMFs)

Internal resistance: $r = R\left(\dfrac{l_1}{l_2} - 1\right)$ where $l_1$ = balance length without R, $l_2$ = with R

Potentiometer has infinite resistance — draws no current, more accurate than voltmeter.

Electrical Power & Energy 🔴 High Freq

$$P = VI = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$
Energy: $W = Pt = VIt = I^2Rt$
Unit: Watt (W) = J/s; Energy in kWh (1 kWh = 3.6×10⁶ J)
Max Power Transfer

$P_{max}$ when $R_{ext} = r$ (internal resistance)

$P_{max} = \dfrac{\varepsilon^2}{4r}$

Heating Effect (Joule's Law)

$H = I^2Rt$ (heat produced in joules)

$H = \dfrac{V^2t}{R}$

$H = VIt$

Cyclotron

$v_{max} = \dfrac{qBr_0}{m}$ ($r_0$ = dee radius)

$KE_{max} = \dfrac{q^2B^2r_0^2}{2m}$

Cannot accelerate electrons (relativistic)

Faraday's Laws & Lenz's Law 🔴 High Freq

Faraday's Law of EMI

$$\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -N\frac{d\Phi}{dt}$$

Magnetic Flux: $\Phi = BA\cos\theta$ (Wb or T·m²)

Lenz's Law: Induced current opposes the change in flux (negative sign in Faraday's law)

Lenz's law = Conservation of Energy. Induced current always creates magnetic field opposing the change — this is why it takes work to move a magnet toward a coil.
