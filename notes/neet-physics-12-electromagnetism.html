Biot-Savart Law Most Tested Formula

The Biot-Savart law is the magnetic equivalent of Coulomb's law. It gives the magnetic field produced by an infinitesimal current element.

Biot-Savart Law

$$d\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{I\,d\vec{l} \times \hat{r}}{r^2}$$

Direction: Right-hand rule on $d\vec{l} \times \hat{r}$. Magnitude: $dB = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{I\,dl\sin\theta}{r^2}$

🎯 Critical Results — NEET Tests These Directly

1. Magnetic Field at Centre of a Circular Loop

This is the most repeated result from this chapter. For a circular loop of radius $R$ carrying current $I$:

$$B_{centre} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R}$$
For $n$ turns: $B = \frac{\mu_0 n I}{2R}$

Direction: Curl the right hand in the direction of current — thumb points in direction of $\vec{B}$ at centre.

For arc of angle $\theta$ (in radians): $B = \frac{\mu_0 I \theta}{4\pi R}$. NEET 2023 asked this — semicircle gives $\theta=\pi$, so $B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4R}$.

NEET Trap: Two semicircles of radii $R_1$ and $R_2$ carrying same current $I$ — what is $B$ at common centre?

Answer: $B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4}\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right)$ if semicircles are wound oppositely. The field at the junction of two straight segments is zero (since $\sin 0° = 0$ in Biot-Savart).

2. Magnetic Field Due to a Straight Wire

$$B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi d}(\sin\phi_1 + \sin\phi_2)$$
Infinite wire: $B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi d}$

Where $d$ is the perpendicular distance from the wire, and $\phi_1, \phi_2$ are angles subtended at the point by the two ends of the wire.

Special Cases to Memorise

Semi-infinite wire (one end at the point): $B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi d}$ (only one $\sin$ term, second is $\sin 90° = 1$, first is $\sin 0° = 0$)  →  half of infinite wire result. This is frequently tested.

🎯 Ampere's Circuital Law

Ampere's law is the magnetic analogue of Gauss's law — use it when there is high symmetry.

Ampere's Circuital Law

$$\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc}$$

$I_{enc}$ = total current threading through the Amperian loop.

Results from Ampere's Law — NEET Classic

Infinite Solenoid
  • $B_{inside} = \mu_0 n I$
  • $n$ = turns per unit length
  • $B_{outside} = 0$
  • Field is uniform inside
  • Most asked: "find B at centre"
Toroid
  • $B = \frac{\mu_0 N I}{2\pi r}$
  • $r$ = radius of toroid
  • $B_{outside} = 0$ (no leakage)
  • $B_{inside\,hole} = 0$
  • Non-uniform field (varies with $r$)
Thick Wire (r < R)
  • $B = \frac{\mu_0 I r}{2\pi R^2}$ (inside)
  • $B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$ (outside)
  • $B \propto r$ inside
  • $B \propto 1/r$ outside
  • Max B at surface ($r = R$)
Classic NEET Trap — Solenoid vs Toroid

A toroid has zero field outside, but a solenoid has a very small but non-zero field outside (treated as zero in NEET). The field inside a toroid is NOT uniform (it depends on $r$). NEET 2019 explicitly tested: "B field at the hollow region inside a toroid" — answer is ZERO.

NEET 2024 A circular coil of radius $r$ has $n$ turns. A current $I$ flows through it. The magnetic field at the centre is $B$. If the coil is re-wound with $2n$ turns using the same length of wire (radius becomes $r/2$), the new magnetic field at centre is:
Answer: 4B

Key insight: Same wire length means $n \cdot 2\pi r = 2n \cdot 2\pi r'$ → $r' = r/2$.

Original: $B = \frac{\mu_0 n I}{2r}$

New: $B' = \frac{\mu_0 (2n) I}{2(r/2)} = \frac{\mu_0 \cdot 2nI}{r} = \frac{4\mu_0 nI}{2r} = 4B$

Pattern: When wire is rewound to double turns with same total length, radius halves, and $B$ becomes 4× original. This pattern has appeared 3 times in different forms.

NEET 2023 A long straight wire carries current $I$. A square loop of side $a$ is placed with one side parallel to the wire at distance $d$. The magnetic flux through the loop is:
Answer: $\frac{\mu_0 I a}{2\pi} \ln\left(\frac{d+a}{d}\right)$

Method: Consider a strip at distance $x$ from wire, width $dx$, length $a$. $dB = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi x}$, $d\Phi = B \cdot a\, dx$.

$$\Phi = \int_d^{d+a} \frac{\mu_0 I a}{2\pi x}dx = \frac{\mu_0 I a}{2\pi}\ln\left(\frac{d+a}{d}\right)$$

This is a standard integration — memorise the setup. The closer the side to the wire, the larger the flux (non-uniform field).

NEET 2022 The ratio of the magnetic field at the centre of a circular coil of radius $R$ and the magnetic field at the axial point at distance $R$ from the centre is:
Answer: $2\sqrt{2}$ : 1

$B_{centre} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R}$

$B_{axial} = \frac{\mu_0 I R^2}{2(R^2+x^2)^{3/2}}$ at $x = R$: $B_{axial} = \frac{\mu_0 I R^2}{2(2R^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\sqrt{2}R}$

Ratio: $\frac{\mu_0 I/2R}{\mu_0 I/4\sqrt{2}R} = \frac{4\sqrt{2}}{2} = 2\sqrt{2}$

Lorentz Force & Charged Particle Motion Concept + Numericals

Lorentz Force

$$\vec{F} = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B})$$

Magnetic force alone: $\vec{F} = q\vec{v} \times \vec{B}$, magnitude $F = qvB\sin\theta$

Circular Motion of Charged Particle in Magnetic Field

When a charged particle enters a uniform magnetic field perpendicular to it, the magnetic force provides centripetal force:

$$r = \frac{mv}{qB} = \frac{p}{qB}$$
Time period: $T = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}$    Frequency: $f = \frac{qB}{2\pi m}$
CRITICAL — What DOESN'T change when B changes?

Time period $T$ and frequency $f$ are independent of velocity and radius — they depend only on $m$, $q$, $B$. This is the principle of the cyclotron. NEET asks: "If speed doubles, what happens to radius?" → radius doubles. "What happens to T?" → T unchanged.

The "Same Kinetic Energy" Trap

NEET frequently compares particles with same KE entering a field:

$r = \frac{mv}{qB} = \frac{p}{qB}$ where $p = \sqrt{2mKE}$ So: $r = \frac{\sqrt{2mKE}}{qB}$ For same KE and B: r ∝ √m / q Proton vs α-particle (same KE): α: m=4u, q=2e → r_α = √(4u·KE)/(2e·B) = √(2u·KE)/(e·B) p: m=u, q=e → r_p = √(2u·KE)/(e·B) (same as α!) KEY RESULT: Proton and α-particle have the SAME radius when they have same KE in a magnetic field!

Three comparison scenarios for NEET:

Same velocity: $r \propto m/q$  |  Same KE: $r \propto \sqrt{m}/q$  |  Same momentum: $r \propto 1/q$

Helical Motion — When Particle Enters at Angle θ to B

Component parallel to $\vec{B}$: $v_\parallel = v\cos\theta$ — moves in straight line (no force). Component perpendicular: $v_\perp = v\sin\theta$ — moves in circle. Combined = helix.

Pitch $= v_\parallel \cdot T = \frac{2\pi m v\cos\theta}{qB}$     Radius of helix $= \frac{mv\sin\theta}{qB}$

Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

$$\vec{F} = I(\vec{L} \times \vec{B}) \quad \Rightarrow \quad F = BIL\sin\theta$$
Fleming's Left Hand Rule

For conventional current: Forefinger → $\vec{B}$, Middle finger → $\vec{I}$, Thumb → Force direction. For electron (negative charge): force is reversed.

Force Between Two Parallel Current-Carrying Wires

$$\frac{F}{L} = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi d}$$
Direction Rule — NEET Tests This Conceptually

Same direction currents → ATTRACT. Opposite direction currents → REPEL. This is the definition of 1 Ampere (force per unit length = $2\times10^{-7}$ N/m when $I_1=I_2=1A$, $d=1m$).

NEET 2025 A proton and an $\alpha$-particle are accelerated through the same potential difference $V$. The ratio of their de Broglie wavelengths is: [Note: this bridges ch with modern physics]
Answer: $\lambda_p / \lambda_\alpha = 2\sqrt{2}$

$KE = qV$. $\lambda = h/p = h/\sqrt{2mqV}$

$\lambda_p = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2 \cdot m_p \cdot e \cdot V}}$,   $\lambda_\alpha = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2 \cdot 4m_p \cdot 2e \cdot V}} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{16m_p eV}}$

Ratio: $\frac{\lambda_p}{\lambda_\alpha} = \frac{\sqrt{16m_p eV}}{\sqrt{2m_p eV}} = \sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2}$

NEET 2021 An electron moving with velocity $v$ in a uniform magnetic field $B$ moves in a circle of radius $r$. If the velocity is doubled, what is the new radius?
Answer: 2r

$r = mv/qB$. Since $m$, $q$, $B$ unchanged: $r \propto v$. So new radius $= 2r$.

The period T is unchanged — this is the most common follow-up trap: "What happens to time taken for one revolution?" → T = 2πm/qB, unchanged.

Torque on Current Loop & Galvanometer Guaranteed 1Q

Torque on a Current Loop

$$\tau = NIAB\sin\theta = MB\sin\theta$$
Magnetic moment: $M = NIA$     Potential energy: $U = -\vec{M}\cdot\vec{B} = -MB\cos\theta$

Stable equilibrium: $\theta = 0°$ ($\vec{M}$ parallel to $\vec{B}$, $U = -MB$, minimum energy). Unstable equilibrium: $\theta = 180°$ ($\vec{M}$ anti-parallel to $\vec{B}$, $U = +MB$, maximum energy). Maximum torque: at $\theta = 90°$.

Moving Coil Galvanometer (MCG)

In an MCG, a radial magnetic field is maintained so that $\sin\theta = 1$ always. The coil deflects against a restoring spring:

$$\text{Deflection: } \phi = \frac{NABI}{k} \quad \Rightarrow \quad I = \frac{k\phi}{NAB}$$
Current sensitivity: $S_I = \frac{\phi}{I} = \frac{NAB}{k}$     Voltage sensitivity: $S_V = \frac{S_I}{R_g} = \frac{NAB}{kR_g}$
Sensitivity Trap — Asked 3 Times

If number of turns $N$ doubles: current sensitivity doubles, but voltage sensitivity may decrease (if $R_g$ also increases proportionally with more turns). NEET 2018 asked this. Always check what parameter changes.

Ammeter and Voltmeter Conversion — NEET's Favourite

Galvanometer → Ammeter (Shunt resistor S in parallel)

$$S = \frac{I_g \cdot R_g}{I - I_g} \approx \frac{I_g R_g}{I} \text{ (when } I \gg I_g\text{)}$$

Galvanometer → Voltmeter (High resistance R in series)

$$R = \frac{V}{I_g} - R_g$$

Ideal Ammeter has zero resistance. Adding shunt brings $R_g$ down to $R_{eff} = \frac{R_g S}{R_g+S}$. An ammeter should have very low resistance. A voltmeter should have very high resistance. NEET will ask: "An ideal voltmeter connected across a circuit draws…" → zero current.

NEET 2024 A galvanometer has resistance $G = 25\,\Omega$ and full scale deflection current $I_g = 1\,\text{mA}$. To convert it into a voltmeter of range $0\text{–}10\,\text{V}$, the resistance that must be connected is:
Answer: 9975 Ω

$R = \frac{V}{I_g} - R_g = \frac{10}{10^{-3}} - 25 = 10000 - 25 = 9975\,\Omega$

Standard pattern: $R \approx V/I_g$ when $R_g \ll R$. Always subtract $R_g$.

NEET 2023 Two galvanometers $G_1$ (current sensitivity $S_1$) and $G_2$ (current sensitivity $S_2 = 2S_1$) but $G_2$ has double the number of turns. If the resistances are equal, what is the ratio of voltage sensitivities?
Answer: 1:2

$S_V = S_I / R_g$. Since $R_g$ are equal: $S_{V2}/S_{V1} = S_{I2}/S_{I1} = 2S_1/S_1 = 2$. Ratio $S_{V1}:S_{V2} = 1:2$.

NEET 2020 A cyclotron is used to accelerate protons. The frequency of the oscillating electric field used is $f = 10\,\text{MHz}$. Find the magnetic field required (mass of proton $= 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg).
Answer: ≈ 0.66 T

Cyclotron frequency: $f = \frac{qB}{2\pi m}$ → $B = \frac{2\pi m f}{q}$

$B = \frac{2\pi \times 1.67\times10^{-27} \times 10^7}{1.6\times10^{-19}} = \frac{2\pi \times 1.67\times10^{-20}}{1.6\times10^{-19}} \approx 0.66\,\text{T}$

Remember: Cyclotron CANNOT accelerate electrons (they become relativistic too quickly) and CANNOT increase frequency beyond a certain limit (relativistic mass increase).

Bar Magnet Fields Most Asked

Axial (end-on) position: $$B_{axial} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{2Md}{(d^2-l^2)^2}$$
For short magnet ($d \gg l$): $$B_{axial} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{2M}{d^3}$$

Equatorial (broad-on) position: $$B_{eq} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{M}{(d^2+l^2)^{3/2}}$$
For short magnet: $$B_{eq} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{M}{d^3}$$

Golden ratio: $B_{axial} = 2 B_{eq}$ for a short dipole at same distance. Direction: axial field is along $\vec{M}$; equatorial field is opposite to $\vec{M}$.

Dimension check: $[M] = [\text{A·m}^2]$ or $[\text{J/T}]$. $[\mu_0 M / d^3]$ = Tesla. ✓

Direction Trap — NEET 2022

The magnetic field at the equatorial point of a bar magnet is directed from N to S (i.e., opposite to the magnetic moment $\vec{M}$). The axial field is in the same direction as $\vec{M}$ (S to N outside the magnet, N to S inside). Don't confuse this with electric dipole where equatorial $\vec{E}$ is also opposite to $\vec{p}$.

NEET 2022 The period of oscillation of a freely suspended bar magnet is 4 s. If it is cut into two equal halves, the time period of each half when freely suspended is:
Answer: 4 s

$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I}{MB_H}}$. When cut into two equal halves (along length):

New magnetic moment $M' = M/2$ (half the poles). New moment of inertia $I' = I/2$ (half mass, same geometry factor).

$T' = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I/2}{(M/2)B_H}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{I}{MB_H}} = T = 4\,\text{s}$

If cut perpendicular to length: $M' = M/2$, $I' = I/8$, so $T' = T/2 = 2$ s. NEET asks both versions.

Curie's Law and Curie Temperature

Curie's Law (Paramagnetics): $$\chi = \frac{C}{T}$$
where $C$ is the Curie constant, $T$ is absolute temperature.

Above the Curie Temperature $T_C$, a ferromagnetic material becomes paramagnetic. For iron, $T_C \approx 770°C$. NEET once asked: "What is the behaviour of a ferromagnetic material above Curie temperature?" → Paramagnetic.

Earth's Magnetic Field

Three Elements of Earth's B
  • Declination (α): angle between geographic North and magnetic North
  • Dip/Inclination (δ): angle of B with horizontal
  • Horizontal component: $B_H = B\cos\delta$
  • Vertical component: $B_V = B\sin\delta$
  • $\tan\delta = B_V/B_H$
NEET Fast Facts
  • At equator: $\delta = 0°$, $B_V = 0$
  • At poles: $\delta = 90°$, $B_H = 0$
  • At dip of 45°: $B_V = B_H$
  • Magnetic south pole ≈ geographic north
  • Apparent dip: observed when compass is tilted
NEET 2021 At a place, the horizontal component of Earth's magnetic field is $B$ and angle of dip is 60°. The value of vertical component of Earth's field and the total intensity of Earth's field are respectively:
Answer: B√3 and 2B

$B_V = B_H \tan\delta = B\tan 60° = B\sqrt{3}$

Total intensity: $B_{total} = B_H/\cos\delta = B/\cos 60° = B/(1/2) = 2B$

Or: $B_{total} = \sqrt{B_H^2+B_V^2} = \sqrt{B^2+3B^2} = 2B$. ✓

Faraday's & Lenz's Laws Core — 2 Qs/yr

Faraday's First Law

Whenever the magnetic flux linked with a closed circuit changes, an EMF is induced in the circuit. The EMF lasts only as long as the flux is changing.

Faraday's Second Law (Magnitude)

$$|\varepsilon| = N\left|\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}\right|$$

For $N$ turns: $\varepsilon = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$ (negative sign = Lenz's law).

Lenz's Law (Direction)

The direction of induced current is such that it opposes the cause producing it. This is a consequence of conservation of energy — you must do work to create the induced current.

Flux Calculation — NEET's Setup

$$\Phi_B = \vec{B}\cdot\vec{A} = BA\cos\theta$$
Units: Weber (Wb) = Volt·second = T·m²

Three ways flux changes in NEET:

(1) $B$ changes (current in nearby coil changes) → $\varepsilon = -NA\frac{dB}{dt}$

(2) Area changes (coil compressed/expanded) → $\varepsilon = -NB\frac{dA}{dt}$

(3) Angle changes (coil rotates) → $\varepsilon = NBA\omega\sin\omega t$ (AC Generator)

Lenz's Law — Direction Problems (NEET Loves These)

The 3-step method that solves every Lenz's law direction problem:

Step 1: Identify the existing magnetic field direction in the coil's plane.

Step 2: Determine if the flux is increasing or decreasing.

Step 3: The induced current creates a B field that opposes the change: if flux increasing → induced B opposes → induced current flows to create opposite B; if flux decreasing → induced B supports → induced current flows to create same-direction B.

Classic NEET scenario: A bar magnet is moved towards a coil, North pole facing coil. As it approaches, flux through coil increases. By Lenz's law, induced current creates a magnetic field opposing this increase, i.e., the coil face nearest the magnet acts as a North pole (repels the magnet). To determine current direction: use right-hand rule — if North pole faces you from the coil, current is anticlockwise when viewed from that face.

NEET 2024 A coil of area $A = 0.1\,\text{m}^2$ has $N = 200$ turns. The magnetic flux through the coil changes from 0 to $\Phi_{max} = 5\times10^{-4}$ Wb in 0.2 s uniformly. The average induced EMF in the coil is:
Answer: 0.5 V (50 mV)

$\varepsilon = N\frac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t} = 200 \times \frac{5\times10^{-4}}{0.2} = 200 \times 2.5\times10^{-3} = 0.5\,\text{V}$

Note: The area $A$ was given but is NOT needed here because $\Delta\Phi$ is directly given. This is a deliberate distractor.

NEET 2023 A circular loop is placed in a time-varying uniform magnetic field $B = B_0\cos\omega t$ perpendicular to the loop. The induced EMF in the loop of area $A$ is:
Answer: $\varepsilon = AB_0\omega\sin\omega t$

$\Phi = BA = B_0 A\cos\omega t$

$\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt} = -B_0 A \cdot (-\omega\sin\omega t) = AB_0\omega\sin\omega t$

Maximum EMF: $\varepsilon_{max} = AB_0\omega$ at $t = \pi/2\omega$ (when $B = 0$, not when $B$ is maximum!)

NEET 2019 A metallic ring is placed in a region where a time-varying magnetic field exists. If the ring is in the plane perpendicular to $\vec{B}$, the ring:
Answer: gets heated (eddy currents flow)

Even without an external circuit, the induced EMF drives eddy currents within the conducting ring. These eddy currents cause $I^2R$ heating. The ring does NOT "repel" the field — it opposes the change in flux but cannot completely cancel it.

Applications of eddy currents: Induction furnace, electromagnetic braking (trains), dead-beat galvanometer, speedometers, energy meters. Minimized by: laminated cores (slotted/thin sheets with insulation between them).

Motional EMF Numerical Guaranteed

When a conductor moves through a magnetic field, free electrons experience Lorentz force, creating a potential difference — motional EMF.

$$\varepsilon = \int (\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \cdot d\vec{l}$$
For a straight rod of length $l$ moving with velocity $v$ perpendicular to $B$: $$\boxed{\varepsilon = Blv}$$
When to use ε = Blv

Rod must be perpendicular to both $v$ and $B$ for the simple formula. If rod is at angle $\theta$ to $v$: $\varepsilon = Blv\sin\theta$. If $B$ is at angle $\phi$ to the plane: $\varepsilon = Blv\cos\phi$. Most NEET problems use the simple perpendicular case.

Power and Force in Motional EMF

If the rod slides on rails of resistance $R$ in a magnetic field $B$:

Induced current: $I = \frac{Blv}{R}$
Retarding force on rod: $F = BIl = \frac{B^2l^2v}{R}$
Power dissipated: $P = F \cdot v = \frac{B^2l^2v^2}{R}$
Energy Conservation Check

Power dissipated as heat in resistor = Power input by external agent keeping rod moving at constant $v$: $P_{external} = F \cdot v = B^2l^2v^2/R$. If no external force, rod decelerates exponentially.

Rotating Rod in Magnetic Field

A rod of length $l$ rotating about one end in a uniform field $B$ (perpendicular to plane of rotation):

$$\varepsilon = \frac{1}{2}Bl^2\omega = \frac{1}{2}Bl^2 \cdot \frac{2\pi}{T} = \pi Bl^2 f$$

Derivation shortcut: Think of the rod as sweeping area $dA = \frac{1}{2}l^2 d\theta$ in time $dt$. Rate of area swept = $\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{1}{2}l^2\omega$. So $\varepsilon = B\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{1}{2}Bl^2\omega$. This appears in NEET as "find EMF of a spoke of a wheel".

NEET 2022 A metallic rod of length $l = 1\,\text{m}$ is rotated about one end in a plane perpendicular to a magnetic field $B = 0.4\,\text{T}$. If it makes 100 rev/min, what is the EMF generated?
Answer: ≈ 2.09 V

$\omega = 2\pi \times \frac{100}{60} = \frac{10\pi}{3}\,\text{rad/s}$

$\varepsilon = \frac{1}{2}Bl^2\omega = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.4 \times 1 \times \frac{10\pi}{3} = \frac{2\pi}{3} \approx 2.09\,\text{V}$

NEET 2020 A rectangular coil of area $A$ and number of turns $N$ rotates with angular velocity $\omega$ in a uniform magnetic field $B$. The maximum EMF and maximum rate of change of flux are:
Answer: NBA ω and NBA ω

$\Phi = NBA\cos\omega t$

$\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt} = NBA\omega\sin\omega t$

$\varepsilon_{max} = NBA\omega$

Max rate of change of flux $= \left|\frac{d\Phi}{dt}\right|_{max} = NBA\omega$ (same value, since $\varepsilon = \left|\frac{d\Phi}{dt}\right|$ by Faraday's law for single turn, or $N$ times for $N$ turns)

Note: Max flux = $NBA$ occurs at $t=0$; max EMF occurs at $t = \pi/(2\omega)$ (90° phase difference).

Self & Mutual Inductance Formula Heavy

Self Inductance

Self Inductance (L)

When current in a coil changes, the changing magnetic flux through itself induces an EMF opposing the change:

$$\varepsilon = -L\frac{dI}{dt}$$

$L$ is self-inductance in Henry (H). It depends only on geometry.

Self Inductance of a Solenoid

$$L = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{l} = \mu_0 n^2 A l = \mu_0 n^2 V$$
where $n = N/l$ = turns per unit length, $V = Al$ = volume

Proportionalities for NEET: $L \propto N^2$, $L \propto A$, $L \propto l^{-1}$ for fixed $N$. If $N$ doubles and length doubles: $n$ unchanged, but $A$ same → $L \propto N^2/l$ doubles. Always express $L$ in terms of what changes.

Energy Stored in an Inductor

$$U = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$$
Energy density: $u = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}$ (J/m³)
Analogy with capacitor: $U_C = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 \longleftrightarrow U_L = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$

Mutual Inductance

Mutual Inductance (M)

EMF induced in coil 2 due to changing current in coil 1:

$$\varepsilon_2 = -M\frac{dI_1}{dt}$$

$M$ is mutual inductance. It is a property of the pair of coils. $M_{12} = M_{21}$.

Mutual Inductance of Two Coaxial Solenoids

$$M = \frac{\mu_0 N_1 N_2 A}{l} = \mu_0 n_1 N_2 A$$
Coupling coefficient: $k = \frac{M}{\sqrt{L_1 L_2}}$ where $0 \leq k \leq 1$
Neumann Formula (Conceptual)

$M$ depends on: geometry (shape, size, relative position), number of turns, medium (μ). $M$ does NOT depend on the current flowing. If coils are perpendicular, $M = 0$ (no flux linkage).

NEET 2024 The self-inductance of a coil is $L$. If the current through it changes from $I_0$ to $0$ in time $t_0$, the average induced EMF is:
Answer: LI₀/t₀

$|\varepsilon_{avg}| = L\left|\frac{\Delta I}{\Delta t}\right| = L \cdot \frac{I_0 - 0}{t_0} = \frac{LI_0}{t_0}$

Sign: Since current decreases, induced EMF tries to maintain current in the same direction (Lenz's law).

NEET 2021 Two coils are placed coaxially. Coil 1 has $N_1 = 500$ turns, area $A = 1\,\text{cm}^2$, length $l = 25\,\text{cm}$. Coil 2 has $N_2 = 200$ turns wound on coil 1. Find mutual inductance $M$.
Answer: ≈ 0.502 mH

AC Fundamentals — Peak, RMS, and Average Values

Alternating current: $i = I_0\sin(\omega t + \phi)$, where $I_0$ is peak current, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is angular frequency.

$$I_{rms} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707\, I_0$$ $$V_{rms} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707\, V_0$$ $$I_{avg} = \frac{2I_0}{\pi} \approx 0.637\, I_0 \quad \text{(over half cycle)}$$
Form factor $= \frac{I_{rms}}{I_{avg}} = \frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{2}} \approx 1.11$
RMS vs Average — NEET Tests Both

Household voltage "220 V" is the RMS value. Peak voltage $= 220\sqrt{2} \approx 311$ V. Average over full cycle = 0 (for sine wave). Average over half cycle = $2V_0/\pi$. NEET 2016: "If V = 220 V is RMS, peak value is?" → $220\sqrt{2}$ V.

Phase Relations — The CIVIL Mnemonic

CIVIL — The Single Most Useful AC Memory Tool

Capacitor: I leads V (current leads voltage by 90°)

Inductor: V leads I (voltage leads current by 90°; or current LAGS voltage)

Pure R: no phase difference. In IL, voltage leads.

Series LCR Circuit Highest Yield Topic

A series LCR circuit is the single most tested circuit in NEET AC chapter. You must know impedance, phase angle, current, and power for any given $L$, $C$, $R$, $f$.

Impedance: $$Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} = \sqrt{R^2 + \left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right)^2}$$
Phase angle: $$\tan\phi = \frac{X_L - X_C}{R}$$
Current: $$I_{rms} = \frac{V_{rms}}{Z}$$

🎯 Resonance — NEET's Most Repeated AC Scenario

Resonance Condition

At resonance: $X_L = X_C$ → $\omega L = \frac{1}{\omega C}$ → $Z = R$ (minimum), $I = V/R$ (maximum), $\phi = 0$ (unity power factor)

$$\omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}$$

Resonance quick-check: At resonance: $V_R = V_{source}$ (all voltage across R), $V_L = V_C$ (may exceed source voltage!), $I$ is maximum. If $V_L = V_C$ stated → it IS resonance regardless of values.

NEET Trap — Voltage at Resonance

At resonance, $V_L$ and $V_C$ can individually be much larger than the source voltage, but they cancel each other. This is NOT a violation of energy conservation. NEET 2020 asked: "At resonance, what is $V_L$?" → $V_L = I \cdot X_L = (V/R) \cdot \omega_0 L$, which can be $\gg V$.

Q Factor (Quality Factor)

$$Q = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_0 CR} = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}}$$
Bandwidth: $\Delta\omega = R/L$    $Q = \omega_0/\Delta\omega$
Higher $Q$ → sharper resonance peak → more selective circuit
NEET 2025 In a series LCR circuit, $L = 1\,\text{H}$, $C = 10\,\mu\text{F}$, $R = 100\,\Omega$, applied voltage $V = 200\sin(100t)$. The impedance of the circuit is:
Answer: 100√2 Ω

$\omega = 100\,\text{rad/s}$

$X_L = \omega L = 100 \times 1 = 100\,\Omega$

$X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C} = \frac{1}{100 \times 10^{-5}} = \frac{1}{10^{-3}} = 1000\,\Omega$

$Z = \sqrt{100^2 + (100-1000)^2} = \sqrt{10^4 + 8.1\times10^5} = \sqrt{10^4(1+81)} = 100\sqrt{82} \approx 906\,\Omega$

Wait — checking: $Z = \sqrt{100^2 + 900^2} = \sqrt{10000+810000} = \sqrt{820000} = 100\sqrt{82}$. Check if $\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC} = 1/\sqrt{10^{-5}} = 316$ rad/s. At $\omega=100$, not resonance.

Alternative version (at resonance): If $\omega = 1/\sqrt{LC}$: $Z = R = 100\,\Omega$, $I = V/R = 200/(100\sqrt{2}) = \sqrt{2}$ A. Always check if resonance first!

NEET 2023 In a series LCR circuit, the frequency is increased beyond resonance. The circuit becomes:
Answer: Inductive (V leads I, or current lags)

At resonance: $X_L = X_C$. Above resonance: $X_L > X_C$ (since $X_L = \omega L$ increases, $X_C = 1/\omega C$ decreases). So net reactance is inductive → $\tan\phi > 0$ → $V$ leads $I$ → circuit is inductive. Below resonance → capacitive.

Memory: "As frequency rises, the inductor takes over." $X_L \propto \omega$ (rises), $X_C \propto 1/\omega$ (falls).

NEET 2022 An LCR circuit has $L = 1\,\text{mH}$, $C = 0.1\,\mu\text{F}$, $R = 10\,\Omega$. What is the Q-factor of this circuit?
Answer: 10

$Q = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} = \frac{1}{10}\sqrt{\frac{10^{-3}}{10^{-7}}} = \frac{1}{10}\sqrt{10^4} = \frac{100}{10} = 10$

Power in AC Circuits High NEET Priority

$$P_{avg} = V_{rms} I_{rms} \cos\phi$$
Power factor: $\cos\phi = \frac{R}{Z}$
Apparent power: $S = V_{rms} I_{rms}$ (VA)
Reactive power: $Q = V_{rms} I_{rms}\sin\phi$ (VAR)
Wattless Current

The component of current that is 90° out of phase with voltage does zero average work: $I_{wattless} = I_{rms}\sin\phi$. The active (power-consuming) component: $I_{active} = I_{rms}\cos\phi$.

For pure L or pure C: $\phi = 90°$, $\cos\phi = 0$, $P_{avg} = 0$. No energy consumed — only stored and returned.

Three critical cases:

Pure R: $\cos\phi = 1$, $P = I^2R$ (max power). Pure L or C: $\cos\phi = 0$, $P = 0$. LCR at resonance: $\cos\phi = 1$, $P = I_{max}^2 R/2 = V^2/2R$ (where $V$ is peak).

NEET 2024 In an AC circuit, $V = 200\sin(100t)$ and $I = 4\sin(100t + \pi/3)$. The average power consumed is:
Answer: 200 W

$V_0 = 200$ V, $I_0 = 4$ A, phase of $I$ leads $V$ by $\pi/3 = 60°$

$P = \frac{V_0 I_0}{2}\cos\phi = \frac{200 \times 4}{2} \times \cos 60° = 400 \times 0.5 = 200$ W

Or: $P = V_{rms} I_{rms} \cos\phi = \frac{200}{\sqrt{2}} \times \frac{4}{\sqrt{2}} \times \frac{1}{2} = 100 \times 2 \times \frac{1}{2} \cdot \sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{2} = 200$ W ✓

NEET 2021 A coil of resistance $10\,\Omega$ and self-inductance $5\,\text{H}$ is connected to an AC source of $V_{rms} = 200$ V, $f = 50$ Hz. The power factor of the circuit is:
Answer: ≈ 0.060 (very low)

$X_L = 2\pi \times 50 \times 5 = 500\pi \approx 1571\,\Omega$

$Z = \sqrt{10^2 + 1571^2} \approx 1571\,\Omega$

$\cos\phi = R/Z = 10/1571 \approx 0.0064$. Essentially wattless — pure inductor behaviour.

Conceptual: When $X_L \gg R$, power factor $\approx 0$. All power is reactive.

Transformer Formula + Concept

Transformer Principle

Based on mutual induction. An AC voltage in the primary coil induces an AC voltage in the secondary coil. Works only with AC — NOT DC (DC gives no changing flux).

$$\frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p} = \frac{I_p}{I_s} \quad \text{(ideal transformer)}$$
Step-up: $N_s > N_p$, $V_s > V_p$, $I_s < I_p$
Step-down: $N_s < N_p$, $V_s < V_p$, $I_s > I_p$
Ideal vs Real Transformer

Ideal: efficiency = 100%, $P_{input} = P_{output}$. Real transformer losses: (1) Copper losses ($I^2R$ in coils) — reduced by thick wire. (2) Iron losses — eddy current losses (reduced by laminated core) + hysteresis losses (reduced by soft iron). (3) Flux leakage. NEET asks: "Which core material for transformer?" → Soft iron (low hysteresis loss, high permeability).

NEET 2023 A transformer has 200 turns in primary and 400 turns in secondary. If input power is 10 kW and primary voltage is 200 V, find the secondary voltage and secondary current (assume ideal).
Answer: V_s = 400 V, I_s = 25 A

$V_s = V_p \times (N_s/N_p) = 200 \times (400/200) = 400$ V

$I_p = P/V_p = 10000/200 = 50$ A

$I_s = I_p \times (N_p/N_s) = 50 \times (200/400) = 25$ A

Check: $P_{out} = V_s I_s = 400 \times 25 = 10000$ W = $P_{in}$ ✓

NEET 2019 A transformer is used to step up 200 V AC to 2000 V to transmit power over long distance through a cable of resistance $R = 10\,\Omega$. If the primary current is 10 A, what is the power loss in transmission?
Answer: 10 W

Step-up ratio = 10. Secondary current $I_s = I_p/10 = 10/10 = 1$ A.

Power loss = $I_s^2 R = 1^2 \times 10 = 10$ W

Key idea: Transmission at high voltage means low current in the cable → dramatically reduced $I^2R$ losses. This is why power is transmitted at 132 kV or 220 kV in India.

LC Oscillations

In an LC circuit, energy oscillates between electric field (in C) and magnetic field (in L) like a pendulum:

$$f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}$$
Total energy: $U = \frac{1}{2}LI_0^2 = \frac{Q_0^2}{2C}$ (conserved, no $R$)
At any time: $U = \frac{1}{2}Li^2 + \frac{q^2}{2C}$ = constant

Analogy with SHM: $q = Q_0\cos\omega t$, $i = -Q_0\omega\sin\omega t$. Max current $I_0 = Q_0\omega = Q_0/\sqrt{LC}$. When $q = 0$: all energy in $L$ (max current). When $i = 0$: all energy in $C$ (max charge). Exactly like KE↔PE in spring.

NEET 2025 The electric field in a plane EM wave is $E_z = 60\cos(5x + 1.5\times10^9 t)$ V/m. The corresponding magnetic field expression is:

Fundamental Properties
  • Transverse waves: $\vec{E} \perp \vec{B} \perp \vec{v}$
  • Speed in vacuum: $c = 3\times10^8$ m/s
  • $c = E_0/B_0 = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}$
  • Carry energy and momentum
  • No medium needed (travel in vacuum)
  • NOT deflected by E or B fields
Speed in Medium
  • $v = c/n$ where $n$ = refractive index
  • $n = \sqrt{\mu_r \varepsilon_r}$ (for non-magnetic: $n = \sqrt{\varepsilon_r} = \sqrt{K}$)
  • $v = 1/\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon}$
  • Frequency unchanged in medium
  • Wavelength changes: $\lambda_m = \lambda_0/n$
Energy and Intensity
  • Energy density: $u = \varepsilon_0 E^2 = B^2/\mu_0$
  • $u_E = u_B = u/2$ (equal contributions)
  • Intensity: $I = uc = \varepsilon_0 E_0^2 c/2$
  • Radiation pressure: $P = I/c$ (absorbing)

Maxwell's Displacement Current

Maxwell predicted that a changing electric field in a capacitor gap produces a magnetic field — even without actual current flow. He called this the displacement current:

$$I_d = \varepsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$$
Modified Ampere's law (Maxwell-Ampere law): $$\oint \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0(I_c + I_d) = \mu_0 I_c + \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$$
Why Displacement Current Matters — NEET Context

Between capacitor plates during charging: no real current, but $E$ is changing. Displacement current $I_d$ = conduction current $I_c$ outside the plates. This ensures continuity of current and explains how EM waves propagate through vacuum (no charges needed). NEET asks: "Between capacitor plates, what kind of current flows?" → displacement current.

NEET 2020 In an EM wave, the electric and magnetic energy densities are compared. The correct statement is:
Answer: Electric energy density = Magnetic energy density (at every point)

$u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$ and $u_B = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}$. Since $E = cB$ and $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}$:

$u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 c^2 B^2 = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 \cdot \frac{1}{\mu_0\varepsilon_0} \cdot B^2 = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0} = u_B$

They are equal at every point in space and time. Total energy density = $u_E + u_B = \varepsilon_0 E^2 = B^2/\mu_0$.

Ohm's Law, Resistance & Resistivity Foundation

Conductivity: $\sigma = 1/\rho = ne^2\tau/m$
where $v_d$ = drift velocity, $\tau$ = relaxation time

Drift Velocity — The Hidden Formula

$$v_d = \frac{eE\tau}{m} = \frac{I}{neA}$$
where $n$ = number density of electrons, $e$ = electron charge, $A$ = cross-section area

From $v_d = I/neA$: If same wire carries double current, $v_d$ doubles. If wire is stretched to double length (same volume → area halves), $v_d$ quadruples for same current (area halved). If two wires of same material, same length but area ratio 1:2 carry same current: $v_d$ ratio is 2:1.

Resistor Combinations

Series Connection
  • $R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + ...$
  • Same current through all
  • Voltage divides: $V_i \propto R_i$
  • If $n$ equal resistors: $R_{eq} = nR$
  • Total power: $P = I^2 R_{eq}$
Parallel Connection
  • $1/R_{eq} = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + ...$
  • Same voltage across all
  • Current divides: $I_i \propto 1/R_i$
  • $n$ equal: $R_{eq} = R/n$
  • $R_{eq} < R_{min}$ always
Two in Parallel (Shortcut)
  • $R_{eq} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$
  • Current divider: $I_1 = I\frac{R_2}{R_1+R_2}$
  • $R_{eq} = R/2$ if $R_1 = R_2 = R$
  • $R_{eq} = R_1$ if $R_2 \gg R_1$
NEET 2024 A wire of length $l$ and resistance $100\,\Omega$ is divided into 10 equal parts. First 5 parts are connected in series, next 5 in parallel. The two groups are connected in series. Total resistance is:
Answer: 52 Ω

Each part has $R = 100/10 = 10\,\Omega$.

First group (5 in series): $R_1 = 5 \times 10 = 50\,\Omega$

Second group (5 in parallel): $R_2 = 10/5 = 2\,\Omega$

Total: $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 = 50 + 2 = 52\,\Omega$

NEET 2024 The terminal voltage of a battery with emf $\varepsilon = 10$ V and internal resistance $r = 1\,\Omega$, connected through external resistance $R = 4\,\Omega$, is:

Kirchhoff's Laws Circuit Analysis Foundation

KCL — Kirchhoff's Current Law (Junction Rule)

$$\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \sum I = 0 \text{ at junction}$$

Conservation of charge. At any node, charge doesn't accumulate.

KVL — Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (Loop Rule)

$$\sum \Delta V = 0 \text{ around any closed loop}$$

Conservation of energy. Sum of EMFs = sum of IR drops in any closed loop.

Solving KVL problems — 4-step method:

Step 1: Assign current directions (guessing is fine — negative answer means opposite direction).

Step 2: Apply KCL at junctions to reduce unknowns.

Step 3: Traverse loops (any direction) — going through resistor in direction of current = $-IR$ drop; against current = $+IR$ drop. Going through battery from − to + = $+\varepsilon$; from + to − = $-\varepsilon$.

Step 4: Solve simultaneous equations.

Wheatstone Bridge

Balanced Wheatstone Bridge

When galvanometer reads zero (balanced condition):

$$\frac{P}{Q} = \frac{R}{S} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad PS = QR$$

At balance, no current through galvanometer regardless of its resistance or source voltage.

Unbalanced bridge: Must use Kirchhoff's laws. For balanced bridge: Simply use the ratio $P/Q = R/S$. Never apply this to unbalanced bridges.

Meter bridge (slide-wire bridge): $R/X = l/(100-l)$ where $l$ is balance length. NEET tests: if balance length shifts from $l$ to $100-l$, the unknown and known resistances swap.

NEET 2025 The current passing through the battery in the given circuit is 2 A (as per NEET 2025 data). If the circuit has $\varepsilon = 12$ V and multiple resistors, find the equivalent resistance.
Pattern: Use $R_{eq} = \varepsilon/I$

For any circuit: total current from battery = $I = \varepsilon / R_{eq}$ (assuming ideal battery). $R_{eq} = \varepsilon/I = 12/2 = 6\,\Omega$.

Strategy for complex resistor networks: (1) Identify series/parallel. (2) If bridge-type, check balance. (3) If symmetric, use symmetry to short/break wires. (4) If none work, use Kirchhoff.

Potentiometer 1–2 Qs Always

The potentiometer is a null method instrument — it measures EMF without drawing current from the source being measured. This is why it's more accurate than a voltmeter.

$$\text{Potential gradient: } k = \frac{V_{AB}}{L} = \frac{I \rho}{A} \quad [\text{V/m}]$$
At balance: $\varepsilon = k \cdot l$ where $l$ = balance length

The EMF of driver cell must be greater than the EMF being measured. If not, balance point will not be found (it would be beyond the wire). NEET asks: "Why does balance point shift when driver cell EMF is reduced?" → $k$ decreases, so balance length increases (may go beyond wire).

NEET 2023 A potentiometer wire of length $10\,\text{m}$ has a resistance of $10\,\Omega$. It is connected in series with a battery of emf $2\,\text{V}$ and resistance $990\,\Omega$. The potential gradient is:
Answer: 2×10⁻³ V/m = 2 mV/m

Total resistance = $10 + 990 = 1000\,\Omega$

Current $I = 2/1000 = 2\times10^{-3}$ A

Voltage across wire $= I \times R_{wire} = 2\times10^{-3} \times 10 = 0.02$ V

Potential gradient $k = V_{wire}/L = 0.02/10 = 2\times10^{-3}$ V/m

NEET 2022 In a potentiometer experiment, a cell of emf $\varepsilon_1$ gives balance at 60 cm. Another cell with emf $\varepsilon_2 = 1.2$ V gives balance at 48 cm. Find $\varepsilon_1$.

$\frac{\varepsilon_1}{\varepsilon_2} = \frac{l_1}{l_2} = \frac{60}{48} = \frac{5}{4}$

$\varepsilon_1 = \frac{5}{4} \times 1.2 = 1.5\,\text{V}$

NEET 2021 In a potentiometer, the balance point for a cell is at 75 cm without external resistance. With a resistance $R = 5\,\Omega$ connected across the cell, balance is at 50 cm. The internal resistance of the cell is:

Cells in Series & Parallel Formula-Based

n Cells in Series
  • $\varepsilon_{eq} = n\varepsilon$
  • $r_{eq} = nr$
  • $I = \frac{n\varepsilon}{R + nr}$
  • Efficient when $R \gg r$
  • Use: high-voltage, low-current
n Cells in Parallel
  • $\varepsilon_{eq} = \varepsilon$ (same as one cell)
  • $r_{eq} = r/n$
  • $I = \frac{\varepsilon}{R + r/n}$
  • Efficient when $R \ll r$
  • Use: high-current, low-voltage
Mixed Grid (m rows of n cells)
  • Total EMF = $n\varepsilon$
  • $r_{eq} = nr/m$
  • Max current when $R = nr/m$
  • $I_{max} = \frac{mn\varepsilon}{2nr} = \frac{m\varepsilon}{2r}$
  • Optimize: $m/n = r/R$

When are cells in series preferable? When external resistance $R$ is much greater than internal resistance $r$ (high-R circuit). When R ≪ r: parallel preferred. When R = r (for single cell): mixed arrangement optimal. NEET 2016 and 2020 both tested maximum power transfer condition: $R = r_{eq}$ for max power in external resistance.

NEET 2020 A battery of 20 cells (each emf $= 1.5$ V, internal resistance $= 1\,\Omega$) is connected to an external resistance of $25\,\Omega$. What is the arrangement for maximum current?
Answer: All in series → I = 1.2 A

For max current through $R = 25\,\Omega$: compare series vs parallel.

Series (20 cells): $I = \frac{20\times1.5}{25+20\times1} = \frac{30}{45} = 0.667$ A

Parallel (20 cells): $I = \frac{1.5}{25+1/20} = \frac{1.5}{25.05} \approx 0.060$ A

Since $R = 25\,\Omega \gg r = 1\,\Omega$: Series is better → $I = 0.667$ A is higher.
