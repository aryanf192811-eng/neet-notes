1. Basis of Classification (NCERT Truths & Traps)

Levels of Organisation:

  • Cellular: Porifera (cells arranged as loose aggregates).
  • Tissue: Coelenterata (Cnidaria) & Ctenophora.
  • Organ: Platyhelminthes (first to have organs) and other higher phyla.
  • Organ System: Aschelminthes to Chordates. (Annelids, Arthropods, Molluscs, Echinoderms).
Symmetry & Coelom Deep Dive
Symmetry:

Asymmetrical: Porifera (mostly).

Radial: Any plane passing through the central axis divides the organism into identical halves. Examples: Coelenterates, Ctenophores, Echinoderms (adults only).

Bilateral: Body can be divided into identical left and right halves in only one plane. Examples: Annelids, Arthropods, etc. (Platyhelminthes onwards).

Coelom:

Acoelomate: No body cavity (solid body). Platyhelminthes.

Pseudocoelomate: Body cavity is not lined by mesoderm; mesoderm is present as scattered pouches between ectoderm and endoderm. Ex: Aschelminthes.

Eucoelomate (True Coelom): Body cavity lined by mesoderm. Ex: Annelida, Mollusca, Arthropoda, Echinodermata, Hemichordata, Chordata.

🚨 NEET Pyq Trap: Notochord Origin

Notochord is a mesodermally derived rod-like structure formed on the dorsal side during embryonic development in some animals. Animals without it = Non-chordates (Porifera to Echinoderms). Hemichordates have a stomochord (not a true notochord).

2. Lower Non-Chordates (Porifera to Aschelminthes)

Phylum 1: Porifera (Sponges)

Generally marine, asymmetrical. Cellular level. Water transport or canal system: Water enters through minute pores (ostia) in the body wall into a central cavity, spongocoel, from where it goes out through the osculum. This pathway aids in food gathering, respiratory exchange, and removal of waste.

  • Choanocytes (collar cells): Line the spongocoel and the canals. Unique to sponges.
  • Digestion: Intracellular.
  • Skeleton: Made up of spicules or spongin fibers.
  • Reproduction: Asexual (fragmentation) & Sexual. Hermaphrodite. Internal fertilization, indirect development (has a larval stage morphologically distinct from the adult).
  • Examples: Sycon (Scypha), Spongilla (Freshwater sponge), Euspongia (Bath sponge).

Phylum 2: Coelenterata (Cnidaria)

Aquatic, mostly marine, sessile or free-swimming, radially symmetrical. Name derived from cnidoblasts or cnidocytes (contain stinging capsules or nematocysts) present on tentacles and body, used for anchorage, defense, and capture of prey.

  • Forms: Polyp (sessile, cylindrical e.g., Hydra, Adamsia) and Medusa (umbrella-shaped, free-swimming e.g., Aurelia/jellyfish).
  • Metagenesis (Alternation of generation): Found in Obelia. Polyps produce medusae asexually; medusae form polyps sexually.
  • Skeleton: Corals have a skeleton composed of calcium carbonate.
  • Examples: Physalia (Portuguese man-of-war), Adamsia (Sea anemone), Pennatula (Sea-pen), Gorgonia (Sea-fan), Meandrina (Brain coral).

Phylum 3: Ctenophora (Sea Walnuts / Comb Jellies)

Exclusively marine, radially symmetrical, diploblastic, tissue level. Body bears eight external rows of ciliated comb plates, which help in locomotion.

  • Bioluminescence: The property of a living organism to emit light is well-marked.
  • Reproduction: Sexes are not separate. Sexual reproduction only. External fertilization with indirect development.
  • Examples: Pleurobrachia and Ctenoplana.

Phylum 4: Platyhelminthes (Flatworms)

Dorso-ventrally flattened body. Mostly endoparasites in animals including humans. Bilaterally symmetrical, triploblastic, acoelomate with organ level. Hooks and suckers are present in parasitic forms. Some absorb nutrients directly through body surface.

  • Flame Cells: Specialized cells that help in osmoregulation and excretion.
  • Reproduction: Sexes are not separate. Internal fertilization. Many larval stages. Planaria possesses high regeneration capacity.
  • Examples: Taenia (Tapeworm), Fasciola (Liver fluke).

Phylum 5: Aschelminthes (Roundworms)

Circular in cross-section. Free-living, aquatic/terrestrial, or parasitic in plants/animals. Organ-system level, bilateral symmetry, triploblastic, pseudocoelomate.

  • Alimentary Canal: Complete with a well-developed muscular pharynx.
  • Excretion: An excretory tube removes body wastes from the body cavity through the excretory pore.
  • Reproduction: Dioecious (sexes are separate). Females are often longer than males. Internal fertilization, direct or indirect development.
  • Examples: Ascaris (Roundworm), Wuchereria (Filaria worm), Ancylostoma (Hookworm).
NEET 2020 Identify the correct statement with reference to Ascaris:

Correct Statement: Presence of neither true coelom nor metamerism. (It is pseudocoelomate and unsegmented!)

3. Higher Non-Chordates (Annelida to Hemichordata)

Phylum 6: Annelida

Aquatic or terrestrial, free-living or parasitic. Organ-system level. Bilateral, triploblastic, metamerically segmented, and coelomate. Body surface is distinctly marked out into segments or metameres.

  • Locomotion: Longitudinal & circular muscles. Aquatic annelids like Nereis possess lateral appendages, parapodia, which help in swimming.
  • Circulation: A closed circulatory system is present.
  • Excretion/Osmoregulation: Nephridia (sing. nephridium).
  • Nervous System: Paired ganglia connected by lateral nerves to a double ventral nerve cord.
  • Reproduction: Nereis is dioecious. Earthworms & Leeches are monoecious.
  • Examples: Nereis, Pheretima (Earthworm), Hirudinaria (Blood sucking leech).

Phylum 7: Arthropoda (Largest Phylum)

Over two-thirds of all named species on earth. Organ-system level, bilateral, triploblastic, segmented, coelomate.

  • Body/Skeleton: Covered by chitinous exoskeleton. Body consists of head, thorax, and abdomen. They have jointed appendages (arthro = joint, poda = appendages).
  • Respiration: Gills, book gills, book lungs or tracheal system.
  • Circulation & Excretion: Open circulatory system. Excretion via Malpighian tubules.
  • Sensory Organs: Antennae, eyes (compound and simple), statocysts (balance organs).
  • Examples: Economically important: Apis (Honey bee), Bombyx (Silkworm), Laccifer (Lac insect). Vectors: Anopheles, Culex, Aedes. Gregarious pest: Locusta. Living fossil: Limulus (King crab).

Phylum 8: Mollusca (Second Largest Phylum)

Terrestrial or aquatic. Organ-system level, bilateral, triploblastic, coelomate. Unsegmented with a distinct head, muscular foot, and visceral hump. A soft and spongy layer of skin forms a mantle over the visceral hump. Space between hump and mantle is the mantle cavity (contains feather-like gills for respiration/excretion).

  • Head & Mouth: Anterior head region has sensory tentacles. Mouth contains a file-like rasping organ for feeding, called radula.
  • Reproduction: Usually dioecious and oviparous with indirect development.
  • Examples: Pila (Apple snail), Pinctada (Pearl oyster), Sepia (Cuttlefish), Loligo (Squid), Octopus (Devil fish), Aplysia (Sea hare), Dentalium (Tusk shell), Chaetopleura (Chiton).

Phylum 9: Echinodermata

An endoskeleton of calcareous ossicles (Echinodermata = spiny bodied). All are marine with organ-system level. Adult is radially symmetrical but larvae are bilaterally symmetrical.

  • Digestive System: Complete with mouth on the lower (ventral) side and anus on the upper (dorsal) side.
  • Unique Feature: The presence of a water vascular system which helps in locomotion, capture and transport of food, and respiration.
  • Excretory System: Absent.
  • Reproduction: Dioecious. External fertilization. Development is indirect with free-swimming larva.
  • Examples: Asterias (Star fish), Echinus (Sea urchin), Antedon (Sea lily), Cucumaria (Sea cucumber), Ophiura (Brittle star).

Phylum 10: Hemichordata

Earlier considered a sub-phylum under Chordata, now a separate phylum under non-chordata. Contains a small group of worm-like marine animals. Cylindrical body composed of an anterior proboscis, a collar, and a long trunk.

  • Circulation & Respiration: Open circulatory system. Respiration through gills.
  • Excretion: Proboscis gland.
  • Examples: Balanoglossus, Saccoglossus.

NCERT Exact Language: Hemichordates have a rudimentary structure in the collar region called **stomochord**, a structure similar to notochord. (But it is not a homologous true notochord derived from mesoderm).

4. Phylum Chordata & Subphyla

Animals belonging to phylum Chordata are characterized by the presence of a notochord, a dorsal hollow nerve cord, and paired pharyngeal gill slits. They have a post-anal tail and a closed circulatory system.

Subphyla of Chordata
  • Urochordata (Tunicata): Notochord is present only in larval tail. Examples: Ascidia, Salpa, Doliolum.
  • Cephalochordata: Notochord extends from head to tail region and is persistent throughout their life. Example: Branchiostoma (Amphioxus or Lancelet).
  • Vertebrata: Possess notochord during the embryonic period. Notochord is replaced by a cartilaginous or bony vertebral column in the adult. (All vertebrates are chordates, but all chordates are not vertebrates).

6. [BONUS] Structural Org: Frog & Cockroach

🐸 Frog (Rana tigrina) System-by-System Map
  • Morphology: Skin is moist, highly vascularized (cutaneous respiration). Ventral side is pale yellow, dorsal is olive green. Males have sound-producing vocal sacs and a copulatory pad on the first digit of the forelimbs.
  • Digestive: Alimentary canal is short (carnivores have short intestines). Liver secretes bile, stored in gallbladder. Pancreas produces pancreatic juice. Food passes to rectum → Cloaca.
  • Respiration: Water = Cutaneous (skin). Land = Buccal cavity, skin, lungs (pulmonary). In aestivation/hibernation = strictly cutaneous.
  • Circulatory: Closed. 3-chambered heart (2 atria, 1 ventricle). Sinus venosus receives blood from vena cava, joins right atrium. Ventricle opens into a sac-like conus arteriosus. Special venous connections: Hepatic portal system and Renal portal system. RBCs are nucleated and biconvex.
  • Excretory: A pair of compact mesonephric kidneys. Ureotelic (excretes urea). Ureters act as urinogenital ducts in males. Emerge and open into the cloaca.
  • Nervous: CNS (brain in cranium, spinal cord), PNS (10 pairs cranial nerves), ANS. Brain divided into forebrain (olfactory lobes, paired cerebral hemispheres, unpaired diencephalon), midbrain (optic lobes), hindbrain (cerebellum and medulla oblongata).
  • Sensory: Tympanum (receives sound). Eyes have a nictitating membrane to protect in water.
  • Reproduction: Male: 2 testes attached to the upper kidney via mesorchium. 10-12 vasa efferentia enter kidneys, open into Bidder's canal, communicate with urinogenital duct. Female: Ovaries situated near kidneys but no functional connection. Oviducts open separately into the cloaca. External fertilization. Larva = Tadpole (ammonotelic).
🪳 Cockroach (Periplaneta americana) Map
  • Exoskeleton: Entire body covered by hard chitinous exoskeleton. Hardened plates called sclerites (tergites dorsally and sternites ventrally) joined by a thin, flexible articular membrane (arthrodial membrane).
  • Head: Formed by fusion of six segments. Bears compound eyes, feelers (antennae), and biting/chewing mouthparts: Upper lip (labrum), lower lip (labium), paired mandibles, paired maxillae, hypopharynx (tongue).
  • Thorax: Prothorax, mesothorax, metathorax. Forewings (mesothoracic) called tegmina are opaque/leathery and cover the hindwings when at rest. Hindwings (metathoracic) are transparent and used in flight.
  • Digestive: Foregut lined by cuticle. Crop stores food. Proventriculus/Gizzard has an outer layer of thick circular muscles and thick inner cuticle forming six highly chitinous plate-like teeth (grinding). Hepatic caeca (6-8 blind tubules) secrete digestive juice.
  • Circulation & Respiration: Open system. Hemolymph (colorless plasma + hemocytes). Heart consists of an elongated muscular tube differentiated into 13 funnel-shaped chambers with ostia. Respiratory system consists of 10 pairs of spiracles leading to tracheal tubes.
  • Excretion: Malpighian tubules (100-150 yellow, thin, filamentous rings at the junction of midgut and hindgut). Uricotelic (excrete uric acid).
  • Vision: Each eye consists of ~2000 hexagonal ommatidia. They receive multiple images. Referred to as mosaic vision (more sensitivity but less resolution, common during night).

7. [BONUS] Digestion & Absorption

Dentition: Thecodont (embedded in jaw sockets), Diphyodont (two sets of teeth - deciduous/milk and permanent), Heterodont (Incisors, Canines, Premolars, Molars). Dental formula for human adult is 2123/2123 (total 32). Boy/Child dental formula is 2102/2102 (Premolars absent). Hard chewing surface made of enamel.

Histology of Alimentary Canal (Outside to Inside)
  • Serosa: Outermost layer, made of thin mesothelium with connective tissue.
  • Muscularis: Smooth muscles usually arranged into an inner circular and outer longitudinal layer.
  • Sub-mucosa: Loose connective tissue containing nerves, blood, and lymph vessels. In duodenum, glands (Brunner's glands) are present here.
  • Mucosa: Innermost lining. Forms rugae in stomach and villi in small intestine. Goblet cells secrete mucus. Gastric glands in stomach, Crypts of Lieberkuhn in intestine.

Enzymatic Master Map:

• Saliva: Salivary amylase (ptyalin) acts at pH 6.8. Contains lysozyme (antibacterial).

• Stomach (pH 1.8): Pepsinogen is activated by HCl to Pepsin. Rennin is found in gastric juice of infants for milk protein.

• Pancreas: Inactive enzymes include trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, procarboxypeptidases, amylases, lipases, nucleases. Trypsinogen is activated by enterokinase (secreted by intestinal mucosa).

• Intestinal Juice (Succus entericus): Dipeptidases, lipases, nucleosidases. Secretions of brush border cells + goblet cells.

🚨 NEET Pyq Trap: Absorption Logic

Small amounts of monosaccharides like glucose, amino acids, and some electrolytes like chloride are generally absorbed by simple diffusion. Fructose and some amino acids are absorbed with the help of carrier ions like Na+ (facilitated transport). Absorption of water, significant amino acids and glucose into blood requires energy (active transport). Fatty acids and glycerol first form micelles (into intestinal mucosa) then chylomicrons which are transported into lymph vessels (lacteals) in villi.

8. Breathing & Exchange of Gases

Human respiratory system: Nostrils → nasal passage → pharynx → larynx → trachea → primary, secondary, tertiary bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli. Trachea, primary, secondary and tertiary bronchi, and initial bronchioles are supported by incomplete cartilaginous rings.

Respiratory Volumes and Capacities (5-Star)
Tidal Volume (TV)500 mL. Volume inspired/expired during normal respiration.
IRV & ERVIRV: 2500-3000 mL. ERV: 1000-1100 mL. (Additional volumes).
Residual Volume (RV)1100-1200 mL. Volume remaining in lungs even after forcible expiration.
Vital Capacity (VC)ERV + TV + IRV. Maximum volume breathed in after a forced expiration.
🔬 Oxygen Dissociation Curve & Transport

A sigmoid curve. Right shift (Favors Dissociation from Hb): High pCO2, High H+ concentration (lower pH), High temperature. Occurs at the tissues. Left shift (Favors Association): High pO2, Low pCO2, High pH, Low temp. Occurs at alveoli.

NEET 2019 Which of the following is an occupational respiratory disorder?

Silicosis / Asbestosis. These involve proliferation of fibrous tissues (fibrosis) leading to serious lung damage due to chronic exposure to dust in grinding/stone-breaking industries.

9. Body Fluids & Circulation

Blood is a special connective tissue. Plasma (55%), Formed Elements (45%).

  • Plasma Proteins: Fibrinogen (clotting), Globulins (defense mechanisms), Albumins (osmotic balance).
  • Erythrocytes (RBCs): Most abundant (5 - 5.5 million/mm3). Formed in red bone marrow. Biconcave, enucleated. 12-16 g Hb per 100 ml blood. Life span 120 days. Destroyed in spleen (graveyard of RBCs).
  • Leukocytes (WBCs): 6000-8000/mm3. Neutrophils (60-65% - phagocytic), Eosinophils (2-3% - resist infections/allergies), Basophils (0.5-1% - secrete histamine, serotonin, heparin, involved in inflammation), Lymphocytes (B and T forms, 20-25% - immune responses), Monocytes (6-8% - phagocytic).
  • Blood Groups: ABO grouping based on antigens A and B on RBCs. Erythroblastosis fetalis: Rh-ve mother with Rh+ve fetus. Resolved by anti-Rh antibodies immediately post delivery.

Cardiac Cycle & Output: Time = 0.8 seconds. Cardiac Output = Stroke Volume (70 mL) × Heart Rate (72 bpm) ≈ 5000 mL or 5 liters/minute. SAN (Sino-atrial node) is the pacemaker. The first heart sound (lub) is associated with closure of tricuspid/bicuspid valves; the second (dup) with closure of semilunar valves.

10. Excretory Products & Elimination

Kidneys are retroperitoneal, situated between last thoracic and third lumbar vertebra. Outer cortex and inner medulla. Columns of Bertin are cortical extensions between medullary pyramids. Nephron is structural and functional unit (~1 million per kidney).

Functions of the Tubules
  • PCT: Lined by simple cuboidal brush border epithelium (increases surface area). Reabsorbs 70-80% electrolytes and water. Maintains pH by secreting H+, NH3, K+.
  • Henle's Loop: Descending limb is permeable to water, impermeable to electrolytes (concentrates filtrate). Ascending limb is impermeable to water, permeable to electrolytes (dilutes filtrate).
  • DCT: Conditional reabsorption of Na+ and water (regulated by aldosterone & ADH). Secretes H+, K+, NH3.
  • Collecting Duct: Extends from cortex to inner parts of medulla. Large amounts of water are reabsorbed. Allows passage of small amounts of urea into medullary interstitium to keep osmolarity.
🔬 Regulation mechanisms (RAAS vs ANF)

RAAS: Fall in GFR/BP → Juxtaglomerular (JG) cells release Renin. Renin converts Angiotensinogen (liver) → Angiotensin I → Angiotensin II. Angiotensin II is a powerful vasoconstrictor (increases BP & GFR) and activates Adrenal Cortex to release Aldosterone (causes Na+ & Water reabsorption in DCT).
ANF (Atrial Natriuretic Factor): Antagonistic to RAAS. Released by heart atria when blood flow is high. Causes vasodilation to decrease BP.

11. Locomotion & Movement

Macaque skeleton = 206 bones in adults. Axial (80) & Appendicular (126).

Sarcomere Engine — Sliding Filament Theory

Muscle fibre > Myofibril > Sarcomere. A dark band = 'A' band (Anisotropic, contains Myosin + overlapping Actin). A light band = 'I' band (Isotropic, contains only Actin). A 'Z' line bisects each I band.

Mechanism: Motor neuron releases Acetylcholine at neuromuscular junction → Action potential generated in sarcolemma → Ca++ ions released from sarcoplasmic reticulum → Ca++ binds to Troponin subunit on actin filaments to expose active sites → Myosin cross-bridge binds to actin using ATP hydrolysis. During contraction, the 'I' bands shorten, 'H' zones disappear, but the 'A' bands retain their length.

Joints & Disorders:

  • Myasthenia gravis: Auto-immune disorder affecting neuromuscular junction.
  • Muscular dystrophy: Genetic disorder, progressive degeneration of skeletal muscle.
  • Tetany: Rapid spasms due to low Ca++ in body fluid.
  • Gout: Inflammation of joints due to accumulation of uric acid crystals.

12. Neural Control & Coordination

Neuron has three parts: cell body, dendrites, axon. Multipolar (cerebral cortex), Bipolar (retina), Unipolar (embryonic stage).

Resting & Action Potential:

• Resting State (-70mV): Axonal membrane is more permeable to K+ and relatively impermeable to Na+. Axoplasm contains high K+ and negatively charged proteins. The sodium-potassium pump transports 3 Na+ outwards for 2 K+ inwards.

• Depolarization (+30mV): Stimulus causes influx of Na+ into the cell (Na+ channels open). Interior becomes positive.

• Repolarization: Na+ channels close, K+ channels open. Efflux of K+ restores resting potential.

🧠 Brain Structures (NEET Targets)
  • Hypothalamus: Base of thalamus. Controls body temp, urge for eating and drinking. Contains neurosecretory cells.
  • Limbic System: Amygdala + Hippocampus + inner parts of cerebral hemispheres. Controls emotional reactions (excitement, pleasure, rage, fear) and motivation.
  • Midbrain: Corpora quadrigemina (four round swellings).
  • Cerebellum: Very convoluted surface for additional neurons. Coordinates posture/balance.
  • Medulla: Contains centers which control respiration, cardiovascular reflexes, and gastric secretions.

Mechanism of Hormone Action
  • Protein/Peptide Hormones: (Insulin, Glucagon, Pituitary, Hypothalamic). Cannot enter target cells. Bind to cell membrane receptors. Generate Second Messengers (e.g., cyclic AMP, IP3, Ca++) which regulate cellular metabolism.

14. Reproductive Anatomy & Gametogenesis

Male Duct Pathway (Sperm route):

Seminiferous Tubules → Rete Testis → Vasa Efferentia → Epididymis → Vas Deferens → Ejaculatory Duct → Urethra → Urethral meatus.

Accessory Glands: Paired seminal vesicles, a prostate gland, paired bulbourethral glands. Secretions form seminal plasma (rich in fructose, calcium, enzymes).

Spermatogenesis vs Oogenesis
Spermatogenesis:Starts at puberty. Spermatogonia (2n) → Primary spermatocyte (2n) → Meiosis I → Secondary spermatocytes (n) → Meiosis II → Spermatids (n).
Spermiogenesis: Transformation of spermatids into spermatozoa (sperm).
Spermiation: Release of sperm from seminiferous tubules.
Oogenesis:Initiated during embryonic stage. Primary oocytes (2n) are arrested in Prophase I (Diplotene) of meiosis.
At puberty (only 60-80K primary follicles left per ovary). Primary follicle matures into Graafian follicle. Primary oocyte completes Meiosis I just before ovulation → produces a large Secondary oocyte (n) and a tiny first polar body. Meiosis II is only completed after sperm entry.
🔬 Sperm Structure

Head: Contains haploid nucleus and Acrosome (filled with hydrolytic enzymes for fertilization). Middle piece: Contains numerous mitochondria (powerhouse for motility). Tail: Facilitates swimming.

15. Menstrual Cycle, Fertilization & Implantation

Hormonal Synchronization Graph (Menstrual Cycle)

Phases of the Menstrual Cycle:

  • Menstrual phase (Day 1-5): Endometrial lining breaks down due to lack of progesterone.
  • Follicular / Proliferative phase (Day 6-13): Primary follicle grows. Endometrium regenerates. Both LH & FSH increase. Estrogen levels rise.
  • Ovulatory phase (Day 14): Peak of LH (LH Surge) induces rupture of Graafian follicle and release of secondary oocyte.
  • Luteal / Secretory phase (Day 15-28): Ruptured follicle becomes Corpus luteum, which secretes large amounts of Progesterone (essential for maintenance of endometrium for implantation).

Fertilization: Takes place in the ampullary region of the fallopian tube. Sperm comes in contact with the zona pellucida layer of the ovum, inducing changes that block entry of additional sperms (prevents polyspermy). Acrosomal secretions help the sperm enter the cytoplasm, inducing completion of Meiosis II of the secondary oocyte.

16. Pregnancy & Parturition

Zygote → Cleavage (mitosis) → Blastomeres (2, 4, 8, 16 cells). The 8 to 16 blastomere stage is a solid ball called Morula. It continues dividing to form a Blastocyst. The outer layer (Trophoblast) attaches to the endometrium, while the Inner Cell Mass differentiates into the embryo. This attachment is called Implantation.

🚨 NEET Pyq Trap: Placental Hormones

Placenta acts as an endocrine tissue. It produces: hCG (Human Chorionic Gonadotropin), hPL (Human Placental Lactogen), Estrogens, Progestogens. Later in pregnancy, Relaxin is secreted from the ovary.
Note: hCG, hPL, and Relaxin are produced ONLY during pregnancy!

Parturition & Lactation:

Gestation period is ~9 months. Vigorous uterine contractions lead to expulsion of the baby. Signals originate from the fully developed fetus and the placenta inducing mild contractions (Fetal ejection reflex). This triggers release of Oxytocin from the maternal posterior pituitary. Oxytocin causes stronger contractions (positive feedback loop).
Mammary glands produce milk towards the end of pregnancy (Lactation). Initial milk is Colostrum (rich in IgA antibodies for passive immunity).

Infertility & ART (Assisted Reproductive Technologies)

  • IVF (In Vitro Fertilization): Fertilization outside the body. Often followed by ET (Embryo Transfer).
  • ZIFT (Zygote Intra Fallopian Transfer): Zygote or early embryo (up to 8 blastomeres) transferred to Fallopian tube.
  • IUT (Intra Uterine Transfer): Embryo with more than 8 blastomeres transferred directly to Uterus.
  • GIFT (Gamete Intra Fallopian Transfer): Transfer of an ovum collected from a donor into the fallopian tube of another female who cannot produce one.
  • ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection): Sperm is directly injected into the ovum in the lab.
  • AI (Artificial Insemination): Semen collected and artificially introduced into vagina or uterus (IUI - Intra-uterine insemination). Used when sperm count is very low.
NEET 2020 / 2023 Identify the correct matching for ART procedures:

ZIFT: Transfer of zygote / early embryo (≤8 blastomeres) to the Fallopian tube.
IUT: Transfer of embryo (>8 blastomeres) to the Uterus.
Mnemonic: Small embryo goes to small tube, large embryo goes to large uterus.
