Basis of Classification NCERT Foundation

Kingdom Animalia is classified on the basis of several fundamental features. Mastering these is the absolute prerequisite before memorizing individual phyla.

🎯 Diploblastic vs Triploblastic

Diploblastic (2 layers)
  • Only Ectoderm + Endoderm
  • Mesoderm absent
  • Phyla: Porifera, Coelenterata, Ctenophora
  • Undifferentiated mesoglea between layers
Triploblastic (3 layers)
  • Ectoderm + Mesoderm + Endoderm
  • All organs derived from these 3
  • Phyla: Platyhelminthes to Chordates
  • Enables true organ systems

🎯 Coelom — The Most Confusing Criterion

TypeDefinition (NCERT Exact)Phyla
AcoelomateBody cavity (coelom) absent. Solid mesodermal mass between gut and body wall.Porifera, Coelenterata, Ctenophora, Platyhelminthes
PseudocoelomateBody cavity present but NOT lined by mesoderm. Mesoderm is present as scattered pouches.Aschelminthes (Nematoda)
Coelomate (Eucoelomate)True body cavity lined by mesoderm on all sides (as peritoneum).Annelida, Arthropoda, Mollusca, Echinodermata, Hemichordata, Chordata

Most Missed Trap: Porifera = Acoelomate AND Asymmetrical AND Diploblastic AND Cellular level. Students often mark diploblastic for Porifera as "correct" when the question asks about organization — Porifera is CELLULAR level, not tissue level.

🎯 Segmentation (Metamerism)

The body of some animals is externally and internally divided into repetitive segments called metameres. First truly seen in Annelida. Also in Arthropoda (insect head, thorax, abdomen are tagmatized segments). Earthworm has 100–120 metameres.

🎯 Notochord — The Defining Chordate Feature

Notochord — NCERT Definition

A mesodermally derived solid rod-like structure formed on the dorsal side during embryonic development. Animals possessing a notochord at some stage of their life are called chordates. All others are non-chordates.

Stomochord Trap: Hemichordates possess a rudimentary structure in the collar region called stomochord. It is NOT a true notochord (not derived from mesoderm). Hemichordates are placed under Non-Chordates despite the name suggesting otherwise.

NEET 2023 Which of the following is not correctly matched? (A) Porifera – Cellular level (B) Cnidaria – Tissue level (C) Platyhelminthes – Organ level (D) Aschelminthes – Organ-system level with true coelom
Answer: (D)

Why D is wrong: Aschelminthes have organ-system level of organization but they are pseudocoelomate, NOT eucoelomate. They do NOT have a true coelom. The body cavity is not lined by mesoderm.

Quick Check: Aschelminthes = Pseudo (false) coelom. Only Annelida onwards have true coelom.

NEET 2021 Which phylum is correctly described as acoelomate and triploblastic?
Answer: Platyhelminthes

Platyhelminthes (flatworms) are triploblastic (three germ layers present) but acoelomate (no body cavity). This is a unique combination tested repeatedly. Porifera and Cnidaria are diploblastic + acoelomate.

Lower Non-Chordates: Porifera → Platyhelminthes High Yield

Phylum 1 — Porifera (Sponges)

Porifera — Complete Feature Map
  • Habitat: Mostly marine; Spongilla is the famous freshwater exception
  • Organisation: Cellular level (lowest)
  • Symmetry: Asymmetrical (mostly); some may show radial
  • Germ Layers: Diploblastic
  • Coelom: Absent (Acoelomate)
  • Canal System: Water enters via Ostia (pores) → Spongocoel (central cavity) → exits via Osculum. Purpose: food gathering, respiration, waste removal
  • Choanocytes (Collar cells): Flagellated cells lining spongocoel. UNIQUE to Porifera. Create water current.
  • Skeleton: Composed of spicules (CaCO₃ or silica) or spongin fibers (Euspongia = bath sponge)
  • Digestion: Intracellular only
  • Reproduction: Asexual (fragmentation, gemmules) + Sexual. Hermaphrodite. Internal fertilization. Indirect development (flagellated larva)

Phylum 2 — Coelenterata / Cnidaria

Cnidaria — Complete Feature Map
  • Habitat: Aquatic, mostly marine; sessile or free-swimming
  • Organisation: Tissue level
  • Symmetry: Radially symmetrical
  • Germ Layers: Diploblastic
  • Coelom: Absent (Acoelomate); body wall is 2-layered with jelly-like mesoglea
  • Defining Feature: Cnidoblasts (Cnidocytes) — contain Nematocysts (stinging capsules). Present on tentacles and body. Used for anchorage, defense, and prey capture.
  • Polymorphism: Two body forms — Polyp (sessile, cylindrical, e.g., Hydra, Adamsia) and Medusa (umbrella-shaped, free-swimming, e.g., Aurelia/jellyfish)
  • Metagenesis: Alternation of polyp and medusa generations. Seen in Obelia. Polyps produce medusae asexually; medusae produce polyps sexually.
  • Skeleton: Corals (Meandrina) have CaCO₃ skeleton
  • Digestion: Intracellular + Extracellular (first phylum with extracellular digestion)

NEET 2022 Which of the following groups of animals belongs to the same phylum? (A) Physalia, Adamsia, Obelia (B) Asterias, Echinus, Balanoglossus (C) Pheretima, Nereis, Hirudinaria (D) Apis, Cancer, Limulus — all are Arthropods
Answer: (A) and (C) and (D) are correct groups

(A) Physalia, Adamsia, Obelia → All Cnidaria ✓. (B) Asterias, Echinus (Echinodermata) + Balanoglossus (Hemichordata) → WRONG. (C) Pheretima, Nereis, Hirudinaria → All Annelida ✓. (D) Apis (bee), Cancer (crab), Limulus (king crab) → All Arthropoda ✓.

Strategy: Group animals by phylum. Look for the odd one out. Balanoglossus is the classic trap — it sounds like it should be with echinoderms (sea = both) but is Hemichordata.

Phylum 3 — Ctenophora (Comb Jellies / Sea Walnuts)

  • Exclusively marine
  • Radially symmetrical, Diploblastic, Tissue level
  • Body has 8 external rows of ciliated comb plates → help in locomotion
  • Bioluminescence is well-marked in this phylum
  • Reproduction: Sexes NOT separate (hermaphrodite). Only Sexual reproduction. External fertilization. Indirect development.
  • Examples: Pleurobrachia, Ctenoplana

Trap: Students mark Ctenophora as "dioecious" — they are hermaphrodite (monoecious). Also, they have NO cnidoblasts unlike Cnidaria.

Phylum 4 — Platyhelminthes (Flatworms)

Platyhelminthes — Complete Feature Map
  • Shape: Dorso-ventrally flattened (hence "flat worms")
  • Organisation: Organ level (first phylum with true organs)
  • Symmetry: Bilateral
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic
  • Coelom: Acoelomate (solid body, no body cavity)
  • Mostly endoparasitic — hooks and suckers present in parasitic forms
  • Excretion/Osmoregulation: Flame cells (protonephridia). Unique to Platyhelminthes.
  • Reproduction: Mostly hermaphrodite (monoecious). Internal fertilization. Many larval stages. Planaria has remarkable regeneration capacity.
  • No anus — incomplete digestive system (or absent in Taenia)

NEET 2020 Flame cells are the excretory structures seen in which of the following animals?
Answer: Platyhelminthes (e.g., Planaria)

Flame cells (protonephridia) are the characteristic excretory structures of Platyhelminthes. They help in osmoregulation and excretion. The cells have cilia that beat like flickering flames — hence the name.

Memory: "FLAT worms use FLAME cells." Aschelminthes use excretory tubes/pores. Annelida use nephridia. Arthropoda use Malpighian tubules.

Phylum 5 — Aschelminthes / Nematoda (Roundworms)

Aschelminthes — Complete Feature Map
  • Shape: Circular in cross section (hence "round worms")
  • Habitat: Free-living (aquatic/terrestrial), or parasitic in plants and animals
  • Organisation: Organ-system level
  • Symmetry: Bilateral
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic
  • Coelom: Pseudocoelomate — the cavity present is NOT lined by mesoderm
  • Alimentary canal: Complete with well-developed muscular pharynx (and anus!)
  • Excretion: Excretory tube removes body wastes through excretory pore
  • Reproduction: Dioecious (sexes are separate). Females are longer than males. Internal fertilization. Direct or indirect development.
  • Epidermis: Syncytial (multinucleate, no cell boundaries)

NEET 2019 Which of the following statements is correct regarding Ascaris?
Answer: Sexes are separate; females are longer than males

Ascaris is dioecious (male and female are separate organisms). It is pseudocoelomate, NOT coelomate. It shows NO true metamerism (unlike Earthworm). The male has a characteristically curved posterior end. The female is larger/longer.

Traps: (1) Ascaris is NOT hermaphrodite. (2) Ascaris is NOT segmented (no metamerism). (3) Ascaris has NO true coelom — it's pseudocoelomate.

Higher Non-Chordates — Annelida to Hemichordata

Phylum 6 — Annelida (Segmented Worms) 2–3 Qs/yr

Annelida — Complete Feature Map
  • Habitat: Aquatic (marine/freshwater) and terrestrial; free-living or parasitic (Hirudinaria)
  • Organisation: Organ-system level
  • Symmetry: Bilateral
  • Germ layers: Triploblastic
  • Coelom: TRUE coelomate (eucoelomate)
  • Metamerism: True segmentation — body divided into segments (metameres), each containing organs
  • Locomotion: Setae (in Pheretima/earthworm) or Parapodia (in Nereis, lateral appendages with setae). Leeches use suckers.
  • Excretion: Nephridia — coiled tube-like structures
  • Circulation: Closed type — blood vessels present (first phylum with closed circulatory system)
  • Respiration: Through body surface (earthworm) or gills (Nereis)
  • Neural: Double ventral nerve cord with a dorsal brain (cerebral ganglia)
  • Reproduction: Pheretima = hermaphrodite, cross-fertilization. Nereis = dioecious. Hirudinaria = hermaphrodite. Indirect development in Nereis (Trochophore larva).

NEET 2022 In which of the following is the excretory structure nephridia absent? (A) Pheretima (B) Hirudinaria (C) Nereis (D) Wuchereria
Answer: (D) Wuchereria

Wuchereria is a nematode (Aschelminthes), not an annelid. Nematodes have an excretory tube system, NOT nephridia. Nephridia are the characteristic excretory structures of Annelida — present in Pheretima, Nereis, and Hirudinaria.

Phylum 7 — Arthropoda (Jointed Appendages) Largest Phylum

Arthropoda — Complete Feature Map
  • Largest phylum: About 2/3 of all known animal species
  • Defining feature: Jointed appendages (arthro = joint, poda = feet)
  • Exoskeleton: Hard chitinous exoskeleton. Moulting (ecdysis) for growth.
  • Body: Segmented and divided into tagmata (head, thorax, abdomen in insects; cephalothorax + abdomen in Prawn)
  • Coelom: Reduced; main cavity = haemocoel (blood-filled)
  • Circulation: Open type circulatory system (blood flows in haemocoel) — most distinctive feature
  • Excretion: Malpighian tubules (insects and myriapods) or Green glands/antennal glands (Prawn, crabs)
  • Respiration: Gills (aquatic), Book gills (Limulus), Tracheae (insects), Book lungs (spiders)
  • Vision: Compound eyes with ommatidia (mosaic vision), Simple eyes (ocelli)
  • Reproduction: Mostly dioecious. Internal fertilization. Mostly oviparous. Direct or indirect development.

NEET 2024 The excretory organ of Cockroach is: (A) Nephridia (B) Green glands (C) Malpighian tubules (D) Flame cells
Answer: (C) Malpighian tubules

Cockroach (Periplaneta americana) is an insect (Arthropoda). Insects excrete through Malpighian tubules — yellow filamentous structures at the junction of midgut and hindgut (100–150 in cockroach). They remove uric acid → uricotelic.

Phylum 8 — Mollusca (Soft-bodied)

Mollusca — Complete Feature Map
  • Second largest phylum in animal kingdom
  • Habitat: Aquatic (mostly marine, some freshwater) and terrestrial
  • Symmetry: Bilateral (some asymmetrical due to torsion)
  • Coelom: Reduced true coelom
  • Body Plan: Head-foot, Visceral hump (contains organs), Soft mantle. Mantle secretes calcareous shell in most.
  • Excretion: Kidneys (metanephridia-like structures called Keber's organ in some)
  • Respiration: Ctenidia (gills) in aquatic; Pulmonary sac in land snails; Body surface in some
  • Circulation: Open type (except Cephalopoda which have CLOSED circulatory system!)
  • Radula: Rasping organ unique to Mollusca for feeding (absent in Pelecypoda/bivalves)
  • Larva: Trochophore in aquatic species → in advanced Cephalopods, direct development

Phylum 9 — Echinodermata (Spiny-skinned) Tricky Traps

Echinodermata — Complete Feature Map
  • Exclusively marine — no freshwater or terrestrial species
  • Adult symmetry: Radially symmetrical (pentamerous radial)
  • Larval symmetry: Bilaterally symmetrical — NEET's favourite trap!
  • Endoskeleton: Calcareous ossicles. Spines on body surface.
  • Water Vascular System: A unique hydraulic system of canals. Used for locomotion, food capture, and respiration. Tube feet (podia) are part of this system.
  • Coelom: Eucoelomate (true coelom)
  • Excretion: No specific excretory organs — diffusion through body surface
  • Regeneration: Remarkable power — Asterias (starfish) can regenerate a whole animal from a single arm + portion of central disc
  • Reproduction: Dioecious (sexes separate). Indirect development. External fertilization.
  • No head, brain, or heart

NEET 2023 Water vascular system is characteristic of: (A) Annelida (B) Echinodermata (C) Mollusca (D) Platyhelminthes
Answer: (B) Echinodermata

The water vascular system is the unique, defining feature of Phylum Echinodermata. It functions in locomotion, food capture, and respiration through tube feet (podia). No other phylum has this system.

Phylum 10 — Hemichordata

  • Aquatic, marine. Vermiform (worm-like) body. Enteropneusta + Pterobranchia
  • Presence of stomochord in collar region (NOT a true notochord — not mesodermal)
  • Open circulatory system; coelom present
  • Respiration: Pharyngeal gill slits — a pre-chordate feature
  • Excretion: Proboscis gland (Glomerulus)
  • One class with true gill slits → considered connecting link between non-chordates and chordates
  • Example: Balanoglossus (Tongue worm), Saccoglossus

NEET Classic Trap: Balanoglossus = Hemichordata (NOT Chordata, NOT Echinodermata). The stomochord is NOT a notochord. Despite having gill slits, it lacks the other three chordate features (dorsal hollow nerve cord at pharyngeal stage, notochord, post-anal tail).

NEET 2021 / 2018 Balanoglossus is: (A) Annelida (B) Echinodermata (C) Hemichordata (D) Chordata
Answer: (C) Hemichordata

Balanoglossus (tongue worm) is the most famous hemichordate. It has pharyngeal gill slits and a proboscis coelom but only has a stomochord (not a true notochord). It was earlier grouped with Chordata but is now placed in Hemichordata.

Phylum Chordata — Subphyla & All Vertebrate Classes

Chordates — Four Defining Characters Must Master

The 4 Fundamental Chordate Characters (ALL 4 must be present at some stage)

1. Notochord — dorsal, mesodermal, rod-like; persists throughout life or replaced by vertebral column

2. Dorsal hollow nerve cord — lies dorsal to notochord (non-chordates have solid, ventral nerve cord)

3. Pharyngeal gill slits — present at some stage; functional in aquatic, vestigial in adults of higher forms

4. Post-anal tail — tail extending beyond the digestive opening at some stage

Sub-phyla of Chordata

Urochordata (Tunicata)
  • Notochord only in LARVAL tail
  • Adults sessile; lose notochord
  • Example: Ascidia, Doliolum, Salpa
  • "Urochordates are more advanced as larvae than as adults"
Cephalochordata
  • Notochord extends from head to tail throughout life
  • Example: Amphioxus (Branchiostoma)
  • Marine, free-swimming/burrowing
  • "Lancelet" — connecting link to vertebrates
Vertebrata
  • Notochord replaced by bony/cartilaginous vertebral column in adults
  • True skull (cranium), brain, paired appendages
  • All 7 classes of vertebrates

Trap: In Urochordata, the larva has a notochord in its tail — adults lose it. Do NOT say "all urochordates have notochord." Only in larval stage. Doliolum, Salpa and Ascidia are the key examples.

Structural Organisation — Earthworm (Pheretima posthuma)

Pheretima posthuma — Complete Morphology & Anatomy 2–3 Qs/yr

🔴 Reproductive system — 1 Q
🔵 Nephridia types — 1 Q
🟡 Excretory/Digestive anatomy — 1 Q

External Morphology

  • Body: Long, cylindrical, bilaterally symmetrical; Reddish-brown
  • Segments: 100–120 metameres. Segment 1 = peristomium (bears mouth). Last = pygidium (bears anus).
  • Prostomium: A lobe on first segment that helps in burrowing. Sensory in function.
  • Clitellum: Thick girdle-like structure on segments 14–16. Secretes cocoon for eggs. Present only in sexually mature adults.
  • Setae: S-shaped chitinous structures on every segment except 1st, last, and clitellar segments. 8 setae per segment (except clitellum). Help in locomotion (movement against soil).
  • Genital openings: Male pores on segment 18. Female pore (single, midventral) on segment 14. Spermathecal pores (4 pairs) on segments 5/6, 6/7, 7/8, and 8/9.

Digestive System — NCERT Line by Line

  • Mouth → Buccal cavity (1–3) → Pharynx (4th) → Oesophagus (5–7) → Muscular Gizzard (8–9) → Stomach (9–14) → Intestine (15–last) → Rectum → Anus
  • Gizzard: Segments 8–9. Grinds soil particles. Strongly muscular.
  • Stomach: Segments 9–14. Has calciferous glands (neutralize humic acid in soil).
  • Typhlosole: Internal median fold of the intestinal wall. Begins from 26th segment. Increases surface area for absorption. NCERT-specific term tested in NEET.
  • Intestinal caeca: A pair of conical, short, hollow projections that arise from the intestine at segment 26. Secrete digestive enzymes.

Excretory System — 3 Types of Nephridia

Nephridial Types (NEET Favourite)
  • Septal nephridia: Attached to septa (from 15th to last segment). Discharge into intestine (enteronephric). Most numerous (400 per septum = thousands total).
  • Integumentary nephridia: Attached to inner surface of body wall (from 3rd to last segment). Discharge waste to exterior through skin (exonephric).
  • Pharyngeal nephridia: Present in segments 4th, 5th, and 6th. Exonephric.

Trap: Earthworm = Ammonotelic (excretes ammonia through body surface and nephridia). The nephridia are the structural unit, but the nature of excretion is ammonotelism. Students confuse this with uricotelism (cockroach/birds) or ureotelism (frog, mammals).

Reproductive System & Reproduction

  • Earthworm is hermaphrodite (bisexual) but NOT self-fertilizing — requires cross-fertilization
  • Testes: 2 pairs, in segments 10 and 11
  • Ovaries: 1 pair, attached to 12th–13th septum
  • Spermathecae: 4 pairs, in segments 6–9. Store sperm received from another worm during mating.
  • Cocoon: Secreted by clitellum (segments 14–16). Contains fertilized eggs. Direct development — no larval stage.
  • Reproduction occurs in rainy season. Two worms align facing opposite directions and exchange sperm.
NEET 2020 / 2023 The typhlosole in Pheretima posthuma is: (A) an excretory fold (B) a circular dorsal fold of intestine to increase SA (C) a median longitudinal fold of intestinal wall (D) a secretory organ
Answer: (C)

Typhlosole = a ridge formed by the internal median fold of the intestinal wall (from 26th segment onwards). It increases the surface area of the intestine for absorption. It is a feature unique to earthworm in the NCERT context — not to be confused with the typhlosole in other invertebrates.

NEET 2022 In Pheretima, spermathecal pores are present in: (A) 4–8 segments (B) 5/6, 6/7, 7/8, 8/9 intersegmental grooves (C) only on segment 14 (D) on segment 18
Answer: (B)

Spermathecal pores: 4 pairs, located in intersegmental grooves of 5/6, 6/7, 7/8, and 8/9. Male pores are on segment 18. Female pore is on segment 14. Clitellum spans segments 14–16. These exact numbers appear in NEET regularly.

Chapter 5 · NCERT Class XI Ch. 7
Structural Organisation — Cockroach (Periplaneta americana)

Periplaneta americana — Complete Anatomy High Yield

External Morphology

  • Body: Divided into head, thorax, abdomen. Dark brown. Chitinous exoskeleton.
  • Sclerites: Hardened plates (tergites = dorsal, sternites = ventral) joined by flexible articular membrane
  • Head: Formed by fusion of 6 segments. Bears compound eyes, antennae (1 pair), and mouthparts: Labrum (upper lip), Mandibles (paired, for biting), Maxillae (paired, for holding & tasting), Labium (lower lip), Hypopharynx (tongue-like)
  • Thorax: 3 segments — Prothorax, Mesothorax, Metathorax. Legs: 3 pairs (one on each). Wings: 2 pairs — Tegmina (mesothoracic, opaque, leathery, protective) + hindwings (metathoracic, transparent, used in flight)
  • Abdomen: 10 segments. Anal cerci (last segment, both sexes). Anal styles (9th segment, male only). 7th sternite (female) forms keel-like structure with 8th & 9th → forms brood pouch/genital pouch.

Digestive System

  • Mouth → Pharynx → Oesophagus → Crop (stores food) → Gizzard/Proventriculus (6 chitinous teeth — grinds food) → Midgut/Mesenteron → Hindgut → Rectum → Anus
  • Hepatic caecae (6–8 blind tubules) at junction of foregut and midgut. Secrete digestive enzymes. NOT excretory!
  • Salivary glands open into buccal cavity; secrete salivary amylase
  • Foregut and hindgut are lined by cuticle; midgut is NOT.

Biggest Trap: Malpighian tubules ≠ Hepatic caecae. Hepatic caecae are DIGESTIVE (at foregut-midgut junction). Malpighian tubules are EXCRETORY (at midgut-hindgut junction). Students confuse these in nearly every exam.

Circulatory System

  • Open type circulatory system. Blood = Haemolymph (colourless plasma + haemocytes)
  • Heart: Elongated, muscular tube with 13 funnel-shaped chambers (each with paired ostia for blood entry)
  • Blood flows from heart through aorta → haemocoel → back to heart through ostia

Excretory System

  • Malpighian tubules: 100–150 yellow filamentous tubules at midgut-hindgut junction. Extract uric acid from haemolymph. Cockroach is uricotelic.
  • Fat body also stores uric acid (uricose glands) — temporary storage.

Nervous System

  • Brain (cerebral ganglia) + Double ventral nerve cord with fused ganglia
  • 3 fused ganglia in thorax + 6 in abdomen
  • Compound eyes: ~2000 hexagonal ommatidia. Mosaic vision — low resolution but high sensitivity (good in low light)
  • 3 simple eyes (ocelli) also present

Reproductive System

  • Male: 1 pair of testes (4th–6th abdominal segments). Mushroom gland = accessory reproductive gland (produces seminal fluid). Phallomere (external genitalia, asymmetrical)
  • Female: 2 pairs of ovaries (2nd–6th abdominal segments). Each ovary has 8 ovarioles. Ootheca = egg case formed by female. Each ootheca has up to 16 eggs.
  • Cockroach is oviparous. Development = paurometabolous (incomplete metamorphosis). Nymph resembles adult but lacks wings.
NEET 2023 / 2018 In the cockroach, which structure opens into the anterior end of the midgut and secretes digestive enzymes?
Answer: Hepatic caecae

Hepatic caecae (6–8 hollow blind tubules) open into the anterior part of the midgut. They secrete digestive enzymes and are involved in digestion. They are NOT excretory structures. Malpighian tubules are at the mid-hindgut junction and are excretory.

NEET 2024 Which of the following statements about cockroach's eye is correct?
Answer: Each eye has ~2000 ommatidia and forms mosaic image

Each compound eye is made of approximately 2000 hexagonal ommatidia. Each ommatidium sees a slightly different angle of view → together form a mosaic image. This provides high sensitivity and wide field but poor resolution. Cockroaches also have 3 simple ocelli for light detection.

Nephron — Segment Functions

  • PCT: Brush border epithelium. Reabsorbs all glucose + amino acids, 70–80% Na⁺, HCO₃⁻, water. Secretes H⁺, K⁺, NH₃.
  • Descending Henle: Water permeable, solute impermeable → filtrate concentrates.
  • Ascending Henle (thick): Water IMPERMEABLE. Actively pumps NaCl out → medullary gradient created.
  • DCT: Na⁺ reabsorption (aldosterone). H₂O reabsorption (ADH). K⁺ secretion.

Counter-Current Mechanism
  • Loop of Henle = Counter-current MULTIPLIER — Creates medullary 300→1200 mOsm/L gradient
  • Vasa Recta = Counter-current EXCHANGER — PRESERVES the gradient (prevents washout)
  • ADH → aquaporins in collecting duct → water follows gradient → concentrated urine formed
  • Gradient: 300 mOsm/L (cortex) → 1200 mOsm/L (inner medulla)

Answer: (B) Maintain osmotic gradient

Loop of Henle CREATES the medullary gradient. Vasa Recta MAINTAINS it. If the vasa recta were absent (or flow was too fast), the gradient would be washed away. The counter-current exchange in vasa recta ensures solutes stay in medulla and water returns to circulation.

Chapter 12 · NCERT Class XI Ch. 20
Locomotion & Movement — Sliding Filament Theory

Sarcomere, Contraction Mechanism & Skeleton 2–3 Qs/yr

Sarcomere — Key Bands

  • A band (Dark/Anisotropic): Contains myosin (thick) filaments + overlapping actin. DOES NOT CHANGE in length during contraction.
  • I band (Light/Isotropic): Only actin (thin) filaments. SHORTENS during contraction.
  • H zone: Only myosin (in centre of A band). DISAPPEARS during full contraction.
  • Z line: Bisects I band; actin attached here. Distance between 2 Z lines = 1 sarcomere.
  • M line: Centre of A band. Myosin anchored here.

Sliding Filament Theory — Step by Step

  • ACh released at NMJ → action potential → spreads along T-tubules
  • Ca²⁺ released from Sarcoplasmic Reticulum → binds Troponin C
  • Tropomyosin shifts → active sites on actin exposed
  • Energized myosin head (ATP→ADP+Pi) binds actin → cross-bridge
  • Power stroke: myosin head bends → actin slides inward → I band + H zone shorten; A band constant
  • New ATP binds → cross-bridge breaks → cycle repeats
  • When Ca²⁺ removed back to SR → Tropomyosin covers actin → muscle relaxes

NEET Trick: A band = ALWAYS SAME. I band + H zone SHORTEN. This is tested by asking "what does NOT change?" → Answer: A band.

NEET 2023 During skeletal muscle contraction, which band/zone remains UNCHANGED?
Answer: A Band

The A band contains myosin filaments. Since myosin does NOT shorten (only actin slides), the A band length stays constant. I bands shorten, H zone disappears, sarcomere shortens. If the question says "A band shortens" → eliminate that option immediately.

  • Spermiogenesis: Transformation of spermatid → spermatozoon (no cell division; morphological change)
  • Spermiation: Release of spermatozoa from Sertoli cells into lumen of seminiferous tubule
  • Net result: 1 spermatogonium → 4 spermatozoa (all haploid)

Sperm Structure (Spermatozoon)

  • Head: Nucleus (condensed haploid DNA) + Acrosome (cap; contains hydrolytic enzymes = acrosin, hyaluronidase for penetrating ovum)
  • Neck (midpiece): Contains numerous mitochondria arranged in spiral → ATP production for motility
  • Tail: Flagellum for motility. 9+2 microtubule arrangement.
  • Human sperm: ~60 µm long; lifespan ~48–72 hours in female reproductive tract
NEET 2023 The cells that nourish developing spermatozoa are: (A) Leydig cells (B) Sertoli cells (C) Spermatogonia (D) Interstitial cells
Answer: (B) Sertoli cells

Sertoli cells (sustentacular cells) are located within the seminiferous tubules and form the blood-testis barrier. They nourish and support developing sperm. They also secrete inhibin (which provides negative feedback to inhibit FSH). Leydig cells = testosterone secretion (outside tubules).

Female Reproductive System — Complete NCERT

Ovaries — Structure

  • 2 ovaries; each ~3×1.5×1 cm; attached to pelvic wall and uterus by ligaments
  • Cortex: Contains follicles at various stages (primordial, primary, secondary, Graafian)
  • Medulla: Connective tissue, blood vessels, nerves
  • At birth: ~2 million immature follicles → by puberty only ~40,000 remain → ~400–500 undergo full maturation during reproductive life

Oogenesis — Step by Step

  • Oogonia (2n): Mitosis during foetal development → form primary oocytes (2n)
  • Primary oocyte (2n): Begins meiosis I but is arrested at diplotene stage of meiosis I prophase until puberty (years to decades!)
  • At puberty + LH surge: Meiosis I completes → 1 large secondary oocyte (n) + 1 small first polar body (n)
  • Secondary oocyte: Begins meiosis II but arrested at metaphase II
  • Fertilisation triggers: Meiosis II completion → mature ovum (n) + 2nd polar body. WITHOUT fertilisation, meiosis II never completes.
  • Net result: 1 oogonium → 1 mature ovum + 3 polar bodies (all haploid). Only 1 functional egg.

Critical Traps: (1) The cell released at ovulation is the secondary oocyte (NOT mature ovum). Meiosis II completes only after fertilization. (2) Primary oocyte is arrested in diplotene of prophase I (NOT metaphase). (3) In oogenesis, 1 oogonium → 1 egg. In spermatogenesis, 1 spermatogonium → 4 sperm.

Menstrual Cycle — NCERT Complete

Menstrual Cycle — 28-day Average
  • Menstrual phase (Day 1–5): Shedding of endometrium (stratum functionalis). FSH levels begin to rise. Low estrogen + progesterone.
  • Follicular/Proliferative phase (Day 1–13): FSH stimulates follicle development. Growing follicle → ↑Estrogen → endometrium proliferates. LH and FSH levels rise. Just before ovulation: LH surge on day 13–14 (most important signal).
  • Ovulation (Day 14): LH surge → Graafian follicle ruptures → secondary oocyte released → ovulation. Fimbriae of fallopian tube catch the oocyte.
  • Luteal/Secretory phase (Day 15–28): Ruptured follicle → Corpus luteum (yellow body). Corpus luteum secretes progesterone + estrogen. Endometrium prepares for implantation.
  • If no fertilisation: Corpus luteum degenerates → corpus albicans → progesterone drops → menstruation begins (day 28/1).
  • If fertilisation occurs: Corpus luteum maintained by hCG (from trophoblast) → progesterone sustained → no menstruation.

NEET 2022 The LH surge in the menstrual cycle triggers: (A) Corpus luteum formation directly (B) Release of secondary oocyte from Graafian follicle (C) Endometrial proliferation (D) FSH secretion
Answer: (B) Release of secondary oocyte (ovulation)

The LH surge (around day 13–14) is the primary trigger for ovulation — it causes Graafian follicle to rupture and release the secondary oocyte. After ovulation, the remnant follicle → corpus luteum (this is secondary to the LH surge, not the direct trigger). Endometrial proliferation is driven by estrogen (not LH surge).

Fertilisation & Implantation

  • Fertilisation occurs in the ampulla of fallopian tube (outer 1/3 of fallopian tube)
  • Sperm → acrosome reaction (releases hyaluronidase + acrosin) → penetrates zona pellucida → fuses with secondary oocyte plasma membrane
  • Fertilisation → polyspermy block (zona reaction / fast block by membrane depolarization)
  • Secondary oocyte now completes meiosis II → mature ovum. Forming zygote (2n).
  • Zygote → mitosis along fallopian tube → morula (16 cells, solid ball) → blastocyst (hollow)
  • Implantation: Blastocyst buries into endometrium (uterine wall) on ~6th day after fertilisation. Trophoblast cells invade endometrium → placenta forms.

ART — Assisted Reproductive Technologies

  • IVF (In Vitro Fertilisation): Egg + sperm fertilized in lab → test-tube baby. Embryo transferred to uterus at 8-cell stage (Embryo Transfer = ET).

NEET 2022 The test-tube baby procedure involves which of the following? (A) GIFT (B) IVF-ET (C) ICSI (D) ZIFT
Answer: (B) IVF-ET

"Test-tube baby" specifically refers to IVF (In Vitro Fertilisation) — fertilisation occurs outside the body in a glass dish ("in vitro"). The resultant embryo is then transferred to the uterus (ET = Embryo Transfer). Louise Brown was the first IVF baby (1978). GIFT transfers gametes, ZIFT transfers zygotes, ICSI is microinjection into egg.
