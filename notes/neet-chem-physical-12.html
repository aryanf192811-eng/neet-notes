Density Formula

🎯 Schottky vs Frenkel:
Schottky — "Skeleton" defect: entire ions gone. Density drops. NaCl, CsCl, KBr (large ions, high CN)
Frenkel — "Freelancer" defect: ion moves to interstitial. Density unchanged. ZnS, AgCl (large size difference)
AgBr has BOTH Frenkel AND Schottky — asked every 3 years in NEET
F-centre (Farbzentrum): colour due to trapped electron absorbing visible light. NaCl+Na vapour → yellow. NOT a Frenkel defect!

Semiconductors

Intrinsic: Pure semiconductor, e⁻ excited by heat. Si, Ge

n-type: Group 15 (P, As) doped into Group 14 (Si) → extra electron → n-type

p-type: Group 13 (B, Al, Ga) doped into Group 14 → electron hole → p-type

Superconductors: Zero resistance below critical T (Tc). e.g., Nb₃Ge at 23K

Magnetic Properties

Paramagnetic: Weakly attracted, unpaired e⁻ (e.g., TiO, VO, CuO)

Ferromagnetic: Strongly attracted, permanent magnetism (e.g., Fe, Ni, Co, CrO₂)

Antiferromagnetic: Net zero magnetism, opposite spin arrangement (e.g., MnO)

Ferrimagnetic: Unequal opposite spins → net moment (e.g., Fe₃O₄, ferrites)

Diamagnetic: Weakly repelled, all e⁻ paired (e.g., NaCl, TiO₂)

NEET 2023 Which of the following point defects are shown by AgBr crystal? (a) Frenkel only (b) Schottky only (c) Frenkel and Schottky (d) Neither
Answer: (c) Frenkel and Schottky
Concept

AgBr is unique — shows BOTH defects: Frenkel (Ag⁺ moves to interstitial, small size difference) AND Schottky (pairs of Ag⁺/Br⁻ go missing)

Rule

AgCl, AgI show mostly Frenkel. NaCl shows Schottky. AgBr shows BOTH — memorise this!

Why

AgBr has intermediate properties — neither too ionic (favoring only Schottky) nor too covalent, allowing both defect types to coexist.

2.1 Types of Solutions & Solubility NCERT §2.2

🔵 2 Qs/paper — colligative properties most tested Boiling point elevation & depression — every year 2018–2024 Osmosis & osmotic pressure — 2024, 2022, 2021
Henry's Law

$p = K_H \cdot x$ (partial pressure of gas ∝ mole fraction in solution)

$K_H$ = Henry's constant (different for each gas-solvent pair)

Higher T → higher $K_H$ → lower solubility of gas

Application: soda fizz, O₂ in blood, diving (bends)

Solubility Rules

"Like dissolves like": polar solvent dissolves polar solute

Gas in liquid: solubility decreases with temperature

Gas in liquid: solubility increases with pressure (Henry's law)

Solid in liquid: generally increases with temperature (exceptions: NaCl slightly decreases)

2.2 Raoult's Law & Vapour Pressure Lowering NCERT §2.3 — Central Formula

$p_A = p_A^\circ \cdot \chi_A$  &&  $p_{\text{total}} = p_A^\circ\chi_A + p_B^\circ\chi_B$
Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure (RLVP)

$\dfrac{p^\circ - p_s}{p^\circ} = \dfrac{\Delta p}{p^\circ} = \chi_{\text{solute}} = \dfrac{n_2}{n_1 + n_2} \approx \dfrac{n_2}{n_1}$ (dilute)

RLVP = mole fraction of solute (non-volatile)

It is a colligative property — depends only on number of solute particles

Ideal vs Non-Ideal Solutions

Ideal: Obeys Raoult's at all concentrations. ΔV_mix = 0, ΔH_mix = 0. A-B = A-A = B-B forces. Example: Benzene + Toluene

Positive deviation: p > Raoult. A-B weaker than A-A/B-B. ΔH_mix > 0. Example: Ethanol + Acetone, Ethanol + Water, CCl₄ + CHCl₃

Negative deviation: p < Raoult. A-B stronger. ΔH_mix < 0. Example: Acetone + Chloroform (CHCl₃), HCl + Water, HNO₃ + Water

⚠ NEET TRAP — Azeotrope

Minimum boiling azeotrope: Positive deviation mixtures. Boils at LOWER temperature than either component. Example: Ethanol-water (95.7% ethanol by mass). Cannot be separated by simple distillation.
Maximum boiling azeotrope: Negative deviation mixtures. Boils at HIGHER temperature. Example: HNO₃-H₂O (68% HNO₃), HCl-H₂O (20.2% HCl)

🎯 Deviation → Azeotrope → Thermodynamics — Complete Chain:

Positive Deviation (e.g. Ethanol + Acetone):
A–B bonds weaker than A–A / B–B → molecules escape easily → Vapour pressure HIGH → Boiling point LOW → Minimum boiling azeotrope
$\Delta H_{mix} > 0$ (Endothermic — heat absorbed to break A–A/B–B bonds)
$\Delta V_{mix} > 0$ (Expansion — molecules less tightly held)

Negative Deviation (e.g. Acetone + Chloroform):
A–B bonds stronger than A–A / B–B → molecules trapped → Vapour pressure LOW → Boiling point HIGH → Maximum boiling azeotrope
$\Delta H_{mix} < 0$ (Exothermic — extra energy released forming strong A–B bonds)
$\Delta V_{mix} < 0$ (Contraction — molecules pack closer together)

Memory: Positive deviation = Positive ΔH = Positive ΔV. Negative deviation = Negative ΔH = Negative ΔV. Signs always match the deviation.

Numerical — Raoult's Law: 18 g glucose (M=180) dissolved in 90 g water (M=18). Find vapour pressure if p°(water) = 17.5 mmHg.
$n_1(\text{water}) = 90/18 = 5$ mol; $n_2(\text{glucose}) = 18/180 = 0.1$ mol
$\chi_1 = 5/(5+0.1) = 5/5.1 = 0.9804$
$p_s = p^\circ \times \chi_1 = 17.5 \times 0.9804 = $ 17.16 mmHg
Or: RLVP $= (17.5 - p_s)/17.5 = 0.1/5.1 = 0.0196$ → $p_s = 17.5(1-0.0196) = 17.16$ mmHg

2.3 Colligative Properties — All Four NCERT §2.4 — Highest NEET Yield

Colligative Properties

Properties that depend ONLY on the number of solute particles, not their nature/identity. The four colligative properties: (1) Lowering of VP, (2) Elevation in BP, (3) Depression in FP, (4) Osmotic Pressure.

1. Elevation in Boiling Point (ΔTb)

$\Delta T_b = K_b \times m$

$K_b$ = ebullioscopic constant (molal elevation constant)

$m$ = molality (mol of solute / kg of solvent)

$K_b(\text{water}) = 0.512$ K·kg/mol

$M_2 = \dfrac{1000 \times K_b \times w_2}{\Delta T_b \times w_1}$ (molar mass determination)

2. Depression in Freezing Point (ΔTf)

$\Delta T_f = K_f \times m$

$K_f$ = cryoscopic constant (molal depression constant)

$K_f(\text{water}) = 1.86$ K·kg/mol

$K_f(\text{benzene}) = 5.12$ K·kg/mol

$M_2 = \dfrac{1000 \times K_f \times w_2}{\Delta T_f \times w_1}$

3. Osmotic Pressure (π)

$\pi = CRT = \dfrac{n}{V}RT$ (van't Hoff equation)

$\pi = iCRT$ (with van't Hoff factor i)

C = molarity (mol/L); R = 0.0821 L·atm·mol⁻¹K⁻¹

Isotonic: same π → same concentration at same T

Best for high molecular mass substances (very sensitive)

Osmosis & Osmotic Pressure

Osmosis: Solvent flows from lower → higher concentration through semipermeabe membrane

Reverse osmosis: Apply π > osmotic pressure → force solvent from concentrated to dilute (water purification)

Hypotonic: cell swells (lower external π)

Hypertonic: cell shrinks/plasmolysis (higher external π)

Numerical — ΔTf: 1.8 g of solute dissolved in 100 g water, ΔTf = 0.186 K. Find molar mass. (Kf = 1.86 K·kg/mol)
$\Delta T_f = K_f \times m = K_f \times \dfrac{w_2 \times 1000}{M_2 \times w_1}$
$M_2 = \dfrac{K_f \times w_2 \times 1000}{\Delta T_f \times w_1} = \dfrac{1.86 \times 1.8 \times 1000}{0.186 \times 100} = \dfrac{3348}{18.6} = $ 180 g/mol (glucose)

Numerical — Osmotic Pressure: 0.1 M sucrose solution at 27°C. Find osmotic pressure.
$\pi = CRT = 0.1 \times 0.0821 \times 300 = $ 2.463 atm

2.4 Van't Hoff Factor (i) — Electrolytes NCERT §2.6 — Master This

$i = \dfrac{\text{observed colligative property}}{\text{normal (calculated) colligative property}} = \dfrac{\text{actual particles}}{\text{formula units dissolved}}$
Modified Colligative Property Formulae

$\Delta T_b = i \cdot K_b \cdot m$

$\Delta T_f = i \cdot K_f \cdot m$

$\pi = i \cdot C \cdot R \cdot T$

$\dfrac{\Delta p}{p^\circ} = i \cdot \chi_{\text{solute}}$

i Values for Common Electrolytes

Non-electrolyte (glucose, urea): i = 1 (no dissociation)

NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻: i = 2 (complete dissoc.)

CaCl₂ → Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻: i = 3

AlCl₃ → Al³⁺ + 3Cl⁻: i = 4

K₂SO₄ → 2K⁺ + SO₄²⁻: i = 3

For association (e.g., acetic acid in benzene): i < 1

Degree of Dissociation from i

For electrolyte $A_x \rightarrow xA^{n+}$:

$i = 1 + (x-1)\alpha$ where α = degree of dissociation

$\alpha = \dfrac{i-1}{x-1}$

For NaCl: $i = 1 + (2-1)\alpha = 1+\alpha$ → $\alpha = i-1$

⭐ Association Formula (NTA LOVES THIS)

For n molecules associating: $nA \rightleftharpoons A_n$

$\boxed{\alpha = \dfrac{1-i}{1 - 1/n}}$

Classic example: Acetic acid / Benzoic acid in Benzene (non-polar solvent). Dimerizes: n = 2.

If 100% dimerized (α = 1): $i = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$

If 50% dimerized (α = 0.5): $i = 1 - \alpha(1 - 1/n) = 1 - 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.75$

Trick: Association → i < 1 (fewer particles). Dissociation → i > 1.

🚫 CRITICAL NTA TRAP — Isotonic Solutions

Two solutions are isotonic when their osmotic pressures are equal: $\pi_1 = \pi_2$
This means: $i_1 C_1 RT = i_2 C_2 RT$ → $i_1 C_1 = i_2 C_2$

⚠️ DO NOT simply equate $C_1 = C_2$ — this only works if BOTH solutions have i = 1 (non-electrolytes).

NTA Classic Trap: "Which solution is isotonic with 0.1 M NaCl?"
NaCl: i = 2 → effective particles = 0.1 × 2 = 0.2
Urea (i=1): needs C = 0.2 M to be isotonic ✓
Glucose (i=1): needs C = 0.2 M ✓
CaCl₂ (i=3): needs C = 0.2/3 = 0.067 M ✓

Always apply: effective concentration = i × C when comparing isotonic solutions.

Numerical — Van't Hoff factor: 1 g NaCl (M=58.5) in 200 g water. ΔTf = 0.639 K. Find i. (Kf = 1.86)
$m = \dfrac{1 \times 1000}{58.5 \times 200} = \dfrac{1000}{11700} = 0.0855$ mol/kg
$i = \dfrac{\Delta T_f}{K_f \times m} = \dfrac{0.639}{1.86 \times 0.0855} = \dfrac{0.639}{0.159} = $ 4.02 ≈ 4

3.1 Electrochemical Cells — EMF & Standard Potential NCERT §3.3

🔴 2-3 Qs/paper — Nernst equation most tested Standard EMF calculation — 2024, 2023, 2022, 2021 Kohlrausch's Law — 2023, 2021, 2019
Standard Cell EMF

$E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cathode}} - E^\circ_{\text{anode}}$

$E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{reduction (cathode)}} - E^\circ_{\text{reduction (anode)}}$

Cathode: reduction (higher SRP) → positive electrode in Galvanic cell

Anode: oxidation (lower SRP) → negative electrode

SRP = Standard Reduction Potential (25°C, 1M, 1 atm)

Cell Notation (IUPAC)

$\text{Anode (oxidation)} \mid \text{Anode sol.} \parallel \text{Cathode sol.} \mid \text{Cathode (reduction)}$

Example: $\text{Zn} \mid \text{Zn}^{2+}(1M) \parallel \text{Cu}^{2+}(1M) \mid \text{Cu}$

Single bar (|) = phase boundary; Double bar (||) = salt bridge

$E^\circ = E^\circ_{\text{Cu}^{2+}/\text{Cu}} - E^\circ_{\text{Zn}^{2+}/\text{Zn}} = 0.34-(-0.76) = 1.10$ V

Key SRP Values (Memorise)

Li⁺/Li: −3.05 V (strongest reducing agent)

Al³⁺/Al: −1.66 V

Zn²⁺/Zn: −0.76 V

Fe²⁺/Fe: −0.44 V

Cu²⁺/Cu: +0.34 V (cathode in Daniel cell)

Ag⁺/Ag: +0.80 V

F₂/F⁻: +2.87 V (strongest oxidising agent)

Gibbs Energy & Equilibrium

$\Delta G^\circ = -nFE^\circ_{\text{cell}}$

$F = 96500$ C/mol (Faraday constant)

$n$ = moles of electrons transferred (from balanced equation)

At equilibrium: $E_{\text{cell}} = 0$ → $\Delta G = 0$ → $Q = K$

$E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = \dfrac{RT\ln K}{nF} = \dfrac{0.0591 \log K}{n}$ (at 298K)

Galvanic vs Electrolytic

Galvanic (Voltaic): Spontaneous reaction → produces electricity. Daniel cell. ΔG < 0, E > 0.

Electrolytic: Non-spontaneous → requires external electricity. Electrolysis of NaCl, electroplating. ΔG > 0.

Fuel cell: Continuous galvanic cell using fuel (H₂ + O₂ → H₂O). No combustion.

⚡ SRP Order Trick:
Higher SRP → better oxidising agent → tends to get reduced → is the cathode
Lower SRP → better reducing agent → tends to get oxidised → is the anode

3.2 Nernst Equation — Most Tested NEET Formula NCERT §3.4 — MASTER THIS

$E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - \dfrac{RT}{nF}\ln Q \quad \Longrightarrow \quad E_{\text{cell}} = E^\circ_{\text{cell}} - \dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log Q \quad \text{(at 298K)}$
Q in Nernst Equation

Q is the reaction quotient (like Kc but at any concentration)

For oxidation at anode: $\text{Zn} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + 2e^-$

For reduction at cathode: $\text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu}$

Overall: $\text{Zn} + \text{Cu}^{2+} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + \text{Cu}$

$Q = \dfrac{[\text{Zn}^{2+}]}{[\text{Cu}^{2+}]}$

Nernst — Key Relationships

At equilibrium ($E = 0$): $E^\circ = \dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log K_c$

Concentration cell: $E^\circ = 0$ → $E = -\dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log\dfrac{c_1}{c_2} = +\dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log\dfrac{c_2}{c_1}$

Electrode potential: $E_{M^{n+}/M} = E^\circ_{M^{n+}/M} + \dfrac{0.0591}{n}\log[M^{n+}]$

Numerical — Nernst: Calculate $E_{\text{cell}}$ for Daniel cell when $[\text{Cu}^{2+}] = 0.1$ M and $[\text{Zn}^{2+}] = 0.01$ M. ($E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = 1.10$ V)
$E = 1.10 - \dfrac{0.0591}{2}\log\dfrac{0.01}{0.1} = 1.10 - 0.02955 \times \log(0.1)$
$= 1.10 - 0.02955 \times (-1) = 1.10 + 0.02955 = $ 1.1296 V ≈ 1.13 V
(log 0.1 = −1, so Q < 1 → E > E°)

Numerical — K from E°: $E^\circ_{\text{cell}} = 1.10$ V, n = 2. Find K.
$\log K = \dfrac{n \times E^\circ}{0.0591} = \dfrac{2 \times 1.10}{0.0591} = \dfrac{2.20}{0.0591} = 37.23$
$K = 10^{37.23} \approx 1.7 \times 10^{37}$ (very large → reaction essentially complete)

⚡ Nernst Trick — When Q < 1 vs Q > 1:
log Q < 0 → E > E° (cell is more favourable than standard)
log Q > 0 → E < E° (cell less favourable)
When [product] increases → Q increases → E decreases (reaction less spontaneous)
Concentration cell: Higher concentration at cathode → cell works better

3.3 Conductance — Kohlrausch's Law NCERT §3.5

Conductance Definitions

Resistance $R = \rho \dfrac{l}{A}$ ($\rho$ = resistivity)

Conductance $G = \dfrac{1}{R} = \kappa \dfrac{A}{l}$ (siemens, S)

Conductivity $\kappa = \dfrac{G \cdot l}{A}$ (S/m or S/cm)

Cell constant $= \dfrac{l}{A}$ (measured experimentally)

Molar Conductivity (Λm)

$\Lambda_m = \dfrac{\kappa \times 1000}{C}$ (C in mol/L, κ in S/cm)

Or: $\Lambda_m = \kappa / C$ (if both in SI units)

Units: S·cm²·mol⁻¹

Strong electrolyte: $\Lambda_m$ increases with dilution slowly

Weak electrolyte: $\Lambda_m$ increases sharply with dilution

Kohlrausch's Law

At infinite dilution, molar conductivity = sum of λ of individual ions:

$\Lambda^\circ_m = \lambda^\circ_+ + \lambda^\circ_-$ (for 1:1 electrolyte)

For CH₃COOH: $\Lambda^\circ_m = \lambda^\circ_{H^+} + \lambda^\circ_{CH_3COO^-}$

$= \Lambda^\circ_m(\text{HCl}) + \Lambda^\circ_m(\text{CH}_3\text{COONa}) - \Lambda^\circ_m(\text{NaCl})$

Used to find $\Lambda^\circ_m$ of WEAK electrolytes (can't measure directly)

Debye-Hückel-Onsager Equation

For strong electrolyte: $\Lambda_m = \Lambda^\circ_m - A\sqrt{C}$

Plot $\Lambda_m$ vs $\sqrt{C}$ → straight line

Intercept = $\Lambda^\circ_m$; Slope = A

Extrapolate to C=0 (infinite dilution) to get $\Lambda^\circ_m$

Degree of Dissociation from Λm

$\alpha = \dfrac{\Lambda_m}{\Lambda^\circ_m}$

$K_a = \dfrac{C\alpha^2}{1-\alpha}$

Degree of dissociation of weak acid increases with dilution

⭐ Equivalent Conductance (Λeq)

$\Lambda_{eq} = \dfrac{\Lambda_m}{n\text{-factor}}$

n-factor: for NaCl = 1, H₂SO₄ = 2, AlCl₃ = 3

Example: $\Lambda_m(\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4) = 860$ S·cm²·mol⁻¹ → $\Lambda_{eq} = 860/2 = 430$ S·cm²·eq⁻¹

Units: S·cm²·eq⁻¹ (equivalent⁻¹ instead of mol⁻¹)

📐 Cell Constant (G*) — Fixed Property

$G^* = \dfrac{l}{A}$ (length / cross-sectional area of the cell)

Cell constant is a fixed property of the conductivity cell — it does NOT change with concentration, type of electrolyte, or temperature of the solution being measured.

It is measured using a standard KCl solution of known conductivity (calibration). Once determined, the same cell constant is used for all subsequent measurements.

$\kappa = G \times G^*$ (conductivity = measured conductance × cell constant)

NEET Trap: If asked "what changes when concentration changes?" — Conductance (G) and Molar conductivity (Λm) change. Cell constant (G*) stays the same.

Numerical — Kohlrausch's Law: Given: Λ°(HCl) = 426; Λ°(NaCl) = 126; Λ°(CH₃COONa) = 91 S·cm²·mol⁻¹. Find Λ°(CH₃COOH).
$\Lambda^\circ_m(\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}) = \Lambda^\circ(\text{HCl}) + \Lambda^\circ(\text{CH}_3\text{COONa}) - \Lambda^\circ(\text{NaCl})$
$= 426 + 91 - 126 = $ 391 S·cm²·mol⁻¹

3.4 Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis NCERT §3.7

Faraday's First Law

Mass of substance deposited/liberated ∝ quantity of electricity (charge) passed.
$w = \dfrac{M \times Q}{n \times F} = \dfrac{M \times I \times t}{n \times F}$
(M = molar mass, n = electrons per atom/ion, F = 96500 C, I = current in A, t = time in seconds)

Electrochemical Equivalent (Z):
$w = Z \cdot I \cdot t$    where    $Z = \dfrac{M}{n \times F}$
Z = mass deposited per coulomb (g/C). Units: g·A⁻¹·s⁻¹ or g·C⁻¹.
Trap: Z is the mass deposited when exactly 1 ampere flows for 1 second (i.e., by 1 coulomb of charge). Different metals have different Z values. Ag has the highest Z among common metals.

Faraday's Second Law

When same charge passes through different electrolytes connected in series, masses deposited are proportional to their equivalent masses.
$\dfrac{w_1}{w_2} = \dfrac{E_1}{E_2}$ where $E = M/n$ (equivalent mass)

Numerical — Electrolysis: How many grams of Cu deposited when 2A current flows for 30 min through CuSO₄ solution? (M Cu = 63.5, n = 2)
$Q = I \times t = 2 \times 30 \times 60 = 3600$ C
$w = \dfrac{63.5 \times 3600}{2 \times 96500} = \dfrac{228600}{193000} = $ 1.185 g

Efficiency = $\dfrac{\Delta G}{\Delta H}$ (much higher than thermal combustion ~40% vs ~80%)

Used in: Apollo space program, modern fuel cell vehicles

Electrolyte: KOH solution. Electrodes: Porous carbon with Pt catalyst

Key advantage: No combustion, no pollution, very high efficiency

Rate Expression

$\text{Rate} = -\dfrac{1}{a}\dfrac{d[A]}{dt} = k[A]^m[B]^n$

Rate law can ONLY be determined experimentally (not from stoichiometry)

Order of reaction w.r.t. A = m (experimentally determined)

Overall order = m + n

Units of Rate Constant k

⭐ Gas-Phase Reactions — Pressure-Based 1st Order Formula (NTA SHIFT ALERT)

For reaction: $A(g) \rightarrow B(g) + C(g)$ or similar gas decompositions, NTA uses total pressure (Pt) instead of concentrations.

Let initial pressure of A = $P_i$. At time t, A decomposes by pressure 'x': pressure of A = $P_i - x$; B and C each = $x$.

Total pressure: $P_t = (P_i - x) + x + x = P_i + x$ → $x = P_t - P_i$

Pressure of A at time t: $P_A = P_i - x = P_i - (P_t - P_i) = 2P_i - P_t$

Substituting into 1st order formula $k = \dfrac{2.303}{t}\log\dfrac{[A]_0}{[A]_t}$:

$k = \dfrac{2.303}{t}\log\dfrac{P_i}{2P_i - P_t}$

Memory: Denominator = $2P_i - P_t$ (double initial minus total). Numerator = $P_i$ (initial). Applicable only when A decomposes into 2 products with equal stoichiometry.

Numerical — Gas Phase 1st Order: $A(g) \rightarrow B(g) + C(g)$. Initial pressure = 500 mmHg. After 10 min, total pressure = 700 mmHg. Find k.
$x = 700 - 500 = 200$ mmHg. $P_A = 500 - 200 = 300$ mmHg (or: $2P_i - P_t = 1000 - 700 = 300$)
$k = \dfrac{2.303}{10}\log\dfrac{500}{300} = \dfrac{2.303}{10} \times \log(5/3) = \dfrac{2.303}{10} \times 0.2218 = $ $5.1 \times 10^{-2}$ min⁻¹

⭐ Collision Theory — New NMC Addition

For a reaction to occur, colliding molecules need BOTH conditions:

(1) Threshold Energy: Kinetic energy ≥ Activation Energy (Ea)

(2) Proper Orientation: Molecules must collide in the correct geometrical arrangement

Rate equation from Collision Theory:

$\text{Rate} = P \cdot Z_{AB} \cdot e^{-E_a/RT}$

P = Steric/Probability factor (accounts for orientation; P ≤ 1)
Z_AB = Collision frequency (number of collisions per unit volume per second)
$e^{-E_a/RT}$ = Boltzmann factor (fraction of molecules with energy ≥ Ea)

Why P < 1? Not all collisions with enough energy lead to reaction — molecules must be oriented correctly. E.g., for $\text{NO} + \text{O}_3 \rightarrow \text{NO}_2 + \text{O}_2$, N atom of NO must hit O of O₃.

Arrhenius A (frequency factor): $A = P \cdot Z_{AB}$ — combines orientation and collision frequency.

4.3 Half-Life (t₁/₂) — Key Formulae NCERT §4.5

Half-Life Summary

Zero order: $t_{1/2} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2k}$ (depends on initial concentration)

First order: $t_{1/2} = \dfrac{0.693}{k}$ (independent of initial concentration)

Second order: $t_{1/2} = \dfrac{1}{k[A]_0}$ (inversely proportional to initial conc.)

First Order — Special Values

$t_{1/2} = \dfrac{0.693}{k}$ (0.693 = ln 2)

After n half-lives: $[A] = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2^n}$

After 1 t½: 50% remains; 2 t½: 25%; 3 t½: 12.5%

For 75% completion (25% remaining): t = 2 × t½

For 87.5% completion (12.5% remaining): t = 3 × t½

Radioactive Decay

All radioactive decay = first order kinetics

$N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$ where λ = decay constant

$t_{1/2} = \dfrac{0.693}{\lambda}$

Age by C-14: $t = \dfrac{t_{1/2}}{0.693}\ln\dfrac{N_0}{N}$

Numerical — Half-Life: A first-order reaction has t½ = 60 min. Starting with 100 mg, how much remains after 3 hours?
3 hours = 180 min; Number of half-lives = 180/60 = 3
Amount remaining $= \dfrac{100}{2^3} = \dfrac{100}{8} = $ 12.5 mg

⚡ Graph Trick — t½ vs [A]₀ (NTA's favourite graph question):

Zero Order: $t_{1/2} = \dfrac{[A]_0}{2k}$ → $t_{1/2} \propto [A]_0$
Graph of $t_{1/2}$ vs $[A]_0$ = straight line through the origin (positive slope = 1/2k)

First Order: $t_{1/2} = \dfrac{0.693}{k}$ → independent of $[A]_0$
Graph of $t_{1/2}$ vs $[A]_0$ = horizontal line parallel to x-axis (constant value = 0.693/k)

Second Order: $t_{1/2} = \dfrac{1}{k[A]_0}$ → $t_{1/2} \propto \dfrac{1}{[A]_0}$
Graph of $t_{1/2}$ vs $[A]_0$ = hyperbola (decreasing curve, t½ → ∞ as [A]₀ → 0)

Instant identifier: If NTA shows a t½ vs [A]₀ graph — horizontal line = 1st order. Straight line through origin = 0th order. Decreasing curve = 2nd order.

4.4 Arrhenius Equation — Temperature & Rate NCERT §4.7 — Every NEET

$k = A \cdot e^{-E_a/RT}$     $\ln k = \ln A - \dfrac{E_a}{RT}$     $\log\dfrac{k_2}{k_1} = \dfrac{E_a}{2.303R}\left(\dfrac{T_2-T_1}{T_1 T_2}\right)$
Arrhenius Parameters

$A$ = pre-exponential factor (frequency factor) — depends on collision frequency and orientation

$E_a$ = activation energy (J/mol or kJ/mol)

$R$ = 8.314 J·K⁻¹·mol⁻¹

$T$ = temperature in KELVIN

Higher $E_a$ → slower reaction (exponential decrease in k)

Graph Interpretation

Plot $\ln k$ vs $1/T$ → straight line (slope = $-E_a/R$)

$\text{Slope} = -\dfrac{E_a}{R}$ → $E_a = -\text{slope} \times R$

Intercept = $\ln A$

Catalyst: lowers $E_a$ → increases k → faster reaction. A unchanged.

Temperature Coefficient

$\dfrac{k(T+10)}{k(T)} \approx 2$ to $3$ (reaction rate doubles per 10°C rise)

Lyophilic sols (starch, albumin, gum, gelatin): Direct mixing with dispersion medium. Stable. No special technique needed.

Positively charged: Fe(OH)₃, Al(OH)₃, TiO₂, haemoglobin (at low pH)

Charge arises due to selective adsorption of ions from the medium

Peptization (Sol from Precipitate)

Adding a small amount of electrolyte to a freshly prepared precipitate converts it to a sol

Example: Fresh Fe(OH)₃ precipitate + FeCl₃ → Fe(OH)₃ sol (Fe³⁺ ions adsorbed → positive charge → dispersion)

The added electrolyte is called the peptizing agent

P.2 Dialysis — Purification of Colloidal Solutions NCERT §5.4.5

Definition — Dialysis

Dialysis is the process of removing dissolved impurities (electrolytes, crystalloids) from a colloidal solution by using a semipermeable membrane (parchment paper or cellophane).

The colloidal solution is placed inside a bag/pouch made of the membrane and immersed in pure water. Small ions and molecules pass through the membrane into the water, while large colloidal particles cannot pass.

Dialysis vs Electrodialysis

Simple Dialysis: Uses parchment membrane in water. Slow process. Used for biochemical purification.

Electrodialysis: Same membrane but external electric field applied → accelerates migration of ions → faster purification

Application: Kidney dialysis machine (haemodialysis) works on this principle — removes urea and toxins from blood

What Passes / What Doesn't

Passes through parchment membrane: Crystalloids, small ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻), electrolytes, small molecules (glucose, urea)

Cannot pass: Colloidal particles (1–1000 nm — too large for membrane pores)

This is why colloidal gold, Fe(OH)₃ sol, starch remain inside the bag after dialysis
