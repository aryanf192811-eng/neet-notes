Chapter 4 (Class 11)
Chemical Bonding & Molecular Structure

Bond Types & Fajan's Rules ~38 PYQs (2009–2025)

Chemical Bonding generates 2 questions per paper reliably. NEET tests: (1) VSEPR geometry and shape, (2) hybridisation, (3) bond angle comparisons, (4) molecular orbital theory — bond order, magnetic properties, (5) Fajan's rules, (6) dipole moment reasoning.

Fajan's Rules — Covalent Character in Ionic Compounds

Covalent character increases when: cation is small & highly charged (high charge density), anion is large & highly polarizable. Correct order for lithium halides: LiI > LiBr > LiCl > LiF (covalent character increases as anion gets larger and more polarizable). Among same anion: Be²⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Ca²⁺ > Sr²⁺ > Ba²⁺ for covalent character.

Key NEET fact: BeF₂ has covalent character despite looking ionic. BeCl₂ is covalent, not ionic — NCERT confirms this.

👉 What NEET asks: VSEPR shape of a molecule or ion (with/without lone pairs). Bond angle comparison orders. Which molecule has zero dipole moment. MOT bond order + magnetic property. Hybridisation of central atom. Number of σ and π bonds in a given structure. Isostructural species. Lone pair count on central atom.
⚠ Typical trap: Lewis structure of O₂ shows all paired → students say diamagnetic (WRONG — it's paramagnetic by MOT). BF₃ looks like it should be pyramidal (it's trigonal planar — no lone pair on B). XeF₂ looks bent (it's linear — 3 equatorial lone pairs). NF₃ dipole > NH₃ (WRONG — NH₃ has larger dipole). Counting σ bonds incorrectly in alkynes.
⚡ Fast solving rule: For any VSEPR question: count bond pairs + lone pairs on central atom → get total electron pairs → assign geometry from table. For dipole moment: draw structure → if all dipoles cancel by symmetry → μ = 0. For MOT: bond order = (bonding e⁻ − antibonding e⁻)/2; if any unpaired e⁻ in MO diagram → paramagnetic.

🔷 Hybridisation — Instant Determination

Formula for Hybridisation

$H = \dfrac{1}{2}[V + M - C + A]$ where V = valence electrons of central atom, M = monovalent atoms attached, C = cationic charge, A = anionic charge.

H = 2 → sp | H = 3 → sp² | H = 4 → sp³ | H = 5 → sp³d | H = 6 → sp³d² | H = 7 → sp³d³

🔷 Formal Charge — NCERT Formula & NEET Usage

Formal Charge Formula (NCERT Ch. 4)

$\text{Formal Charge} = V - \frac{1}{2}B - L$ where V = valence electrons in free atom, B = number of electrons in bonding (shared) pairs, L = number of non-bonding (lone pair) electrons on atom.

Usage: The structure with smallest formal charges (or with negative FC on more electronegative atom) is most stable/preferred Lewis structure. For CO₂: on O with double bond, FC = 6 − ½(4) − 4 = 0. Structure with FC = 0 on all atoms is preferred. For N₂O (linear): most stable is N=N=O where middle N has FC = 0.

NEET trap: Formal charge ≠ oxidation state. Formal charge assumes equal sharing; oxidation state assumes complete transfer.

🔷 Lattice Enthalpy & Born-Haber Cycle

Lattice Enthalpy — NCERT Key Concept

Lattice enthalpy = energy required to separate 1 mole of ionic compound into its gaseous ions. Always positive (endothermic process). Higher lattice enthalpy → stronger ionic bond → higher melting point. Lattice enthalpy ∝ charge of ions / distance between ions.

Order: LiF > NaF > KF (same charge, decreasing lattice enthalpy as cation size increases). MgO > NaF (both isoelectronic ions, but Mg²⁺/O²⁻ has higher charge than Na⁺/F⁻ → much higher lattice enthalpy). MgO (3795 kJ/mol) vs NaCl (787 kJ/mol) — the charge effect dominates.

Born-Haber Cycle (NCERT): Indirect method to calculate lattice enthalpy using Hess's law. For NaCl: ΔH_f = ΔH_sublimation(Na) + ΔH_dissociation(½Cl₂) + IE₁(Na) + EGA(Cl) + ΔH_lattice. Since ΔH_f is known (−411 kJ/mol for NaCl), all other terms allow calculation of lattice enthalpy (−787 kJ/mol for NaCl).

🔷 Hydrogen Bonding — Types & Examples

Hydrogen Bond — NCERT Definition & Examples

Condition: H must be bonded to a highly electronegative atom (F, O, N only). The H then attracts another electronegative atom. Strength order: F-H···F > O-H···O > N-H···N.

Intermolecular H-bonding (between molecules): HF, H₂O, NH₃, alcohols, carboxylic acids, proteins. Causes: high boiling point (H₂O: 100°C, HF: 19.5°C vs HCl: −85°C), high surface tension, anomalous density of water (ice floats — H-bonds create open cage structure). HF boils higher than HCl, HBr, HI despite lower MW.

Intramolecular H-bonding (within same molecule): o-nitrophenol (lowers bp, decreases water solubility compared to p-nitrophenol which has intermolecular H-bonding and is more water soluble, higher bp). Salicylaldehyde, o-chlorophenol.

NEET Decision Rule: "Which has higher boiling point?" — check for H-bonding first. If both have H-bonds, check molecular weight. If one has H-bond and other doesn't → H-bonded compound boils higher regardless of MW (usually).

⚗ Micro Topics — Back Bonding, Resonance & Polarization

Back Bonding (pπ→dπ or pπ→pπ*) — NEET Hidden Concept

Definition: Back bonding occurs when a filled orbital (usually a lone pair in a p orbital) of one atom donates electron density INTO an empty or low-energy orbital of the adjacent atom. This creates partial multiple-bond character, shortening and strengthening the bond beyond what a single bond predicts.

Classic example — BF₃: B has an empty 2p orbital (sp², trigonal planar). Each F has lone pairs in filled 2p orbitals. The 2p(F) lone pairs can donate into the empty 2p(B) orbital → this is pπ→pπ back bonding. Result: B-F bond has partial double bond character → B-F bond in BF₃ is shorter (130 pm) than expected for a pure B-F single bond (~152 pm). This makes BF₃ a weaker Lewis acid than expected (the empty orbital is partially filled by back bonding).

Why BCl₃ is a stronger Lewis acid than BF₃: In BCl₃, the Cl lone pairs are in 3p orbitals — back donation into B's 2p orbital is less effective due to poor 2p-3p overlap (size mismatch). Therefore the empty 2p orbital of B remains more available for accepting electron pairs → BCl₃ is a better Lewis acid. Lewis acid strength: BF₃ < BCl₃ < BI₃ (back bonding decreases down the halogen series).

Back bonding in SiO₂ vs CO₂: SiO₂ forms a polymeric solid (quartz structure) because Si uses its 3d orbitals to back-bond with O's lone pairs, reducing the tendency to form discrete Si=O double bonds. CO₂ forms discrete molecules with C=O double bonds (pπ-pπ, period 2 elements form strong multiple bonds). Contrast: CO₂ = gas, SiO₂ = solid at room temperature.

Back bonding in PF₃ complexes (metal chemistry): CO, CN⁻, and NO are strong field ligands partly because they accept electron density from metal d orbitals into their antibonding π* orbitals (π-back bonding from metal to ligand). This stabilises the metal-ligand bond and places these at the top of the spectrochemical series.

Resonance Ranking Rules — Which Structure Is Most Stable?

Definition: When a molecule cannot be represented by a single Lewis structure, the set of contributing structures are resonance structures. The actual molecule is the resonance hybrid — it has properties intermediate between all structures. NEET asks: which resonance structure contributes MOST to the hybrid.

Stability ranking rules (in order of importance):

Rule 1 — Formal charge minimisation: The structure with SMALLEST formal charges (ideally all zero) is most stable and contributes most to the hybrid. FC = V − ½B − L.

Rule 2 — Negative FC on more electronegative atom: If FC cannot be zero, the structure where the negative formal charge is on the MORE electronegative atom is preferred. Example: In CO (carbon monoxide), the structure :C≡O: has FC(C) = −1, FC(O) = +1, which is less stable than C⁻≡O⁺ → WAIT, for CO the preferred structure actually has FC(C) = −1 and FC(O) = +1 because the triple bond structure has these values. The correct interpretation: the structure :C≡O: is preferred despite placing negative FC on C (less electronegative) because it has lower total charge magnitude AND reflects experimental bond length (close to triple bond).

Rule 3 — Octet completion for electronegative atoms: Structures where electronegative atoms (N, O, F) complete their octets are preferred. Electropositive atoms (like B, Al) may tolerate less than octet.

Rule 4 — Like charges not adjacent: Structures where same-sign formal charges are on adjacent atoms are less stable (like-charge repulsion). Opposite formal charges on adjacent atoms → more stable (electrostatic attraction).

NEET Applications: For O₃: two equivalent resonance structures (O-O=O ↔ O=O-O) → hybrid has both O-O bonds equal at 128 pm (between single 148pm and double 121pm). For SO₃: three equivalent resonance structures → all S-O bonds equal → planar, 120°. For CO₃²⁻: three equivalent structures → all C-O bonds equal at 129 pm. The structure with MORE double bonds generally has lower formal charges → preferred.

Polarization vs Polarizability — The Critical Distinction for NEET

Polarization: The DISTORTION of the electron cloud of an anion caused by the cation's electric field. Polarization is what the CATION does TO the anion. High polarization → high covalent character in ionic compounds (Fajan's Rules).

Factors increasing polarization (cation side): (1) Small size of cation (high charge density), (2) High charge on cation (+3 > +2 > +1). Highly polarizing cations: Be²⁺, Li⁺ (small for their charge), Al³⁺.

Polarizability: The EASE with which the electron cloud of an anion (or atom) can be DISTORTED. Polarizability is a property of the ANION (or neutral species). High polarizability = easily distorted.

Factors increasing polarizability (anion side): (1) Large size of anion (electrons farther from nucleus, held loosely), (2) High negative charge (more electrons, greater repulsion, easier distortion). Most polarizable anions: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻ (size order). Large negative ions = highly polarizable.

Fajan's Rules in terms of polarization/polarizability: High covalent character when: (a) Cation is SMALL and HIGHLY CHARGED (highly polarizing) AND (b) Anion is LARGE (highly polarizable). LiI has maximum covalent character among lithium halides: Li⁺ is small (polarizing), I⁻ is large (polarizable). LiF has minimum covalent character: F⁻ is small and not polarizable.

NEET question pattern: "Which has maximum covalent character?" → Apply: small cation + large anion. "Arrange in order of covalent character: LiF, LiCl, LiBr, LiI" → LiI > LiBr > LiCl > LiF (anion size increases). "Which is more covalent: AlCl₃ or NaCl?" → AlCl₃ (Al³⁺ has much higher charge → more polarizing than Na⁺).

Polarizability in covalent molecules — London Forces: Larger molecules with more electrons → more polarizable → stronger London (dispersion) forces → higher boiling point. This explains why I₂ boils at 184°C and F₂ at −188°C despite both being diatomic. Polarizability ∝ molecular size/electron count.

📋 PYQ Solved — Chemical Bonding

NEET 2023 Which of the following species is NOT isostructural with SiCl₄?
Answer: XeF₄ is NOT isostructural with SiCl₄
Concept

Isostructural species must have identical bonding (same number of bond pairs AND same number of lone pairs on central atom). Same shape alone is insufficient — the full electron geometry must match.

Rule

SiCl₄: Si = 4 valence e⁻, 4 Cl attached, 0 lone pairs → sp³ → Tetrahedral. For isostructural: need 4 bond pairs + 0 lone pairs on central atom.

Application

CCl₄ ✓ (4bp, 0lp, sp³), SO₄²⁻ ✓ (4 S-O bonds, sp³), BF₄⁻ ✓ (sp³), NH₄⁺ ✓ (sp³). XeF₄: Xe has 8 valence e⁻, 4 bonds to F → 2 lone pairs remaining → sp³d² → Square planar (NOT tetrahedral). XeF₄ has same shape as SiCl₄? No — XeF₄ is square planar, SiCl₄ is tetrahedral. NOT isostructural.

Answer

XeF₄ — 4 bonds BUT 2 lone pairs → sp³d² → square planar. Bond pair count matches but lone pair count does NOT. Isostructural requires BOTH to match.

NEET 2022 Which one of the following molecules/ions does NOT contain unpaired electrons? (A) O₂⁻ (B) B₂ (C) N₂ (D) O₂
Answer: (C) N₂
Concept

Molecular Orbital Theory (MOT) determines magnetic nature. Unpaired electrons in MOs → paramagnetic. All paired → diamagnetic. Lewis structures are WRONG for O₂ (NEET classic trap).

Rule

Fill MOs in order: σ1s, σ*1s, σ2s, σ*2s, [π2p (before σ2p for B₂,C₂,N₂)], σ2p, π*2p, σ*2p. Use Hund's rule for degenerate π* levels.

Application

N₂ (14e⁻): fills all bonding MOs completely → bond order 3, all paired → diamagnetic. O₂ (16e⁻): 2 electrons in degenerate π*2p → each in separate orbital by Hund's rule → 2 unpaired → paramagnetic. O₂⁻ (17e⁻): 3 electrons in π*2p → 1 unpaired → paramagnetic. B₂ (10e⁻): π2p fills before σ2p → 2 unpaired → paramagnetic.

Answer

(C) N₂ — Bond order 3, all electrons paired, diamagnetic. O₂ paramagnetic is the classic trap (Lewis structure fools students into thinking it's diamagnetic).

NEET 2021 The correct order of bond angles in BF₃, NH₃ and H₂O is:
Answer: BF₃ > NH₃ > H₂O
Concept

VSEPR: Lone pairs repel more than bond pairs. Each lone pair on the central atom reduces the bond angle because lp-lp > lp-bp > bp-bp repulsion compresses the remaining bond pairs together.

Rule

More lone pairs on central atom → smaller bond angle. BF₃ has 0 lp (120°), NH₃ has 1 lp (107°), H₂O has 2 lp (104.5°). Each additional lone pair reduces angle by approximately 2-3°.

Application

BF₃: sp², 0 lp → ideal trigonal planar → 120°. NH₃: sp³, 1 lp → pyramidal → 107° (1 lp compresses). H₂O: sp³, 2 lp → bent → 104.5° (2 lp compress further).

Answer

BF₃ (120°) > NH₃ (107°) > H₂O (104.5°) — Decreasing order matches increasing lone pair count: 0 → 1 → 2.

NEET 2019 The number of σ and π bonds in pent-2-en-4-yne (CH₃–CH=CH–C≡CH) are:
Answer: 10σ bonds, 3π bonds
Concept

Every bond (single, double, or triple) contributes exactly 1σ bond. Each double bond contributes an additional 1π. Each triple bond contributes an additional 2π. Count all C-H and C-C bonds separately for σ count.

Rule

σ bonds = all C-H bonds + all C-C bonds (regardless of bond order, each C-C linkage = 1σ). π bonds = 1 per double bond + 2 per triple bond. For any C-H bond = 1σ, C-C single = 1σ, C=C = 1σ+1π, C≡C = 1σ+2π.

Application

CH₃–CH=CH–C≡CH: C-H bonds: CH₃(3) + CH=(1) + =CH(1) + ≡CH(1) = 6 σ(C-H). C-C bonds: C1-C2(1σ), C2=C3(1σ+1π), C3-C4(1σ), C4≡C5(1σ+2π) = 4σ + 3π. Total: σ = 6+4 = 10, π = 3.

Answer

10σ, 3π — σ comes from all bonds counted once (10 total C-H + C-C bonds). π comes from the alkene double bond (1π) + alkyne triple bond (2π) = 3π.

NEET 2016 Which of the following has the highest dipole moment? (A) CHCl₃ (B) CH₂Cl₂ (C) CCl₄ (D) CH₃Cl
Answer: (D) CH₃Cl — highest dipole moment
Concept

Dipole moment depends on both bond polarity and molecular geometry (vector addition of individual bond dipoles). As Cl atoms replace H atoms in CH₄, the C-Cl bond dipoles increasingly oppose the resultant — beyond CH₃Cl, adding more Cl atoms causes cancellation.

Rule

Correct dipole moment values: CCl₄ = 0 D (symmetric, all cancel). CHCl₃ = 1.08 D. CH₂Cl₂ = 1.60 D. CH₃Cl = 1.87 D. Order: CH₃Cl > CH₂Cl₂ > CHCl₃ > CCl₄. Adding one Cl to CH₄ gives maximum net dipole (C-Cl dipole + 3 C-H dipoles all roughly reinforce).

Application

In CH₃Cl: three C-H dipoles point "toward Cl side" and the C-Cl bond is polar → all dipoles add up → largest resultant. In CHCl₃: three C-Cl dipoles partly cancel each other; only C-H dipole is different → smaller net. In CCl₄: full tetrahedral symmetry → zero.

Answer

(D) CH₃Cl (1.87 D) — Maximum dipole. Classic NEET trap: students wrongly choose CH₂Cl₂ or CHCl₃. The key insight is that maximum asymmetry occurs at mono-substitution, not at higher substitutions. CCl₄ = 0 D confirms symmetry cancels all dipoles.

⚠ CRITICAL TRAP (NEET 2016): Many students choose CH₂Cl₂ thinking "two Cl atoms = more polarity." This is wrong. Dipole moment is a vector quantity. As Cl substitution increases beyond CH₃Cl, the Cl-side dipoles start cancelling each other. CH₃Cl has the highest net dipole because all bond vectors point in the most reinforcing direction. Memorise order: CH₃Cl (1.87D) > CH₂Cl₂ (1.60D) > CHCl₃ (1.08D) > CCl₄ (0D).

NEET 2018 Which species has the maximum number of lone pairs on the central atom? (A) ClF₃ (B) SF₄ (C) XeF₂ (D) XeF₄

Concept

🔷 Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff) — Deep Dive

Z_eff: The Master Concept Behind ALL Periodic Trends

Definition: Z_eff = Z − σ, where Z = actual nuclear charge (proton count) and σ = shielding constant. Z_eff is the NET nuclear charge "felt" by an outer electron after accounting for the screening (shielding) by inner electrons.

Why it drives all trends: Higher Z_eff → nucleus pulls electrons tighter → smaller radius, higher IE, higher electronegativity. This single concept explains the entire periodic table of trends if you understand how Z and σ change across periods and down groups.

Slater's Rules for σ (screening constant) — NEET simplified:

1. Electrons in shells higher than the electron of interest contribute 0 to shielding (they don't shield it). 2. Electrons in the SAME shell: each contributes 0.35 (except 1s pair where it's 0.30). 3. Electrons in (n-1) shell: each contributes 0.85. 4. Electrons in (n-2) and below: each contributes 1.00. 5. For d and f electrons: electrons in same subshell contribute 0.35, all those to the left contribute 1.00.

Key result — Across a period: As we move left→right, Z increases by 1 each step. The new electron enters the SAME subshell. Same-subshell shielding is only 0.35 (much less than 1). So Z_eff increases by ~0.65 per step → electrons feel a significantly stronger pull. Result: atomic radius decreases, IE increases.

Key result — Down a group: New electron enters a new (higher) principal quantum level (new shell). The (n-1) shell shielding increases dramatically (0.85 per electron), and there are more core electrons → σ increases nearly as fast as Z → Z_eff increases only slightly. BUT the new electron is in a farther shell (higher n), and atomic radius depends on n². Result: radius increases despite slightly higher Z_eff.

Z_eff: Period 2 (2s/2p electrons)

Li: Z=3, σ=2×0.85=1.70, Z_eff=1.30
B: Z=5, σ=2×0.85+2×0.35=2.40, Z_eff=2.60
N: Z=7, σ=2×0.85+4×0.35=3.10, Z_eff=3.90
F: Z=9, σ=2×0.85+6×0.35=3.80, Z_eff=5.20
→ Z_eff increases → radius decreases across Period 2

Shielding Effectiveness Ranking

s electrons: BEST shielders (high probability near nucleus)
p electrons: Good shielders
d electrons: POOR shielders (diffuse orbitals, don't shield well)
f electrons: WORST shielders
→ d-block: adding d electrons → Z_eff increases rapidly → lanthanide contraction

Penetration Order

Within same shell: s > p > d > f
s electrons penetrate closest to nucleus → most strongly shielded AND best shielders.
f electrons penetrate least → poorest shielders → poor at protecting outer electrons from nuclear charge.

Z_eff vs Shielding: The Distinction

Shielding = how much inner electrons REDUCE nuclear charge felt by outer electrons.
Penetration = how close the electron itself gets to the nucleus.
High penetration (s) = electron is often near nucleus = it IS well-shielded (large σ from inner e⁻) AND it shields others well.

🚨 Cause-Based Exceptions — The "Why" Behind Every NEET Anomaly

IE₁ Exception: Be > B — Cause Analysis

Expected: IE₁ should increase B > Be (higher Z). Actual: Be(899) > B(800) kJ/mol.

Root Cause: Be has 2s² (full subshell). Fully-filled subshells have extra stability due to symmetrical charge distribution AND perfect pairing. B has a 2p¹ electron which (a) is at higher energy than 2s, (b) is better shielded by the full 2s² of Be, (c) the 2p orbital has a node at the nucleus (lower penetration than 2s). The 2p¹ electron of B is MORE easily removed than the 2s electron of Be.

Slater's confirmation: For B's 2p¹: σ = 2(1.00) + 2(0.85) + 2(0.35) = 4.40; Z_eff = 5 - 4.40? No — recalculate: inner electrons = 1s²(core) and 2s² (same shell). σ = 2×0.85 (1s² are n-1) + 2×0.35 (2s² are same shell) = 1.70 + 0.70 = 2.40; Z_eff(B,2p¹) = 5 - 2.40 = 2.60. For Be's 2s²: σ = 2×0.85(1s²) + 1×0.35(other 2s) = 1.70 + 0.35 = 2.05; Z_eff(Be,2s) = 4 - 2.05 = 1.95. The 2p orbital's higher energy more than compensates for the slightly higher Z_eff of B, making the 2p¹ electron easier to remove.

IE₁ Exception: N > O — Cause Analysis

Expected: IE₁ of O > N (higher Z). Actual: N(1402) > O(1314) kJ/mol.

Root Cause: N has half-filled 2p³ configuration. Each of the three 2p electrons occupies a DIFFERENT orbital (Hund's rule) → all electrons are unpaired → no electron-electron repulsion within the 2p subshell → each electron is in its own orbital in a stable half-filled arrangement with exchange energy stabilisation. O has 2p⁴ → one 2p orbital MUST have two electrons (pairing) → those two electrons in the same orbital repel each other strongly (electron-electron repulsion) → it is easier to remove one of this pair → lower IE₁ for O despite higher Z.

NEET rule: Whenever two consecutive elements are from the same period and the first has a half-filled or fully-filled subshell, expect the first to have higher IE₁ despite lower Z. Mg > Al (Mg: 3s², Al: 3p¹) and P > S (P: 3p³, S: 3p⁴) follow the SAME logic in Period 3.

EGE Exception: Cl > F — Cause Analysis

Expected: F (most electronegative) should have most negative EGE. Actual: EGE of Cl (−348 kJ/mol) is more negative than F (−328 kJ/mol).

Root Cause: F has a very small atomic radius (2p orbital is compact). When an extra electron tries to enter F's 2p subshell, the existing 7 electrons in a very small space create SEVERE electron-electron repulsion. This repulsion partially opposes the nuclear attraction, reducing the net energy gained. Cl is larger (3p subshell) → more diffuse → the incoming electron experiences less repulsion → greater net energy release → more negative EGE. This SAME reasoning applies to O having less negative EGE than S.

NEET 2020 Which of the following is the correct order of size of the given species? O, O⁻, O²⁻
Answer: O < O⁻ < O²⁻
Concept

Ionic radius in an isoelectronic series (all have same Z=8 here, but different electron counts). More electrons with same nucleus → more shielding of each other → larger radius.

O = 8 protons, 8 electrons. O⁻ = 8 protons, 9 electrons (one extra, more repulsion). O²⁻ = 8 protons, 10 electrons (two extra, maximum repulsion in this set).

O < O⁻ < O²⁻ — Size increases as electrons are added. Same nuclear charge cannot hold more electrons as tightly.

Reactions of alkali metals with water (NCERT):

Inert Pair Effect — Appears Every Year

Down Group 13: Tl⁺ is more stable than Tl³⁺ (Tl prefers +1). Down Group 14: Pb²⁺ more stable than Pb⁴⁺ (Pb prefers +2). Down Group 15: Bi³⁺ more stable than Bi⁵⁺. 🔷 Decision Rules — Groups 13 & 14 NEET Questions

Quick Decision Framework

Q: Is X a Lewis acid or Brønsted acid? → If the compound has an electron-deficient central atom (B, Al) with empty orbital: Lewis acid. Boric acid = Lewis acid (NOT Brønsted). AlCl₃ = Lewis acid.

📋 PYQ Solved — Group 15

NEET 2022 The basicity of H₃PO₃ is:
Answer: 2 (Dibasic)
Concept

Basicity of a phosphorus oxoacid = number of ionisable hydrogen atoms = number of P-OH groups. P-H bonds are NEVER ionisable and do NOT count towards basicity.

Rule

H₃PO₂: structure H₂P(=O)(OH) → 2 P-H bonds (non-ionisable) + 1 P-OH → monobasic. H₃PO₃: structure HP(=O)(OH)₂ → 1 P-H (non-ionisable) + 2 P-OH → dibasic. H₃PO₄: structure P(=O)(OH)₃ → 0 P-H + 3 P-OH → tribasic.

Application

H₃PO₃ has the structure where one H is directly bonded to P (not via O) → that H cannot be donated to a base → only the 2 OH groups can ionise → basicity = 2.

Answer

Dibasic (2) — Despite the formula suggesting 3 ionisable H (H₃), only 2 are actually ionisable. This is the signature NEET trap for phosphorus oxoacids.

NEET 2024 Among Group 16 elements, which one does NOT show −2 oxidation state?
Answer: Po (Polonium)
Concept

Group 16 elements typically gain 2 electrons to reach stable noble-gas configuration, showing −2 oxidation state. But the heaviest members (Po) have such large atomic radius and metallic character that gaining electrons is less favourable.

Rule

Down Group 16: metallic character increases. Po (Z=84) is a radioactive metalloid/metal. Metals show positive oxidation states preferentially. Po shows +2 and +4 as dominant states, not −2.

Application

O(−2 in H₂O), S(−2 in H₂S), Se(−2 in H₂Se), Te(−2 in H₂Te) — all show −2. Po: nuclear instability + metallic character + large size → does NOT show −2 under normal conditions.

Answer

Po (Polonium) — Largest Group 16 element, metallic character dominates, does not commonly exhibit −2 state. Also the only radioactive Group 16 element.

F₂ is strongest oxidising agent (due to weak F-F bond + high hydration enthalpy of F⁻, compensates for low electron affinity). Bond dissociation enthalpy: Cl₂ > Br₂ > F₂ > I₂ (F-F is anomalously weak due to lone pair repulsion). Electronegativity: F > Cl > Br > I. Acidic strength of HX: HF << HCl < HBr < HI.

F₂ Anomalies — NEET Repeatedly Asks These

1. F₂ does NOT show positive oxidation states (no d orbitals, most electronegative). 2. HF is a weak acid (strong H-F bond, F⁻ is small → tightly held). HCl, HBr, HI are all strong acids. 3. F₂ reacts with cold NaOH to give NaF + O₂ (not NaFO as expected). 4. Bond energy of F₂ (158 kJ/mol) < Cl₂ (244) < Br₂ (193) < I₂ (151). Actually order: Cl₂ > Br₂ > F₂ > I₂ — F₂ is anomalously weak. 5. Fluorine cannot be obtained by chemical oxidation (only electrolysis). 6. F₂ reacts with water: 2F₂ + 2H₂O → 4HF + O₂ (F₂ is so reactive it oxidises water). 7. Boiling points: F₂(−188°C) < Cl₂(−34°C) < Br₂(59°C) < I₂(184°C) — increases down group (increasing van der Waals forces).

Geometrical Isomerism — When Does it Exist?

Square planar [MA₂B₂] type: cis and trans (2 isomers). Square planar [MABCD]: 3 isomers. Octahedral [MA₄B₂]: cis (B trans to A) and trans (B trans to B). Optical Isomerism — When Does it Exist?

For a complex to show optical isomerism, it must be non-superimposable on its mirror image (chiral). Common NEET cases: [Co(en)₃]³⁺: octahedral tris-chelate → always chiral → 2 optical isomers (d and l). cis-[CoCl₂(en)₂]⁺: chiral → 2 optical isomers. trans-[CoCl₂(en)₂]⁺: has plane of symmetry → NOT chiral (only 1 isomer). Square planar complexes: generally NOT optically active (usually have plane of symmetry in the plane of the complex).

Crystal Field Theory (CFT) — Highest Tested Topic in Coordination

CFT — Core Concepts

Octahedral Field: d orbitals split into two levels. Higher energy: e_g (d_z² and d_x²-y²) — point towards ligands. Lower energy: t_2g (d_xy, d_xz, d_yz) — point between ligands. Energy gap = Δ_o (10Dq). t_2g is stabilised by −0.4Δ_o per e⁻, e_g destabilised by +0.6Δ_o per e⁻. Tetrahedral Field: Splitting = Δ_t = (4/9)Δ_o (smaller). t_2 > e (reversed order — no subscript g in tetrahedral, no centre of symmetry). Tetrahedral complexes are almost always high-spin (Δ_t small).

Spectrochemical Series — Ligand Field Strength (Must Memorise)

Increasing field strength (Δ_o increases →):

I⁻ < Br⁻ < S²⁻ < SCN⁻ < Cl⁻ < NO₃⁻ < F⁻ < OH⁻ < C₂O₄²⁻ < H₂O < NCS⁻ < edta < NH₃ < en < bipy < phen < NO₂⁻ < CN⁻ < CO

Weak field ligands (I⁻ to F⁻): cause high-spin complexes, smaller Δ_o. Strong field ligands (CN⁻, CO, en, NH₃): cause low-spin complexes, larger Δ_o, more d-d absorption → more intense colour. Rule: strong field → low spin → fewer unpaired → diamagnetic or low paramagnetism. Weak field → high spin → more unpaired → more paramagnetic.

EAN Rule (18-Electron Rule) — Effective Atomic Number

EAN = atomic number of metal − charge on metal ion + electrons donated by ligands (2 per ligand for 2-electron donors, 1 per NO in some). Stable complexes often have EAN = 36 (Kr), 54 (Xe), or 86 (Rn). Metal carbonyls obey EAN rule. Examples: [Ni(CO)₄]: Ni (Z=28), OS=0, Ni⁰ = [Ar]3d⁸4s² → with CO pairing occurs → effectively 3d¹⁰ (10 electrons on Ni), CO donates 2e⁻ each × 4 = 8. EAN = 28 + 8 = 36 (Kr) ✓. [Fe(CO)₅]: Fe (Z=26), OS=0, Fe⁰ = [Ar]3d⁶4s² — Fe⁰ has 8 valence electrons (6 in 3d + 2 in 4s), NOT d¹⁰. 5 CO ligands donate 5×2 = 10 electrons. EAN = 26 + 10 = 36 ✓. [Cr(CO)₆]: Cr (Z=24), OS=0, Cr⁰ has 6 valence electrons, 6×2=12. EAN = 24+12 = 36 ✓. Stable!

Werner's theory (NCERT): Werner postulated: (1) Metals show two types of valency — primary (ionisable) and secondary (non-ionisable = coordination). (2) Primary valency = oxidation state (satisfied by negative ions). (3) Secondary valency = coordination number (satisfied by ligands, directed in space). This theory explained isomerism before modern quantum mechanics.

Crystal Field Theory — CFSE values (NCERT): In octahedral field: d orbitals split into t₂g (lower, −0.4Δₒ each) and eₒ (higher, +0.6Δₒ each). CFSE = −0.4Δₒ × (no. of e⁻ in t₂g) + 0.6Δₒ × (no. of e⁻ in eₒ). For Co³⁺ (d⁶) with strong field: [t₂g⁶ eₒ⁰] → CFSE = 6×(−0.4Δₒ) = −2.4Δₒ (maximum CFSE → very stable). For Fe³⁺ (d⁵) high spin: [t₂g³ eₒ²] → CFSE = 3×(−0.4) + 2×(+0.6) = −1.2+1.2 = 0 (no CFSE — explains why [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ is labile).

Stability of complexes — chelate effect (NCERT): "Chelate complexes are more stable than non-chelate complexes of similar composition. This extra stability is due to the chelate effect — formation of multiple rings increases entropy (ΔS > 0) making ΔG more negative." [Co(en)₃]³⁺ is MUCH more stable than [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ (same donor atoms N, but en chelates). NEET asks reason for chelate stability = entropy-driven (thermodynamic factor).

Important complexes and their applications (NCERT): cis-[PtCl₂(NH₃)₂] = Cisplatin → anticancer drug (binds to guanine residues in DNA, prevents replication). Important Metallurgical Processes — All NEET Tested

Blast Furnace (Fe): Temperature zones: slag forming zone (600–900°C), reduction zone (900–1500°C), combustion zone (1500–1900°C). Reactions: C + O₂ → CO₂ → 2CO (reducing agent). Thermodynamics of Extraction — Ellingham Diagram

Ellingham Diagram — Key NEET Points

Plots ΔGf° for metal oxide formation (MO) vs temperature. More negative ΔGf° = oxide more stable. Reduction occurs when reducer's oxide has MORE NEGATIVE ΔGf° (reducer's line is BELOW metal oxide line). Carbon (C) can reduce metal oxides where C/CO line is below MO line. At high temperatures (>700°C), CO becomes better reducer than C. At very high T (>1700°C), C is better than CO. Key fact: Al can reduce Cr₂O₃ (thermite reaction: Al + Cr₂O₃ → Al₂O₃ + Cr) because Al₂O₃ line is always below Cr₂O₃ line. Al₂O₃ itself cannot be reduced by C (too stable). ZnO can be reduced by C (moderate temperature).

At anode: 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻ (O₂ reacts with carbon anode → CO₂, so anodes burn away gradually). Cryolite functions: (a) lowers melting point of Al₂O₃ from 2323K to ~1270K (b) increases electrical conductivity of the melt.

Bayer process for Al₂O₃ (NCERT): Bauxite (Al₂O₃·2H₂O) + NaOH → NaAlO₂ + H₂O (dissolves Al₂O₃, leaving Fe₂O₃ "red mud"). NaAlO₂ + H₂O → Al(OH)₃↓ + NaOH (dilution and seeding with Al(OH)₃ crystals). Al(OH)₃ → Al₂O₃ + H₂O (calcination). The Fe₂O₃ remains as insoluble residue (red mud) — separated by filtration.

Van Arkel method (NCERT exact): Ti + 2I₂ → TiI₄ (at ~500K, volatile titanium tetraiodide forms). TiI₄ → Ti + 2I₂ (on a hot tungsten filament at ~1700K). I₂ is recycled. This gives ultra-pure Ti. Also used for Zr, Hf. The method is called "iodide process" or "Van Arkel-de Boer process."

Ellingham diagram NEET decision rules (NCERT): If reducer's oxide line is BELOW the metal oxide line → reduction occurs. C can reduce metal oxides where the C→CO line crosses below the MO line. C→CO line has negative slope (ΔG decreases with T because ΔS positive — more moles of gas produced). At ~983 K: C/CO line crosses Ni line → C can reduce NiO above 983 K. At very high T (>1200K): C reduces all oxides except Al₂O₃, CaO, MgO. Al can reduce any oxide whose line is ABOVE Al₂O₃ line (thermite: Al + Cr₂O₃, Al + Fe₂O₃, Al + MnO₂). Al₂O₃ itself: only electrolysis can reduce it.

Zone refining principle (NCERT): "Impurities are more soluble in the melt than in the solid state. As the molten zone moves across the impure metal bar, impurities are swept to one end which is then discarded." Used for: Si, Ge, Ga, In, B — semiconductor-grade purification. Also: crystalline B (99.9999% pure by zone refining).

📚 NCERT Key Lines — Environmental Chemistry (Class 11, Ch. 14)

NCERT definition of pollutant: "A substance which is harmful to the environment or human health when present in concentrations greater than the natural level is called a pollutant." Primary pollutants are produced directly from a source; secondary pollutants arise from reactions between primary pollutants (or with natural components).

Tropospheric pollution (NCERT exact): "Gaseous air pollutants include oxides of sulphur, nitrogen and carbon, hydrogen sulphide, hydrocarbons, ozone and other oxidants." SO₂ from burning coal/fuel → H₂SO₃/H₂SO₄ in clouds → acid rain (pH < 5.6). NOₓ (mainly NO, NO₂) from combustion processes. CO is produced by incomplete combustion.

CO toxicity mechanism (NCERT): "CO combines with haemoglobin to form carboxyhaemoglobin (carboxyHb) which is about 300 times more stable than oxyhaemoglobin. Therefore blood loses its ability to carry oxygen to different parts of the body." CO₂ competes with O₂ for same site on Hb. Safe CO level in air: <10 ppm.

Smog types (NCERT): Classical smog (London type): cold humid climate + fog + SO₂ + particulates → reducing atmosphere. Photochemical smog (Los Angeles type): warm dry sunny climate + NOₓ + hydrocarbons → ultraviolet light → O₃ + PAN + aldehydes → oxidising atmosphere. "Photochemical smog has high concentrations of oxidising agents and is called oxidising smog."

Stratospheric pollution — ozone hole (NCERT): "CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) like CCl₂F₂ (Freon) are used as refrigerants. In stratosphere, UV radiation decomposes CFCs to give Cl atoms: CCl₂F₂ → ·CClF₂ + Cl·. Cl· + O₃ → ClO· + O₂. ClO· + O· → Cl· + O₂. Net: O₃ + O· → 2O₂. One Cl atom can destroy ~100,000 molecules of O₃." Annual ozone hole over Antarctica: occurs September–October, caused by ice crystals on polar stratospheric clouds (PSCs) that concentrate Cl species.

Water pollution — NCERT key standards: WHO permissible limits: Total dissolved solids (TDS) < 500 mg/L; fluoride < 1.5 mg/L; nitrate < 45 mg/L; lead < 0.01 mg/L; arsenic < 0.01 mg/L. Eutrophication: excess nutrients (N, P from fertilisers and detergents) → algal bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose → consume DO → fish die. "Eutrophication causes oxygen depletion in water bodies."

Soil pollution (NCERT): Pesticides (DDT, BHC) are persistent organic pollutants (POPs). They undergo biomagnification — concentration increases at each trophic level. DDT concentration: phytoplankton → zooplankton → small fish → large fish → birds (up to 10⁶ times). NCERT: "DDT is non-biodegradable and accumulates in fatty tissues." At high concentrations: reproductive failure in birds (thin eggshells), neurological damage in humans.

Green chemistry (NCERT): "Green chemistry is a way of thinking and practising chemistry in such a manner that helps in preventing pollution." 12 principles — NEET may ask: which is NOT a green chemistry principle? Atom economy (use maximum atoms of reactants in product), use of renewable feedstocks, avoid hazardous reagents, design chemicals that degrade after use.

NEET consistently asks 1–2 questions from salt analysis every year. All facts are directly from NCERT Class 12 Appendix VII and Lab Manual. Nessler's reagent for NH₄⁺ is asked almost every year.

👉 What NEET asks: Which reagent detects NH₄⁺ (Nessler's — brown ppt). Which test confirms NO₃⁻ (brown ring test). Which cation group is precipitated by H₂S in acidic medium (Group II). Colour of AgCl, AgBr, AgI precipitates. Which anion gives white ppt insoluble in HCl with BaCl₂ (SO₄²⁻). Colour of Fe(OH)₃ (rust/reddish brown) vs Al(OH)₃ (white gelatinous). Which gas turns lead acetate paper black (H₂S from S²⁻). Chromyl chloride test details. Brown ring formula.

Nessler's reagent gives brown ppt (NOT yellow). NH₄⁺ does NOT belong to any standard group I–VI — it must be tested first (Group 0) before destroying it with boiling. Chromyl chloride test is for Cl⁻ (NOT Br⁻ or I⁻ — these do NOT give chromyl chloride).
⚡ Fast solving rule: For cation identification: match the GROUP REAGENT used. Group I = HCl (white ppts). Group II = H₂S + dilute HCl (black/yellow ppts of CuS, PbS, HgS). Group III = NH₃ + NH₄Cl (white/rust hydroxide ppts). Group IV = H₂S in NH₃ (white ZnS). Group V = (NH₄)₂CO₃ (white carbonate ppts). For anions: systematic elimination — volatile acid → CO₃²⁻, SO₃²⁻, S²⁻. Non-volatile anion tests follow.

SO₄²⁻ Test — BaCl₂ + HCl

White ppt with BaCl₂ + insoluble in dilute HCl = SO₄²⁻. BaSO₃ (from SO₃²⁻) also gives white ppt with BaCl₂ but dissolves in HCl. This solubility in HCl is the key distinction. Add HCl after BaCl₂ ppt forms — if ppt persists = SO₄²⁻, if dissolves = SO₃²⁻.

NO₃⁻ — Brown Ring Test (NEET Very High Frequency)

Reagent: Fresh FeSO₄ solution + conc. H₂SO₄ (layered carefully below aqueous layer).

Result: Brown ring at the interface of the two liquids.

Chemistry: NO₃⁻ + H₂SO₄ → NO gas. NO + Fe²⁺ → [Fe(H₂O)₅NO]²⁺ (brown nitroso complex).

Brown ring forms at the INTERFACE (bottom of aqueous layer / top of H₂SO₄ layer) — NOT at the top of the tube.

Must use FRESH FeSO₄ — old FeSO₄ is oxidised to Fe³⁺ which does not form the complex.

Brown colour disappears on heating (complex decomposes).

Br⁻ and I⁻ interfere — remove with AgNO₃ before doing this test.

⚠ Brown Ring Test — 4 NEET Traps

1. Brown colour is due to [Fe(H₂O)₅NO]²⁺ — NOT NO₂. 2. Ring forms at the INTERFACE, not at the top. 3. FRESH FeSO₄ required — old FeSO₄ has Fe³⁺ → no complex. 4. Conc. H₂SO₄ required — dilute will not work.

Cl⁻ — Chromyl Chloride Test (NEET Direct)

Reagents: Solid chloride salt + K₂Cr₂O₇ + conc. H₂SO₄ → orange-red fumes of CrO₂Cl₂ (chromyl chloride).

Confirmation: Pass fumes into NaOH → yellow Na₂CrO₄. Add acetic acid + Pb(CH₃COO)₂ → yellow PbCrO₄ ppt = confirms Cl⁻.

CRITICAL: Chromyl chloride test is SPECIFIC to Cl⁻. Br⁻ and I⁻ do NOT give CrO₂Cl₂.

NEET 2024 Nessler's reagent is used for detection of which ion? (A) Fe³⁺ (B) Cu²⁺ (C) NH₄⁺ (D) Pb²⁺
Answer: (C) NH₄⁺
Concept

Nessler's reagent (K₂[HgI₄] in KOH) detects NH₄⁺ specifically. It is the Group 0 test done before all other groups because NH₄⁺ is destroyed by subsequent boiling with NaOH.

Rule

NH₄⁺ + 2[HgI₄]²⁻ + 4OH⁻ → brown ppt (Millon's base iodide) + 7I⁻ + 3H₂O. The ppt is brown — NOT yellow. Fe³⁺ → KSCN (blood red). Cu²⁺ → excess NH₄OH (deep blue). Pb²⁺ → K₂CrO₄ (yellow PbCrO₄).

Answer

(C) NH₄⁺ — Nessler's reagent exclusively detects NH₄⁺, giving brown ppt. Asked almost every NEET cycle.

NEET 2023 In the brown ring test for nitrate, the brown ring is due to formation of: (A) [Fe(H₂O)₅(NO)]²⁺ (B) FeSO₄·3H₂O (C) Fe₂(SO₄)₃ (D) Fe(NO₃)₂
Answer: (A) [Fe(H₂O)₅(NO)]²⁺
Concept

NO₃⁻ is reduced to NO gas by the acid. NO coordinates to Fe²⁺ forming the brown nitroso complex [Fe(H₂O)₅NO]²⁺ at the liquid-liquid interface. The complex is a coordination compound of Fe²⁺ with NO as ligand.

Rule

Formula = [Fe(H₂O)₅NO]²⁺. CN of Fe = 6 (5 H₂O + 1 NO). Brown ring at INTERFACE, disappears on heating. Must use fresh FeSO₄ (Fe²⁺ needed, not Fe³⁺).

Answer

(A) [Fe(H₂O)₅(NO)]²⁺ — asked multiple times. Know the formula, know it forms at the interface, know it is NOT due to NO₂.

NEET 2022 A white ppt forms with NH₄OH and dissolves in excess NaOH. The cation is: (A) Al³⁺ (B) Fe³⁺ (C) Ca²⁺ (D) Mg²⁺
Answer: (A) Al³⁺
Concept

Amphoteric hydroxides dissolve in excess NaOH. Al(OH)₃ is amphoteric → dissolves giving [Al(OH)₄]⁻ (aluminate). Fe(OH)₃ is reddish-brown (not white) and NOT amphoteric. Mg(OH)₂ is white but NOT amphoteric — doesn't dissolve in NaOH. Ca(OH)₂ is sparingly soluble, does not dissolve in NaOH.

Rule

White ppt + dissolves in excess NaOH = amphoteric hydroxide = Al³⁺ (or Zn²⁺ or Pb²⁺). Fe³⁺ eliminated (rust-brown ppt). Mg²⁺ eliminated (not amphoteric). Ca²⁺ eliminated (not dissolved by NaOH).

Answer

(A) Al³⁺ — Al(OH)₃ is white and amphoteric. This pattern (white ppt + dissolves in NaOH) = Al³⁺ or Zn²⁺.

NEET 2021 Which silver halide is insoluble in NH₄OH? (A) AgCl (B) AgBr (C) AgI (D) AgF
Answer: (C) AgI
Rule

AgCl: soluble in dilute NH₄OH. AgBr: slightly soluble in conc. NH₄OH. AgI: INSOLUBLE in NH₄OH. AgF: water-soluble (no ppt at all). Solubility in NH₄OH decreases Cl > Br >> I.

Answer

(C) AgI — yellow precipitate, insoluble in NH₄OH. Dissolves in Na₂S₂O₃ (thiosulphate). Classic NEET distinction between silver halides.

NEET 2020 Which confirmatory test distinguishes Fe³⁺ from Fe²⁺? (A) NaOH (B) KSCN (C) BaCl₂ (D) Flame test
Answer: (B) KSCN

Answer

(B) KSCN — blood red complex ONLY with Fe³⁺. Most sensitive and specific test for Fe³⁺ in NEET.

NEET 2019 The chromyl chloride test confirms the presence of: (A) Cl⁻ (B) Br⁻ (C) I⁻ (D) F⁻
Answer: (A) Cl⁻

Answer

(A) Cl⁻ — CrO₂Cl₂ forms ONLY with Cl⁻, not Br⁻/I⁻/F⁻. This is a NEET classic.

NEET 2018 Which of the following does NOT give a ppt with BaCl₂? (A) SO₄²⁻ (B) SO₃²⁻ (C) CO₃²⁻ (D) Cl⁻

Answer

(D) Cl⁻ — Ba²⁺ + 2Cl⁻ → BaCl₂ (soluble). Cl⁻ is detected by AgNO₃ or chromyl chloride test, not BaCl₂.

NEET 2017 Which reagent gives bright pink/red precipitate with Ni²⁺? (A) Dimethylglyoxime (B) Alizarin (C) Nessler's (D) KSCN

Rule

Answer

(A) DMG — dimethylglyoxime gives striking bright pink ppt with Ni²⁺. This is the Group IV confirmatory test for Ni²⁺.
