Organism & Its Environment — Abiotic Factors, Adaptations & Responses

Levels of Ecological Organisation NCERT Exact

  • Organism → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biome → Biosphere
  • Ecology (Ernst Haeckel, 1869): study of interactions between organisms and their environment
  • Habitat: place where organism lives (physical locale)
  • Niche: functional role of organism in ecosystem — what it DOES, not where it lives
  • Competitive exclusion (Gause's law): two species with identical niches CANNOT coexist stably. One outcompetes and eliminates the other.

Major Abiotic Factors Foundation

Temperature
  • Most important abiotic factor
  • Affects enzyme activity, metabolism
  • Mean annual temp: tropics 25–30°C; poles below 0°C
  • Eurythermal: wide temp range — most mammals
  • Stenothermal: narrow range — many fish, coral
Water
  • Availability determines biome type
  • Euryhaline: wide salinity tolerance (salmon)
  • Stenohaline: narrow range (goldfish)
  • Quality, pH, dissolved O₂ also critical
Light
  • Photoperiod controls flowering, breeding
  • Deep ocean: no light (aphotic zone)
  • UV — harmful; filtered by ozone
  • Phytoplankton: only surface water
Soil
  • pH, texture, mineral composition
  • Pedalfers: humid regions; leached
  • Pedocals: arid; rich in CaCO₃
  • Determines plant community type

Adaptations — Temperature & Water 2 Qs/yr

Allen's Rule & Bergmann's Rule
  • Bergmann's Rule: Warm-blooded animals in colder climates tend to be LARGER (greater volume:surface ratio = less heat loss). E.g., polar bears > tropical bears.
  • Allen's Rule: Animals in cold climates have SHORTER appendages (ears, limbs, tail) to reduce heat loss. E.g., Arctic fox = small ears; desert fox = large ears.
Thermoregulation Mechanisms
  • Ectotherms (poikilotherms): body temp varies with environment — reptiles, fish, amphibia. Use behavioural strategies (basking).
  • Endotherms (homeotherms): generate own heat — birds and mammals. Use metabolic heat + insulation (fur, feathers, fat).
  • Hibernation (winter dormancy): bears, ground squirrels. Metabolic rate drops dramatically. Body temp may fall.
  • Aestivation (summer dormancy): lungfish in summer. Protects from heat and drying.
  • Torpor: Short-term metabolic depression. Hummingbirds at night.
NEET 2022 Organisms which can tolerate a wide range of salinities are called: (A) Stenohaline (B) Euryhaline (C) Osmotic conformers (D) Halophytes
Answer: (B) Euryhaline

Euryhaline = wide salinity range tolerance (eury = broad). Examples: salmon, tilapia. Stenohaline = narrow range (steno = narrow) — goldfish, tuna. Halophytes = salt-tolerant PLANTS (not animals). Osmotic conformers adjust their internal osmotic concentration to match external.

Population Ecology — Growth Models, Attributes & Interactions

Population Attributes NCERT Essential

  • Population: Group of individuals of SAME species in same area at same time; capable of interbreeding
  • Birth rate (Natality): New individuals added per capita per unit time
  • Death rate (Mortality): Individuals lost per capita per unit time
  • Sex ratio: Ratio of males to females in population
  • Age pyramid (Age structure): Proportion of individuals in different age groups — pre-reproductive, reproductive, post-reproductive
  • Expanding pyramid = young individuals dominate → rapid growth. Stable = even distribution. Declining = old individuals dominate.

Population Growth Models Every NEET

Exponential (Geometric) Growth — "J-shaped curve"

Occurs when resources are unlimited. Population grows at constant rate r (intrinsic rate of natural increase).

dN/dt = rN where N = population size, r = birth rate (b) − death rate (d)

Nt = N₀e^(rt) — exponential growth formula

J-shaped curve. No carrying capacity. Bacterial growth in ideal conditions. Never truly sustained in nature.

Logistic Growth — "S-shaped (Sigmoid) curve"

More realistic. Growth slows as population approaches carrying capacity (K) — maximum population an environment can sustainably support.

dN/dt = rN[(K−N)/K]

When N is small → (K−N)/K ≈ 1 → grows nearly exponentially. When N → K → (K−N)/K → 0 → growth stops.

S-shaped (sigmoidal) curve. Inflection point at N = K/2 (maximum growth rate).

Verhulst-Pearl logistic growth equation. Realistic growth model in nature.

NEET Trap: "J-curve = logistic" → WRONG. J-curve = EXPONENTIAL (unlimited resources). S-curve = LOGISTIC (with K). Examiner swaps these — be alert.
NEET 2024 In logistic population growth, the maximum growth rate occurs when population size is: (A) Equal to K (B) Equal to K/2 (C) Less than K/4 (D) Equal to zero
Answer: (B) K/2

In logistic growth dN/dt = rN(K−N)/K. Taking d/dN of this = 0 to find maximum → N = K/2. At this point, the sigmoid curve has its steepest slope (inflection point). This is the maximum sustainable yield concept — fishing/harvesting at K/2 gives maximum yield while maintaining population.

Predation — Ecological Role & Adaptations

  • Predators keep prey populations in check → prevent overgrazing → maintain plant diversity
  • Prey defences: Cryptic colouration (camouflage); Mimicry; Aposematism (warning colouration — poison dart frog, monarch butterfly); Thorns; Chemicals (milkweed cardiac glycosides)
  • Batesian mimicry: Harmless species mimics harmful/toxic species (monarch vs viceroy butterfly)
  • Müllerian mimicry: Two or more harmful species resemble each other (mutual benefit — all gain protection)
NEET 2023 Mycorrhiza is an example of: (A) Commensalism (B) Parasitism (C) Mutualism (D) Competition
Answer: (C) Mutualism

Mycorrhiza = symbiotic association between fungus and plant roots. BOTH benefit (+,+) = mutualism. Fungus benefits: gets photosynthate (sugar) from plant. Plant benefits: hugely expanded absorptive surface for water and minerals (especially phosphorus). Nearly 90% of plant species have mycorrhizal associations. NOT commensalism (that's +,0).

Coevolution — Arms Races Conceptual

  • Coevolution: Reciprocal evolutionary changes in interacting species
  • Classic: fig tree and fig wasp — obligate mutualism. Fig wasp pollinates fig; fig provides nursery for wasp larvae. ONLY fig wasps pollinate figs.
  • Orchids + pollinators: Mediterranean orchid (Ophrys) mimics female bee body → male bee attempts to mate → picks up pollen. Pseudocopulation.
  • Prey-predator coevolution: faster cheetah → faster gazelle → faster cheetah (Red Queen hypothesis)

Community Ecology — Succession, Zonation & Ecological Characteristics

Ecological Succession 2–3 Qs/yr

Succession — Definition

The sequential, directional, non-seasonal change in community composition over time in an area, ultimately leading to a stable climax community. Driven by species modifying the environment.

FeaturePrimary SuccessionSecondary Succession
Starting pointBare, uninhabited area with no/little soil — no previous communityArea with pre-existing soil where community was disturbed/destroyed
Pioneer speciesLichens (on bare rocks); phytoplankton in aquaticGrasses, weeds — hardy r-strategists
Time requiredVery slow — thousands of yearsFaster — decades to centuries (soil already present)
ExamplesBare rock → lichen → moss → grass → shrub → forest. Newly cooled lava; glacial moraineAbandoned farmland; burnt forest; flooded area drained
Climax communitySame as secondary — final stable state determined by climateSame climax as primary for that region

Hydrarch vs Xerarch Succession

Hydrarch (Hydrosere)
  • Begins in water (pond/lake)
  • Gradually fills up with sediment
  • Phytoplankton → rooted hydrophytes (Vallisneria) → reed swamp → shrub → climax forest
  • Direction: hydric → mesic (towards mesophytic)
Xerarch (Xerosere)
  • Begins on bare rock (dry area)
  • Soil gradually builds up
  • Lichen → moss → grass → shrub → climax forest
  • Direction: xeric → mesic
Key Rule — Succession Direction

Both hydrarch (wet start) and xerarch (dry start) succession move towards a mesic (moderate moisture) climax community. The end point (climax) is determined by the region's climate, NOT the starting conditions.

NEET 2022 The pioneer community in bare rock ecological succession is: (A) Mosses (B) Lichens (C) Grasses (D) Ferns
Answer: (B) Lichens

On bare rock, lichens are the pioneer species. Lichens secrete acids that weather the rock, creating cracks and accumulating debris → first thin soil. Then mosses can establish. On bare rock, mosses cannot establish first (need some substrate). In hydrarch succession, phytoplankton are pioneers. In secondary succession, grasses may be first.

Species Interactions in Communities Conceptual

Keystone Species

  • Species that has disproportionately large effect on ecosystem relative to its abundance
  • Removal causes dramatic restructuring of community (trophic cascade)
  • Sea otter → eats sea urchins → prevents urchins from destroying kelp forest. Remove otter → urchin explosion → kelp destruction → collapse of ecosystem
  • Fig trees in forests: fruit all year → keystone food source for hundreds of fruit-eating species
  • Wolf reintroduction, Yellowstone: wolves → reduce elk overgrazing → return of willows → return of beavers → engineered streams → more biodiversity (trophic cascade)

Ecosystem Structure & Components NCERT Core

Abiotic Components
  • Inorganic substances (C, N, CO₂, H₂O)
  • Organic compounds (proteins, lipids — link biotic & abiotic)
  • Climatic regime (temperature, light, humidity)
Biotic — Producers
  • Autotrophs (plants, phytoplankton, cyanobacteria)
  • Fix inorganic C → organic via photosynthesis
  • Base of all food chains
Biotic — Consumers
  • Herbivores = primary consumers (P₁)
  • Carnivores = secondary (P₂), tertiary (P₃)
  • Omnivores = multiple levels
Biotic — Decomposers
  • Fungi, bacteria (saprotrophs)
  • Break dead organic matter → inorganic
  • Release nutrients back → soil
  • Also called detritivores (for fragmentation)

Productivity Values — NCERT Exact (memorise)
  • Tropical rainforest: NPP ≈ 2,000 g dry wt/m²/yr — HIGHEST terrestrial
  • Estuaries: NPP ≈ 1,500 g dry wt/m²/yr — HIGHEST aquatic (often stated as most productive aquatic)
  • Open ocean: NPP ≈ 125 g dry wt/m²/yr — LOWEST (but huge area)
  • Desert: NPP ≈ 3 g dry wt/m²/yr — LOWEST terrestrial
  • Total global NPP ≈ 170 × 10⁹ tonnes/yr (Land: 115, Ocean: 55)

Energy Flow — 10% Law Every NEET

Lindeman's 10% Law (1942)

Only 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. The remaining 90% is lost as heat (respiration) + excretion + not consumed. Therefore food chains are short (4–5 trophic levels max — energy runs out).

Example: If producers have 1,000,000 J → Herbivores receive 100,000 J → Primary carnivores receive 10,000 J → Secondary carnivores receive 1,000 J

NEET Calculation Trap: If primary producers = 10,000 cal and top carnivore is at 4th trophic level → energy available = 10,000 × (10/100)³ = 10,000 × 0.001 = 10 cal. COUNT TRANSFERS, not trophic levels. Between T₁ and T₄ = 3 transfers = (0.1)³ multiplier.

Ecological Pyramids Comparison

NEET 2023 Which pyramid can never be inverted? (A) Pyramid of number (B) Pyramid of biomass (C) Pyramid of energy (D) Both A and B
Answer: (C) Pyramid of energy

Pyramid of energy is ALWAYS upright because energy is lost (as heat) at each trophic level due to the 2nd Law of Thermodynamics. You cannot have more energy at a higher trophic level than at the level below. Pyramid of numbers CAN be inverted (tree-insect-bird). Pyramid of biomass CAN be inverted (aquatic system).

Food Chains — Grazing vs Detritus NCERT

Grazing Food Chain (GFC)
  • Starts with green plants (living)
  • Plant → Herbivore → Carnivore
  • Grass → Deer → Lion
  • Phytoplankton → Zooplankton → Fish
  • In terrestrial: only 10% energy flows this way
Detritus Food Chain (DFC)
  • Starts with dead organic matter (detritus)
  • Detritus → Decomposers → Detritivores
  • In terrestrial: 90% energy flows this way
  • In aquatic: GFC dominates
  • Detritivores: earthworm, millipedes, woodlice

Biogeochemical Cycles — Nutrient Cycling 2 Qs/yr

Carbon Cycle — Key Points

  • Carbon reservoir: Atmosphere (CO₂), Oceans (dissolved CO₂), Biomass (organic C), Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas), Limestone (CaCO₃)
  • Entry into biotic: Photosynthesis (CO₂ → organic matter)
  • Return to atmosphere: Respiration, decomposition, combustion, volcanic eruption
  • Human impact: Burning fossil fuels adds ~7 billion tonnes C/yr → rising atmospheric CO₂ → global warming
  • Ocean = largest carbon sink (dissolved CO₂ + marine organisms → CaCO₃ in shells)
CO₂ in atmosphere→ Photosynthesis → Organic carbon in plants→ Eaten → Organic C in consumers→ Respiration/Decomposition → CO₂ back to atmosphere

Phosphorus Cycle — Sedimentary (No Gaseous Phase)

  • Phosphorus has no gaseous phase — does NOT cycle through atmosphere (unlike C, N)
  • Reservoir: Rocks (phosphate minerals), Soil, Water bodies
  • Weathering of rocks → phosphate ions → absorbed by plants → animals → decomposers → soil
  • Phosphorus is often the limiting nutrient in freshwater lakes (eutrophication trigger)
  • NEET trap: Phosphorus cycle = sedimentary cycle (gaseous cycles = C, N, S, H₂O)

Nitrogen Cycle — Complete

  • N₂ = 78% of atmosphere but most organisms cannot use it directly (triple bond too strong)
  • Nitrogen Fixation: N₂ → NH₃/NH₄⁺ — by Rhizobium (in root nodules), Azotobacter (free-living), Cyanobacteria (Anabaena), lightning
  • Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ — by Nitrosomonas (NH₄⁺→NO₂⁻) then Nitrobacter (NO₂⁻→NO₃⁻)
  • Assimilation: Plants absorb NO₃⁻ → organic N (amino acids, nucleotides)
  • Ammonification: Dead organic N → NH₄⁺ — by decomposer bacteria and fungi
  • Denitrification: NO₃⁻ → N₂ — by Thiobacillus denitrificans (converts nitrates back to N₂ — removes N from ecosystem)
NEET 2024 Which of the following bacteria converts ammonia into nitrite? (A) Nitrobacter (B) Nitrosomonas (C) Thiobacillus (D) Azotobacter
Answer: (B) Nitrosomonas

Nitrification has two steps: (1) Nitrosomonas: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻. (2) Nitrobacter: NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻. Together they convert ammonia → nitrate making N available to plants. Thiobacillus = denitrification (NO₃⁻ → N₂). Azotobacter = free-living nitrogen fixation (N₂ → NH₃).

Decomposition — Steps & Factors Marks Guaranteed

Steps of Decomposition (FCLHS)

Fragmentation → Catabolism (leaching) → Humification → Mineralisation

  • Fragmentation: Detritivores (earthworm, millipede) break large pieces → smaller
  • Leaching: Water-soluble nutrients leach into soil (absorbed by decomposers)
  • Catabolism: Bacteria/fungi enzymatically degrade detritus → simpler inorganic substances
  • Humification: Formation of humus (dark, amorphous, organic substance). Humus is resistant to decomposition — long-term C storage.
  • Mineralisation: Humus further broken down → release of inorganic nutrients (N, P, K) → available to plants again
Rate of Decomposition — Factors: WARM + MOIST + ADEQUATE O₂ = fast decomposition. Cold, waterlogged (anaerobic) conditions → slow → peat formation. High C:N ratio (e.g. woody material) → slow. Low C:N ratio (fresh leaves) → fast.

Levels of Biodiversity Definition First

  • Genetic Diversity: Variation in genes within a species. E.g., India has 50,000 varieties of rice; 1,000+ mango varieties. Enables adaptation, evolution. Reduced by inbreeding.
  • Species Diversity: Variety of species in an area. Measured by species richness (number of species) and species evenness (relative abundance).
  • Ecological (Community) Diversity: Variety of ecosystems, habitats, and ecological processes in a region. E.g., India has deserts, rainforests, mangroves, coral reefs, wetlands.

Biodiversity Hotspots Exam Critical

Hotspot Criteria (Myers, 1988)

An area is a hotspot if: (1) ≥1500 species of vascular plants are endemic AND (2) has lost ≥70% of its original habitat. Currently 34 biodiversity hotspots worldwide (originally 25 by Myers; later revised).

India's 4 Biodiversity Hotspots
  • 1. Himalayas — includes entire Hindu Kush-Himalayan region. Rich in medicinal plants, snow leopard, red panda.
  • 2. Western Ghats & Sri Lanka — India's most biodiverse region. Rich in endemic amphibians (60% endemic), reptiles, flowering plants. Lion-tailed macaque, purple frog, Nilgiri tahr.
  • 3. Indo-Burma — NE India (includes Assam, Arunachal). Freshwater biodiversity hotspot. Hoolock gibbon.
  • 4. Sundaland — Andaman and Nicobar Islands (Indian part). Rich marine and island biodiversity.

Causes of Biodiversity Loss — "HIPPO" IUCN Framework

  • Habitat loss & fragmentation — #1 cause globally. Deforestation, urbanisation, agriculture. Fragmentation creates isolated habitat islands ("island effect").
  • Invasive alien species — introduced species outcompete natives. E.g., Nile perch → extinction of 200+ cichlid fish in Lake Victoria. Water hyacinth (Eichhornia crassipes) = "terror of Bengal" — clogs waterways, depletes oxygen.
  • Pollution — land, water, air. Pesticide bioaccumulation (DDT → raptor eggshell thinning → population crash).
  • Population growth (human) — increasing demand for land, food, water.
  • Overexploitation — hunting, fishing beyond sustainable limits. E.g., dodo extinction (1681); passenger pigeon (1914).
  • Also: Co-extinctions — when one species disappears, dependent species also vanish (obligate mutualists, specialized parasites)

NEET 2023 Which of the following is an example of ex situ conservation? (A) Wildlife sanctuary (B) Biosphere reserve (C) Cryopreservation of gametes (D) National park
Answer: (C) Cryopreservation of gametes

Ex situ = "outside habitat." Cryopreservation of gametes (freezing sperm, eggs, embryos at liquid nitrogen temperature −196°C) = ex situ conservation in gene bank. Wildlife sanctuaries, biosphere reserves, national parks = IN SITU conservation (conservation in natural habitat). Botanical gardens and zoos are also ex situ.

Photochemical Smog

NOₓ + Hydrocarbons + Sunlight→ Ozone (O₃, tropospheric)+ PAN (Peroxyacetyl Nitrate)+ Aldehydes→ Photochemical Smog
  • PAN (Peroxyacetyl Nitrate) — the key chemical in photochemical smog. Restricts plant growth, eye irritant.
  • Tropospheric ozone (ground-level) = pollutant (harms plants, lungs). Stratospheric ozone = beneficial (UV shield).
  • Classic smog (London type) = fog + smoke + SO₂ (cold, humid). No sunlight needed.
  • Photochemical smog (LA type) = requires sunlight. Hot, dry conditions. Brown colour.

Ozone Depletion — Montreal Protocol 2 Qs/yr

Ozone Layer and CFCs

Stratospheric ozone (15–35 km altitude) absorbs UV-B and UV-C radiation. Thinning allows UV-B to reach Earth's surface → skin cancer, cataracts, reduced immunity, damage to phytoplankton (aquatic food web disruption).

CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons / Freons) — main culprit. Used in refrigerators, AC, aerosols, foam packaging. CFCs are stable → reach stratosphere intact → UV breaks them → release Cl• radicals.

CFC released→ UV radiation → Cl• radical released→ Cl• + O₃ → ClO + O₂→ ClO + O → Cl• + O₂ (Cl recycled!)→ One Cl• destroys ~1,00,000 O₃ molecules
  • Ozone hole — largest over Antarctica (extreme cold + polar stratospheric clouds enhance CFC destruction)
  • Montreal Protocol (1987) — international treaty to phase out ozone-depleting substances. Milestone of global environmental cooperation. Ozone layer slowly recovering.

Global Warming — Greenhouse Effect Climate Crisis

  • Greenhouse effect: Natural → Earth traps some of Sun's heat via greenhouse gases (GHGs) → essential for life (without it: Earth temp = −15°C)
  • Enhanced greenhouse effect = problem. Burning fossil fuels → rising CO₂ → more heat trapped → rising temperatures
  • GHG rank by warming potential (GWP): SF₆ >> N₂O > CH₄ > CO₂ (GWP per molecule). But CO₂ = most important by total volume.
  • Pre-industrial CO₂ ≈ 280 ppm; current ≈ 420+ ppm (highest in 3 million years)
  • Effects of global warming: Glacial melting → sea level rise; shifting seasons; coral bleaching; spread of tropical diseases; changed precipitation patterns; extinction of species.
  • Carbon sequestration: Forests, oceans absorb CO₂. Afforestation crucial.
  • IPCC (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change) — monitors global warming
  • Kyoto Protocol (1997): Nations committed to reducing GHG emissions. Industrialized nations to reduce emissions by 5.2% below 1990 levels.
  • Paris Agreement (2015): Limit global temperature rise to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels; pursue 1.5°C target.

Water Pollution — Eutrophication & BOD NCERT

Eutrophication

Over-enrichment of water body with nutrients (mainly N and P from fertilizers, sewage, detergents) → explosive algae growth (algal bloom) → algae die → decomposition consumes O₂ → hypoxia/anoxia → death of fish and other aerobic organisms. Dead zones.

Cultural eutrophication = human-accelerated eutrophication (naturally slow process in oligotrophic → eutrophic lakes over thousands of years).

BOD — Biological Oxygen Demand

Amount of dissolved O₂ needed by biological organisms to decompose organic matter in water at a given temperature over 5 days (BOD₅). HIGH BOD = highly polluted water. Clean water BOD <5 mg/L. Sewage BOD = 200–400 mg/L.

Biomagnification (Bioaccumulation) DDT Classic

Biomagnification Definition

Increase in concentration of a pollutant at each successive trophic level. Non-biodegradable pollutants (DDT, methylmercury, PCBs, lead) accumulate in fatty tissues and concentrate as they move up the food chain.

Water: 0.003 ppb DDT→ Phytoplankton: 0.04 ppm→ Small fish: 0.5 ppm→ Large fish: 2 ppm→ Fish-eating birds: 25 ppm → eggshell thinning
  • DDT in birds of prey (osprey, pelican, peregrine falcon): causes thin eggshells → eggs crack → reproductive failure → population crash
  • Minamata disease (Japan): methylmercury in industrial effluents → fish → humans → severe neurological damage, birth defects
  • Biomagnification ≠ bioaccumulation. Bioaccumulation = buildup in an organism. Biomagnification = increase across trophic levels.

Solid Waste Management NCERT Section

  • Municipal Solid Waste (MSW): Household + commercial + institutional waste. India generates ~62 MT/yr.
  • Landfill: Controlled burial. Problem: leachate contamination of groundwater; methane generation.
  • Incineration: Burning waste. Problem: releases dioxins, furans, heavy metals.
  • Bioremediation: Using microorganisms to clean up polluted soil/water. E.g., Pseudomonas degrades hydrocarbons.
  • E-waste: Discarded electronics (contains lead, cadmium, mercury, flame retardants). Major growing problem. Needs formal recycling chains.
  • Integrated Solid Waste Management: Reduce → Reuse → Recycle (3R principle)
  • Case study: Shenzen, China (recycling city); Bamako Convention — ban import of hazardous waste into Africa.

Radioactive Waste & Noise Pollution Brief NCERT

  • Nuclear waste must be stored in thick concrete vaults — buried underground or under ocean floor
  • Nuclear fallout → radioactive isotopes (Sr-90, Cs-137) enter food chains
  • Noise >150 dB → permanent ear damage. Firecrackers, jet aircraft, industrial machinery.
  • Green crackers: emit fewer pollutants than conventional crackers
NEET 2022 Which of the following statements about BOD is correct? (A) BOD of clean water is high (B) BOD of polluted water is low (C) BOD measures the amount of oxygen consumed by bacteria (D) BOD is used to measure air quality
Answer: (C) BOD measures amount of oxygen consumed by bacteria

BOD (Biological Oxygen Demand) = O₂ required by microorganisms to decompose organic matter in water. HIGH BOD = HIGH organic pollution (much O₂ needed). Clean water = LOW BOD (<5 mg/L). Used for WATER quality, not air. Polluted water = HIGH BOD (not low).

🧠 "If NEET Asks…" Decision Blocks

If NEET asks: Which pyramid can never be inverted?

→ Answer: Pyramid of Energy

❌ Pyramid of Number — CAN be inverted (tree → insects → birds) ❌ Pyramid of Biomass — CAN be inverted (aquatic: phytoplankton < zooplankton) ✅ Pyramid of Energy — ALWAYS upright (energy always decreases; 2nd Law of Thermodynamics)
If NEET asks: About J-curve vs S-curve

→ J-curve = Exponential (unlimited resources, dN/dt = rN); S-curve = Logistic (with K, Verhulst-Pearl)

❌ "J-curve shows logistic growth" — TRAP. J = Exponential, S = Logistic ❌ "Maximum growth rate at N=K in logistic" — TRAP. Maximum at N=K/2 ✅ Logistic growth: dN/dt = rN[(K−N)/K]; inflection point at K/2; S-shaped
If NEET asks: Type of interaction (+, 0) — one benefits, one unaffected

→ Answer: Commensalism. Classic examples: orchid on tree (epiphyte), cattle egret + cattle, barnacle on whale

❌ Mutualism = both benefit (+,+). Mycorrhiza, lichen, Rhizobium-legume ❌ Amensalism = one unaffected, other HARMED (0,−). Penicillium → bacteria ✅ Commensalism = (+,0). Epiphyte on mango: epiphyte gets support, mango unaffected
If NEET asks: Which bacteria is involved in nitrification first step?

→ Nitrosomonas (NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻). Second step: Nitrobacter (NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻)

❌ Azotobacter = nitrogen FIXATION (N₂ → NH₃), not nitrification ❌ Thiobacillus = DENITRIFICATION (NO₃⁻ → N₂) ✅ Nitrosomonas → Nitrobacter = nitrification chain (remember: Nitrosomonas comes first alphabetically AND functionally)
If NEET asks: Primary succession vs Secondary — which is faster?

→ Secondary succession is FASTER (soil already present)

❌ Primary succession starts on bare substrate with no soil → very slow (1000s of years) ✅ Secondary succession — disturbed area with pre-existing soil → decades to centuries ✅ Pioneer in primary xerarch = LICHEN; Pioneer in secondary = grasses/weeds
If NEET asks: About ozone depletion — gas responsible, treaty, effect

Gas → CFCs. Treaty → Montreal Protocol (1987). Effect → UV-B reaching Earth → skin cancer, cataracts, phytoplankton damage

❌ Kyoto Protocol = greenhouse gas reduction (NOT ozone) ❌ CO₂ does NOT deplete ozone — it causes greenhouse effect ✅ CFCs release Cl• radical → each destroys ~1,00,000 O₃ molecules → ozone hole (Antarctica)
