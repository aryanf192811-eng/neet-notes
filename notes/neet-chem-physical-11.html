1.1 The Mole Concept NCERT §1.7

🔴 2–3 Qs/paper · Calculation-based Limiting reagent — tested 8 of last 10 years Mole fraction — every NEET since 2018
Definition

One mole = $6.022 \times 10^{23}$ entities (Avogadro's number, $N_A$). The mole is the SI unit of amount of substance. 1 mole of any substance has a mass equal to its molecular mass in grams (molar mass, $M$).

Core Mole Formulae

$n = \dfrac{m}{M}$   (moles = mass ÷ molar mass)

$n = \dfrac{N}{N_A}$   (moles = particles ÷ Avogadro's no.)

$n = \dfrac{V(\text{L})}{22.4}$   (gas at STP: 1 mol = 22.4 L)

$n = \dfrac{V(\text{mL}) \times M_{\text{conc}}}{1000}$   (from molarity)

Avogadro's Number Applications

Atoms in $n$ mol of element = $n \times N_A$

Molecules in $n$ mol = $n \times N_A$

Electrons: 1 mol H₂O has $10 N_A$ electrons (Z=1 for H gives 2e, Z=8 for O gives 8e; total 10)

$N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23}$ mol⁻¹

STP Conditions (IUPAC 2010)

Old STP: 0°C (273K), 1 atm → 22.4 L/mol

New IUPAC STP: 0°C, 100 kPa → 22.7 L/mol

NEET uses 22.4 L/mol — old standard still tested!

NTP: 25°C (298K), 1 atm

Numerical: How many molecules are in 9 g of water?
$M_{\text{H}_2\text{O}} = 18$ g/mol, $n = 9/18 = 0.5$ mol
Molecules $= 0.5 \times 6.022 \times 10^{23} = 3.011 \times 10^{23}$
Atoms $= 3 \times 3.011 \times 10^{23} = 9.033 \times 10^{23}$ (2H + 1O per molecule)

⚡ Fast Mole Calculation:
Step 1: Write molar mass from formula (just add atomic masses)
Step 2: Divide given mass by molar mass → get moles
Step 3: Multiply moles by what's asked: $N_A$ for particles, 22.4 for gas volume (STP)
Atomic masses to memorise (exact for NEET): H=1, C=12, N=14, O=16, Na=23, Mg=24, Al=27, S=32, Cl=35.5, Ca=40, Fe=56, Cu=63.5, Zn=65, Br=80, Ag=108, I=127

$M = \dfrac{10 \times 1.84 \times 98}{98} = 18.4$ mol/L ≈ 18.4 M

1.3 Stoichiometry & Limiting Reagent NCERT §1.9 — MUST KNOW

Limiting Reagent

The reactant that is completely consumed first in a reaction, limiting the amount of product formed. Identify by dividing moles of each reactant by its stoichiometric coefficient — the smallest ratio gives the limiting reagent.

Numerical: 3.0 g H₂ reacts with 32.0 g O₂. Find limiting reagent and water formed.
Reaction: $2\text{H}_2 + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$
Moles: $n(\text{H}_2) = 3/2 = 1.5$ mol; $n(\text{O}_2) = 32/32 = 1.0$ mol
Ratio required: $\text{H}_2:\text{O}_2 = 2:1$; available ratio $= 1.5:1.0 = 1.5:1$
Need 2:1 but have 1.5:1 → H₂ is limiting
Moles H₂O $= 1.5$ mol (same as H₂, ratio 2:2) → Mass $= 1.5 \times 18 = $ 27 g

⚡ Limiting Reagent Fast Method:
For reaction $aA + bB \rightarrow$ products:
Divide moles by coefficient: $\frac{n_A}{a}$ and $\frac{n_B}{b}$
Smaller value → limiting reagent. Use that value to find product moles.
Product moles $= \frac{n_{\text{LR}}}{\text{coeff of LR}} \times \text{coeff of product}$

% Yield & % Purity

$\%\text{ yield} = \dfrac{\text{Actual yield}}{\text{Theoretical yield}} \times 100$     $\%\text{ purity} = \dfrac{\text{Pure substance}}{\text{Total mass}} \times 100$

1.4 Empirical & Molecular Formula NCERT §1.10

Step-by-step method (NEET standard):

  • Convert % composition → assume 100 g sample → mass = %
  • Divide each mass by atomic mass → moles of each element
  • Divide all mole values by smallest value → simplest whole number ratio
  • If ratio has .5 → multiply all by 2; if .33 → multiply by 3; if .25 → multiply by 4
  • This gives Empirical Formula (EF)
  • $n = \frac{M_r(\text{compound})}{M_r(\text{EF})}$ → Molecular Formula = EF $\times n$

Numerical: Compound: C=40%, H=6.67%, O=53.33%. $M_r = 60$. Find molecular formula.
Moles: C = 40/12 = 3.33; H = 6.67/1 = 6.67; O = 53.33/16 = 3.33
Ratio: C:H:O = 3.33:6.67:3.33 → divide by 3.33 → 1:2:1
EF = CH₂O; $M_r(\text{EF}) = 12+2+16 = 30$
$n = 60/30 = 2$ → Molecular formula = C₂H₄O₂ (acetic acid)

🎯 Empirical Formula Rules:
— If ratio ends in .5 → multiply by 2
— If ratio ends in .33 → multiply by 3
— If ratio ends in .25 → multiply by 4
— Whole numbers mean EF = MF (no multiplier needed)

1.P PYQ Pattern Analysis 2015–2025

NEET 2024 What is the number of oxygen atoms in 0.5 mol of K₂Cr₂O₇?
Answer: 2.107 × 10²⁴
Concept

K₂Cr₂O₇ has 7 oxygen atoms per formula unit

Calculation

0.5 mol × 7 × $N_A$ = 0.5 × 7 × 6.022×10²³ = 2.107×10²⁴

Key Step

Always count atoms per formula unit FIRST. Then multiply by moles × $N_A$.

NEET 2022 The molality of a solution of H₂SO₄ containing 9.8 g H₂SO₄ in 250 g water is:
Answer: 0.4 mol/kg
Concept

Molality = moles of solute / kg of solvent

Calculation

n(H₂SO₄) = 9.8/98 = 0.1 mol; m = 0.1/(250/1000) = 0.1/0.25 = 0.4 mol/kg

Trap

Use SOLVENT mass (250 g), NOT solution mass. Water goes in denominator as kg.

NEET 2020 Empirical formula of a compound is CH₂O. Its molecular mass is 90 g/mol. Molecular formula is:
Answer: C₃H₆O₃
Concept

n = M(compound) / M(EF) = 90/30 = 3

Rule

Molecular formula = (CH₂O) × 3 = C₃H₆O₃

Note

C₃H₆O₃ could be lactic acid or glyceraldehyde — NEET doesn't distinguish, just needs the formula.

2.1 Bohr Model of Hydrogen Atom NCERT §2.4 — Very High Yield

🔴 3-4 Qs/paper — Bohr model numericals Quantum numbers — conceptual Qs every year de Broglie + Heisenberg — 2023, 2022, 2021 tested
Bohr Formulae for Hydrogen-like Atoms

Energy: $E_n = -\dfrac{13.6 Z^2}{n^2}$ eV

Radius: $r_n = \dfrac{0.529 \; n^2}{Z}$ Å (Bohr radius $a_0 = 0.529$ Å)

Velocity: $v_n = \dfrac{2.18 \times 10^6 Z}{n}$ m/s

Frequency: $\nu = \dfrac{RcZ^2}{\hbar}\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)$

Rydberg Formula (Spectral Lines)

$\dfrac{1}{\lambda} = R_H Z^2 \left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)$

$R_H = 1.097 \times 10^7$ m⁻¹ (Rydberg constant)

For H-atom: $\bar{\nu} = R_H\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1^2} - \dfrac{1}{n_2^2}\right)$

Number of spectral lines from level n: $\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2}$

🎯 Memory Aid — Spectral Series:
Lyman — UV — Lets Us (L=1, UV)
Balmer — Visible — Black Vans (B=2, Visible)
Paschen — IR — Poor Infrared (P=3, IR)
In order: n₁ = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 for Lyman, Balmer, Paschen, Brackett, Pfund

Numerical: Calculate the energy of electron in 2nd orbit of He⁺ (Z=2).
$E_n = -\dfrac{13.6 \times Z^2}{n^2} = -\dfrac{13.6 \times 4}{4} = -13.6$ eV
Note: He⁺ in n=2 has same energy as H in n=1 (both = -13.6 eV)

⚡ Bohr Model Energy Trick:
$E_n \propto Z^2/n^2$ and $r_n \propto n^2/Z$
So for H-like ions: if $Z$ doubles → energy decreases 4× (more negative), radius halves
Energy of He⁺(n=1) = $-13.6 \times 4 = -54.4$ eV (4× more bound than H)
Ionisation energy = $-E_1 = 13.6Z^2$ eV (always positive)

Rules for Electronic Config

Aufbau: Fill lowest energy first (1s→2s→2p→3s→3p→4s→3d...)

Pauli exclusion: No 2 electrons can have all 4 QNs identical

Hund's rule: Maximum spin multiplicity in degenerate orbitals

Energy Order (n+l rule)

Lower (n+l) → filled first; if same (n+l) → lower n fills first

1s(1) < 2s(2) < 2p(3) < 3s(3) < 3p(4) < 4s(4) < 3d(5) < 4p(5)...

∴ 4s fills BEFORE 3d despite 3d having lower n

NEET Exception — Anomalous Config

Cr (Z=24): Expected [Ar]3d⁴4s² but actual = [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ (half-filled d is extra stable)
Cu (Z=29): Expected [Ar]3d⁹4s² but actual = [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹ (fully-filled d is extra stable)
Also: Mo(42), Ag(47), Au(79) have similar anomalies. The stability comes from exchange energy in half-filled or fully-filled subshells.

Numerical: How many electrons in Fe³⁺ have l=2 (d electrons)?
Fe (Z=26): [Ar]3d⁶4s² → Fe³⁺ removes 3e from outermost first: [Ar]3d⁵
d electrons (l=2): 5

2.3 Wave-Particle Duality & Uncertainty NCERT §2.7

de Broglie Wavelength

$\lambda = \dfrac{h}{mv} = \dfrac{h}{p}$   ($p$ = momentum)

For electron accelerated through V volts:

$\lambda = \dfrac{h}{\sqrt{2meV}}$

$h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s; $m_e = 9.1 \times 10^{-31}$ kg

Practical: $\lambda(\text{Å}) \approx \sqrt{150/V}$ for electrons

Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle

$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \dfrac{h}{4\pi}$

$\Delta x \cdot \Delta v \geq \dfrac{h}{4\pi m}$

$\Delta E \cdot \Delta t \geq \dfrac{h}{4\pi}$

Cannot simultaneously know exact position AND momentum. Applies to all particles but only significant for sub-atomic level.

⚡ de Broglie NEET Trick:
At same KE: heavier particle has shorter wavelength ($\lambda = h/p = h/\sqrt{2mKE}$ → $\lambda \propto 1/\sqrt{m}$)
At same velocity: heavier particle has shorter wavelength ($\lambda = h/mv$ → $\lambda \propto 1/m$)
Order: proton, deuteron, α-particle — at same KE, α has shortest λ (heaviest)
NEET 2023 asked: "Which has smallest de Broglie wavelength at same KE?" → Heaviest particle!

Numerical: Calculate de Broglie wavelength of an electron moving at $2 \times 10^6$ m/s.
$\lambda = \dfrac{h}{mv} = \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{9.1 \times 10^{-31} \times 2 \times 10^6}$
$= \dfrac{6.626 \times 10^{-34}}{1.82 \times 10^{-24}} = 3.64 \times 10^{-10}$ m = 3.64 Å

Photoelectric Effect (Einstein)

$KE_{\max} = h\nu - h\nu_0 = h\nu - \phi$    ($\phi = h\nu_0$ = work function)
  • Threshold frequency $\nu_0$: minimum frequency to eject electron ($KE = 0$)
  • Increasing frequency → increases $KE_\text{max}$; increasing intensity → increases number of electrons (NOT KE)
  • $eV_{\text{stop}} = KE_{\max}$ (stopping potential $V_\text{stop}$ arrests electrons)

2.P PYQ Pattern Analysis 2015–2025

NEET 2023 Light of wavelength 3000 Å falls on a metal. Threshold wavelength is 4000 Å. Find max KE of emitted electrons. ($h = 6.6×10⁻³⁴$ J·s, $c = 3×10⁸$ m/s)
Answer: 1.65 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
Concept

$KE = h\nu - h\nu_0 = hc(1/\lambda - 1/\lambda_0)$

Calculation

$KE = 6.6\times10^{-34}\times3\times10^8(1/3000-1/4000)\times10^{10}$
$= 1.98\times10^{-25}\times(3.33-2.5)\times10^6 = 1.65\times10^{-19}$ J

Key

$\lambda < \lambda_0$ for photoelectric effect. Shorter wavelength = higher frequency = higher energy.

NEET 2022 Which of the following has the maximum number of unpaired electrons: Mn²⁺, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺, Co²⁺?
Answer: Mn²⁺ and Fe³⁺ (both have 5 unpaired e⁻)
Concept

For transition metal ions, remove electrons from 4s first, then 3d

Config

Mn²⁺: [Ar]3d⁵ → 5 unpaired; Fe²⁺: [Ar]3d⁶ → 4 unpaired; Fe³⁺: [Ar]3d⁵ → 5 unpaired; Co²⁺: [Ar]3d⁷ → 3 unpaired

Rule

d⁵ configuration = 5 unpaired (maximum possible for d subshell by Hund's rule)

5.2 Ideal Gas Equation & Dalton's Law Core Formulae

Ideal Gas Equation

$PV = nRT$   ($R = 8.314$ J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹ = $0.0821$ L·atm mol⁻¹ K⁻¹)

$PV = \dfrac{m}{M}RT$   (m = mass, M = molar mass)

$P = \rho \dfrac{RT}{M}$   ($\rho$ = density = m/V)

Molar mass from gas density: $M = \dfrac{\rho RT}{P}$

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

$P_{\text{total}} = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + \ldots$ (at const T, V)

Partial pressure: $P_A = \chi_A \times P_{\text{total}}$

Wet gas (over water): $P_{\text{dry gas}} = P_{\text{total}} - P_{\text{water vapour}}$

$P_{\text{water vapour}}$ at 25°C = 23.8 mmHg ≈ 25 mmHg (NEET uses 25)

Graham's Law of Diffusion

$\dfrac{r_A}{r_B} = \sqrt{\dfrac{M_B}{M_A}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{d_B}{d_A}}$

Rate of diffusion ∝ 1/√M (lighter gas diffuses faster)

$\dfrac{r_A}{r_B} = \dfrac{t_B}{t_A}$ (inverse of time taken)

At same T & P: applies to same-T effusion/diffusion

Numerical: A gas occupies 500 mL at 27°C and 1 atm. Find volume at 127°C and 2 atm.
$\dfrac{P_1V_1}{T_1} = \dfrac{P_2V_2}{T_2}$ → $V_2 = \dfrac{P_1V_1T_2}{T_1P_2}$
$T_1 = 300K$, $T_2 = 400K$, $P_1 = 1$, $P_2 = 2$, $V_1 = 500$ mL
$V_2 = \dfrac{1 \times 500 \times 400}{300 \times 2} = \dfrac{200000}{600} = $ 333.3 mL

Numerical — Graham's Law: Rate of diffusion of SO₂ vs O₂?
$M(\text{SO}_2) = 64$, $M(\text{O}_2) = 32$
$\dfrac{r_{\text{O}_2}}{r_{\text{SO}_2}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{64}{32}} = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.41$
O₂ diffuses 1.41 times faster than SO₂ (lighter → faster)

5.3 Kinetic Molecular Theory & Speed Types NCERT §5.7

Types of Molecular Speeds

Most probable speed (u*): $u^* = \sqrt{\dfrac{2RT}{M}}$

Average speed ($\bar{u}$): $\bar{u} = \sqrt{\dfrac{8RT}{\pi M}}$

RMS speed ($u_{\text{rms}}$): $u_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{3RT}{M}}$

Order: $u^* < \bar{u} < u_{\text{rms}}$

Ratio: $u^* : \bar{u} : u_{\text{rms}} = 1 : 1.128 : 1.225 \approx \sqrt{2}:\sqrt{8/\pi}:\sqrt{3}$

KMT Postulates (NEET tested)

1. Gas = tiny particles in continuous random motion

2. Collisions are perfectly elastic (no energy loss)

3. No intermolecular attractions/repulsions in ideal gas

4. Average KE ∝ T (absolute): $\overline{KE} = \dfrac{3}{2}RT$ per mol

5. Pressure due to collision of molecules with walls

Kinetic Energy Expressions

Average KE per molecule: $\dfrac{3}{2}k_BT$ ($k_B = R/N_A$)

Average KE per mole: $\dfrac{3}{2}RT$

Total KE of n moles: $\dfrac{3}{2}nRT = \dfrac{3}{2}PV$

$KE \propto T$ (absolute temperature)

⚡ Speed Comparison Trick:
All three speeds $\propto \sqrt{T/M}$, so:
— At same T: lighter gas has higher speed ($u \propto 1/\sqrt{M}$)
— At same M: higher T → higher speed
— RMS speed of H₂ at 27°C: $u = \sqrt{3 \times 8.314 \times 300 / 0.002} \approx 1934$ m/s
NEET loves asking: "At what temperature will RMS speed of a gas double?" → T must become 4× (since $u \propto \sqrt{T}$)

5.4 Real Gases & Van der Waals Equation NCERT §5.8

$\left(P + \dfrac{an^2}{V^2}\right)(V - nb) = nRT$
Van der Waals Constants

a: intermolecular attractive forces. Larger a = stronger attractions

b: excluded volume (co-volume), effective volume of molecules

Greater 'a' → more easily liquefied (stronger attractions)

Real gas behaves ideally at low P and high T

Compressibility Factor Z

$Z = \dfrac{PV}{nRT}$ (ideal gas: Z = 1)

Z < 1: repulsion < attraction (gas easier to compress)

Z > 1: repulsion dominates (volume effect, high P)

At Boyle temperature: $Z = 1$ over wide P range

Liquefaction Facts

Critical temperature ($T_c$): max T above which gas cannot be liquefied. Above $T_c$ = gas only.

$T_c = \dfrac{8a}{27Rb}$; $P_c = \dfrac{a}{27b^2}$; $V_c = 3nb$

Higher 'a' → higher $T_c$ → more easily liquefied at room T

6.1 First Law of Thermodynamics NCERT §6.3 — Always Tested

🔴 3 Qs/paper — highest yield physical chem chapter Gibbs free energy & spontaneity — every NEET Hess Law — 9 of last 10 exams
$\Delta U = q + w$      or      $\Delta U = q - P\Delta V$ (work done BY system = $P\Delta V$)
Work (w)

Work done ON system (compression): $w = -P_{\text{ext}}\Delta V$ (when $\Delta V < 0$, w is positive)

Isothermal reversible expansion (ideal gas):
$w = -nRT\ln\dfrac{V_2}{V_1} = -2.303nRT\log\dfrac{V_2}{V_1}$

$w = -nRT\log\dfrac{P_1}{P_2}$ (equivalent)

Free expansion (into vacuum): $w = 0$ ($P_{\text{ext}} = 0$)

Sign Conventions

q > 0: heat absorbed by system (endothermic)

q < 0: heat released by system (exothermic)

w > 0: work done ON system (compression)

w < 0: work done BY system (expansion)

$\Delta U > 0$: internal energy increases

State Functions vs Path Functions

State functions: $\Delta U$, H, S, G — depend only on initial & final states

Path functions: q and w — depend on the path taken

For cyclic process: $\Delta U = 0$ (returns to same state)

Isothermal process on ideal gas: $\Delta U = 0$ (U depends only on T)

$w = -2.303 \times 5 \times 8.314 \times 300 \times \log(25/5)$
$= -2.303 \times 5 \times 8.314 \times 300 \times 0.699$
$= -2.303 \times 1247.1 \times 0.699 = $ $-2006$ J $= -2.006$ kJ
(Negative = work done BY system = expansion)

6.2 Enthalpy (H) & Bond Enthalpy NCERT §6.4

$H = U + PV$    ∴    $\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta(PV) = \Delta U + \Delta n_g RT$
Key Relation

$\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta n_g RT$ where $\Delta n_g$ = moles of gaseous products − moles of gaseous reactants. For reactions involving only solids/liquids: $\Delta H \approx \Delta U$.

Standard Enthalpies (Memorise)

$\Delta H_f^\circ$: enthalpy of formation (from elements in standard states)

$\Delta H_c^\circ$: enthalpy of combustion (products: CO₂ and H₂O)

$\Delta H_{\text{sub}}$: enthalpy of sublimation (solid → gas)

$\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$: enthalpy of hydration (ion + water)

$\Delta H_{\text{soln}}$: $\Delta H_{\text{latt}} + \Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$

Bond Enthalpy Method

$\Delta H_{\text{rxn}} = \sum \Delta H(\text{bonds broken}) - \sum \Delta H(\text{bonds formed})$

Breaking bonds: always endothermic (+ΔH)

Forming bonds: always exothermic (−ΔH)

Use: when ΔH of individual molecules not given

Exo vs Endo

Exothermic: ΔH < 0, products more stable

Endothermic: ΔH > 0, reactants more stable

Combustion, neutralization, formation of H₂O, HCl formation: all exothermic

Photosynthesis, vaporization, dissolving NH₄Cl: endothermic

6.3 Hess's Law of Constant Heat Summation NCERT §6.5

Hess's Law

The total enthalpy change of a reaction is the same whether the reaction occurs in one step or multiple steps (because enthalpy is a state function). This allows calculation of ΔH for reactions that cannot be directly measured.

Numerical — Hess's Law: Calculate ΔH for C(s) + ½O₂(g) → CO(g) given:
(i) C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g), $\Delta H_1 = -393.5$ kJ/mol
(ii) CO(g) + ½O₂(g) → CO₂(g), $\Delta H_2 = -283.5$ kJ/mol
Target = (i) − (ii): $\Delta H = \Delta H_1 - \Delta H_2 = -393.5 - (-283.5) = $ $-110$ kJ/mol

⚡ Hess's Law Trick:
1. Write the target equation
2. Reverse any given equation if needed (flip sign of ΔH)
3. Multiply equations by coefficients (ΔH scales proportionally)
4. Add equations to get target
5. Sum the ΔH values (including signs)
Hot tip: If a species appears on same side twice → it's an intermediate, not a product!

Born-Haber Cycle (Ionic Compounds)

For NaCl formation: $\Delta H_f = \Delta H_{\text{sub}} + IE_1 + \frac{1}{2}\Delta H_{\text{diss}} + EA + U_L$

  • $\Delta H_{\text{sub}}$: sublimation enthalpy of Na(s) (+108 kJ/mol)
  • $IE_1$: first ionization energy of Na (+496 kJ/mol)
  • $\frac{1}{2}\Delta H_{\text{diss}}$: dissociation of ½Cl₂ (+122 kJ/mol)
  • $EA$: electron affinity of Cl (−349 kJ/mol)
  • $U_L$: lattice enthalpy of NaCl (−788 kJ/mol)
  • $\Delta H_f(\text{NaCl}) = -411$ kJ/mol (resultant)

6.4 Entropy (ΔS) & Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG) NCERT §6.7 — Central to Spontaneity

Entropy (S) — Disorder/Randomness

$\Delta S = \dfrac{q_{\text{rev}}}{T}$   (at constant T)

$\Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} = -\dfrac{\Delta H_{\text{system}}}{T}$

$\Delta S_{\text{universe}} = \Delta S_{\text{system}} + \Delta S_{\text{surroundings}} \geq 0$

Second Law: Total entropy of universe always increases in spontaneous processes

Third Law: Entropy of pure perfect crystal at 0K = 0

Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG)

$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$

$\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K = -2.303RT\log K$

At equilibrium: $\Delta G = 0$ (not $\Delta G^\circ$!)

$\Delta G < 0$: spontaneous (product-favoured)

$\Delta G > 0$: non-spontaneous

$\Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + RT\ln Q$

ΔHΔSΔG = ΔH - TΔSSpontaneity
−+Always −Always spontaneous at all T
+−Always +Never spontaneous at any T
−−− at low T, + at high TSpontaneous at low T only
+++ at low T, − at high TSpontaneous at high T only

🎯 Spontaneity Memory Table:
− − (exo, dec S): "Cold spontaneous" → low T spont. (ΔH wins at low T)
+ + (endo, inc S): "Hot spontaneous" → high T spont. (TΔS wins at high T)
− + (exo, inc S): "Always spontaneous" → double advantage, always ΔG < 0
+ − (endo, dec S): "Never spontaneous" → double disadvantage, always ΔG > 0

Numerical: For a reaction, ΔH = -30 kJ/mol and ΔS = -100 J/mol·K. At what temperature does it become non-spontaneous?
At crossover: $\Delta G = 0 \Rightarrow T = \dfrac{\Delta H}{\Delta S} = \dfrac{-30000}{-100} = 300$ K
Below 300K: spontaneous (ΔG < 0). Above 300K: non-spontaneous (ΔG > 0)
[Note: Convert ΔH to J for consistent units]

7.1 Chemical Equilibrium — K_c and K_p NCERT §7.4

🟢 2–3 Qs/paper Ksp and solubility — most tested ionic equilibrium topic pH calculations — 2023, 2022, 2021, 2020
Equilibrium Constants

For: $aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD$

$K_c = \dfrac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}$   (concentrations in mol/L)

$K_p = \dfrac{(P_C)^c(P_D)^d}{(P_A)^a(P_B)^b}$   (partial pressures in atm)

$K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n_g}$   (R = 0.0821 L·atm·mol⁻¹K⁻¹)

Properties of K

K has no units (standard concentrations/pressures used)

K depends ONLY on temperature

If K > 1: forward reaction favoured (more products)

If K < 1: reverse reaction favoured (more reactants)

K > 10³: essentially complete; K < 10⁻³: barely proceeds

Reaction Quotient Q

$Q$ has same expression as $K$ but at non-equilibrium conditions

Q < K: forward reaction proceeds (more products needed)

Q > K: reverse reaction proceeds (excess products)

Q = K: system at equilibrium

Numerical — Kp from Kc: For N₂O₄(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g), $K_c = 0.07$ mol/L at 25°C. Find $K_p$.
$\Delta n_g = 2 - 1 = 1$
$K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n_g} = 0.07 \times (0.0821 \times 298)^1 = 0.07 \times 24.47 = $ $1.71$ atm

⚡ When Kp = Kc:
$K_p = K_c$ ONLY when $\Delta n_g = 0$ (no change in moles of gas)
Examples: H₂ + I₂ ⇌ 2HI (Δng = 0), PCl₃ + Cl₂ ⇌ PCl₅ (Δng = -1 ≠ 0)
PCl₃ + Cl₂ → Δng = 1 - 2 = -1, so Kp = Kc(RT)⁻¹ = Kc/RT < Kc

For acidic buffer (weak acid + conjugate base):
